1.   I ntroduction
In higher education, encouraging learner autonomy is vital for sparking sustained, in - depth engagement with academic material. Students should do more than sit in lectures and jot down
notes; they must take part in lively discussions, testing ideas ,   and working with peers to sharpen their understanding through critical questioning and shared exploration   (Draper & Fisher, 2020; Fox &
Docherty, 2019).
Nevertheless, a spate of recent investigations has drawn attention to troubling trends. First, numerous college students take part in class learning only half - heartedly   (Gebeyehu & Regasa, 2016;
Konopka et al., 2015; Metzger & Langley, 2020; Mundt & Hänze, 2023) .   It is common to observe passive habits, such as being present in lecture hall seats yet failing to raise questions or share ideas.
Second, student autonomy in learning remains low ;   students consistently rely on   others   (Bundela Gupta & Gupta, 2023; Hermawan et al., 2024) .   Third, peer - to - peer discussions about course material
occur   rarely, and this limitation blocks long - term memory and deep   understanding   (Chou & Lin, 2015; Preszler, 2009) .
Within Indonesian universities, teacher - centred lecturing still dominates, a reality that keeps many learners on the sidelines and slows the cultivation of self - directed study skills. Research shows
that such conventional approaches harm both grades and le vels of active involvement   (Bahar et al., 2020; Namira et al., 2023) .
To counter these shortcomings, the present study trials the   P eer   L esson   S trategy   (PLS) , a cooperative framework in which students work in structured teams, lead brief sessions, and discuss
tasks with one another.   This approach seeks not only to broaden   students'   grasp of course material but also to encourage them to take an active role in their own learning   (M. Silberman, 1996).   Evidence
from earlier studies shows that peer - teaching formats can raise achievement (Erni Yuliati, 2020; Nuralam & Juslinda, 2019) ,   classroom involvement   (Isaleha et al., 2021; Suketi, 2019) , and foster vital
interpersonal skills   (Maharani et al., 2018).
Many higher education institutions are still dominated by lecture - based practices, which tend to deprive students of opportunities to practice autonomy and sustained participation. Addressing this
issue calls for learner - constructivist practice frameworks   for problem - centric skill acquisition. This study explores these peer lesson strategies to increase student independence and activity during
instructional periods. How can Peer Lesson Study increase students’ autonomy and engagement during higher education   learning?
2.   Literature Review
### 2.1 .   Peer Lesson Strategy (PLS)
The Peer Lesson Strategy (PLS) is an instructional model based on Vygotsky’s Sociocultural Theory   (1978) , Piagetian constructivism (Saravanan & Murphy, 2023) , and cognitive apprenticeship
(Collins et al., 2018) . Under this approach, students typically   organize in groups to   plan and teach lessons to their peers. As a result of this strategy, they assume the dual role of learners and
knowledge facilitators. This encourages them to systematically integrate subject matter synthesis with appropriate pedagogical development and re flective teaching.
Unlike traditional lecture formats ,   where college students are treated as passive information receptacles (Wing‐yi Cheng et al., 2008) ,   PLS offers students opportunities for active engagement with
their peers, fostering more lively interaction. When college students participate in peer teaching, the learning experience becomes   interactive,   and student   driven . This approach incites dialogue as well
as critical and collaborative thinking, which deepens understanding while building community and ownership of the learning process   (Bovill, 2020; Dwijayanti & Pathoni, 2016; Rianita, 2022) .

### 2.2 .   Independent Learning
Learning is an evolving process during which a person acquires new insights and knowledge that often results in behavioral modification   (Hergenhahn & Olson, 2012) . This journey can occur at any
time and in any place due to the interaction of the learner with their environment.   E ffective   learning , therefore, necessitates independence, which is essential for the   development of both skills and
character.
Independent learning fosters initiative among students, allowing them to achieve their learning goals without instructor dependence   (Sari & Zamroni, 2019; Tanjung, 2019) .   It is a self - managed
endeavor where learners plan , monitor,   and evaluate their activities. In this case, teaching aids facilitate rather than control understanding. Instruction designed to promote autonomy helps foster
cognitive and emotional abilities as well as reflective thinking processes associated with learn ing (Meyer et al., 2008; Nuris & Istyaningputri, 2021; Sarsekeyeva et al., 2019) . As espoused by   Knowles
(1975) , independent learning is self - directed but collectively undertaken with guidance from instructors, mentors, tutors ,   or peers while learners take full responsibility for progress.   The
introduction of andragogy is important because it transitions from considering students as mere consumers of information to recognizing   their engagement in the learning process. It focuses more
on the learner and their experience, defining teaching as a guiding and not commanding task. Also, Kozma et al.   (1978)   make a difference between independent learning systems and individual learning
systems. Independent learning systems grant learners the ability to create experiences based on their personal objectives, available resources, and other relevant factors. Indiv idual learning systems tend
to be more hierarchical in nature where teachers or policymakers set goals, materials, activities, and https://doi.org/10.22521/edupij.2025.18.4 44   Published online by Universitepark Press

al. (2020) , assert that customized digital frameworks for study foster independence by granting students control over the timing, order   and content of instruction made available to them.
### 2.3. Active Learning
The concept of active learning , derived from the works of Jean Piaget and Lev Vygotsky, views learning as an experience where students synthesize information through participatory interaction
with the content, defining it as a social and mental initiative rather than   simply listening to a lecture (Saravanan & Murphy, 2023) . Constructivist strategies problem solving, discussion, and even peer
instruction which foster independent critical thinking ,   have also been found effective on deeper understanding   (Amaefuna & Ezeliora, 2021; Bashir, 2020; Deng, 2025; Maynes & Straub, 2024) . There
is research evidence regarding the efficacy of engagement, comprehension as well as performance enhancement with peer lesson strategies. For instance, first - year mathematics students reported
better assessment outcomes using peer reasoning   (Vozzo et al., 2024) , while   (Y. Zhang & Wang, 2024) reported increased participation in courses alongside   a more profound understanding of   peer lesson
hardware courses. Through participating in online engineering courses, other students showed positive results with peer instructional team - based learning   (Harvie et al., 2024) .
Nonetheless, some research offers more critical or nuanced views. Fakoya et al.   (2023)   remarked that peer lesson strategy poses active learning benefits ,   however, it has some challenges like time
limitations and instructor flexibility. As Knight et al.   (2013)   illustrates, peer instruction does not always promote higher academic achievement in the absence of well - designed questions and supportive
classroom culture. These findings imply that although peer lesson strategy has great potential in propelling active   learning, its impact will vary depending on the context in which it is applied and
requires careful consideration.

3. 3 . Participants
The sample of this study   consisted of 67 students enrolled in the Economics Education Program at Sebelas Maret University, comprising   28 males and 39 females. The students were drawn from a
class that displayed very low independent learning skills and low engagement in the classroom.   The following are criteria. 1. The participant must be a sophomore in the active status of the Economics
Education Program. 2. The participant must have an attendance of at least 75%. 3. The participant must be willing to assist in implementing the peer te aching sessions. 4. The participant must have no
record of formal teaching training. Despite these chall enges, most students had a GPA above 3.0 .

5. Discussion
The findings of Cycle 2 confirm that the Peer Lesson Strategy (PLS) effectively improves independent learning across four indicators.   Firstly, the peer lesson atmosphere fostered both
explorative and creative learning because students attempted to invent   and prepare games, videos, and other creative ways to teach their colleagues, and they actively tried to teach.   This follows
Vygotsky   (1978)   sociocultural theory on learning, which puts emphasis on the importance of dialogue on one’s development, and is also supported by peer lesson studies which state that peer lessons
positively aid in critical and creative thinking in psychologically safe en vironments   (Laal & Ghodsi, 2012; van Leeuwen & Janssen, 2019) .   Creativity was particularly evident when learners combined
goal - orientation with autonomy,   confirming earlier   findings   that hands - on,   student - centered activities enhance creative thinking   (Beghetto & Kaufman, 2014; Heldanita, 2019; Hidayati &
Sarumaha, 2024) .   Also, a peer teaching session encouraged students to take responsibility for their learning. In the student’s role as a teacher, they created lessons and materials, asked questions, and
ensured their peers would grasp the content. This fostered independent   learning by developing the capacity for accountability and self - regulation (Zimmerman, 2002) .   Furthermore, there is existing
research demonstrating that peer teaching fosters autonomy, professional competence, and self - efficacy   (Dewantono & Murtisari, 2023; Peets et al., 2009) .
Furthermore, PLS enhanced students’ ability to try new things, particularly through the use of different teaching tools, as well as digital and varied teaching methods. The need to create engaging
peer   lessons   stimulated   and   nurtured   curiosity   and   exploration,   essential   components   of independent and active learning   (Prince, 2004) . Through providing safe spaces to experiment, peer
lessons encouraged autonomy and creative problem - solving   (Silberman, 1996; Stigmar, 2016)   .
Finally, students’ focus on learning material increased as they assumed dual roles as teachers and learners, requiring thorough preparation, critical engagement, and participation in debates. This not
only enhanced mastery and retention   (Gillies, 2016) , but also illustrated the impact of peer lesson on attention and ownership in learning, which empowered learners to set milestones and devise self -
determined, actionable plans to reach those milestones and strategies   (Njoku & Nwagbo, 2020; Pechinthorn et al., 2020; Shrivastava et al., 2023) .   Altogether, these results validate the conclusion
that the Peer Lesson Strategy fosters autonomy, engagement, and independent learning by nurturing self - determined, creative, responsible, and focused learning.
The Peer Lesson Strategy (PLS) augments active learning in multiple ways. Visual engagement attention is gained during peer teaching with the use of diagrams, color coding, and other relevant
materials, leading to better understanding, retention, and motiv ation   (Fiorella & Mayer, 2015; Mayer,
2017) .   This is in agreement with the multimodal learning theory which claims that the incorporation
of verbal and visual components is more effective   (Moreno & Mayer, 2007) . Moreover, PLS fosters verbal participation by engaging students in ‘teacher’ and ‘learner’ roles. This motivates them to talk,
to express and exchange ideas, and to ask questions, resulting in a more interactive and relaxed classroom. Prior research conf irms peer teaching enhances participation and verbal confidence,
especially in mathematics   (Vozzo et al., 2024) , language learning   (Chen & Lin, 2021) , and health education   (H. Zhang et al., 2022) . PLS improves active listening since students participate more due
to the roles they assume, which aids attention, information processing, and participation. These outcomes are consistent with other literature that supports peer teaching improves communic ation
and   responsibility   skills   (Gillies, 2016; Topping, 2005) .   Lastly, PLS positively influences the skills of summarizing and note - taking. Focus enhances as students gear up to “teach” their peers. Attention
is focused, and notes taken are systematic and precise. More importantly, students exhibit mastery of the mate rial, perform better on standardized and unit tests, and show improved academic
standing overall   (Crouch & Mazur, 2001; Topping, 2005b; Vickrey et al., 2015).

### 5.1. Study Contribution and Implications
This study contributes to the growing body of literature by showing how the Peer Lesson Strategy (PLS) improves both independent and active learning in a variety of ways, integrating the
comprehensive approach that previous studies have overlooked by focus ing on the more disparate facets of the learning process. Using Vygotsky’s sociocultural theory along with the theory of
multimodal learning, the study advances understanding of the creative, autonomous, responsible, and   engaged   roles   that   learners   activel y   and   willingly   assume   in   peer - supported   learning
environments. More importantly, the findings have adapted the concept of peer teaching for Indonesian higher education, demonstrating its use not only for enhancing academic achievement,
but for developing   critically important skills of the twenty - first century, which include: thinking, communication, collaboration, and learning throughout one’s life. These findings argue that PLS has
the potential to act as an instructional framework for higher education in stitutions that aim to promote learning and teaching for active and student - centered education. At the same time, the
impact of the study is not limited to the region, as its findings can be applied to address other issues in education around the world.
6.   Conclusion
The findings from the current study revealed the extent to which the Peer Lesson Strategy (PLS) promotes active and independent learning among students. Four specific aspects of independent
learning were clearly visible. Firstly, students demonstrated crea tivity by designing learning games and videos and developing learning resources to assist peers and facilitate learning. This type of
creativity demonstrated the ability to generate knowledge autonomously. Secondly, students' ownership of learning increase d as they prepared, taught, and ensured mastery of the lesson content
to their peers. Thirdly, increased student engagement and motivation were demonstrated when learners actively used different teaching skills and materials to involve students. Fourthly,   students
could concentrate and focus on learning materials, especially during the teacher and learner interaction, more than ever. This was evidenced by the mastery of content to be learnt, the
preparation required, and the focus needed to endure the learn ing activities actively.
In PLS, certain aspects of active learning occurred simultaneously. Students created diagrams and color - coded notes, which enhanced visual learning and the construction of significant learning
outcomes. Students' verbal participation increased as they assu med dual - role responsibilities, which encouraged them to ask, contribute, and discuss with their peers. In addition, active listening
improved as students attended, processed, and responded to peer - led lessons taught in class.
Students also learned advance d note - taking techniques. Their notes were shorter and more structured, and the material was better understood and remembered.
In short, the results support the effectiveness of the Peer Lesson Strategy in reinforcing independent and dependent learned indicators, including creativity, exploration, and learning
responsibility, trying new things, and concentrating on the taught mate rial, as well as active visual and verbal participation, listening, summarizing, and note - taking. These outcomes and behaviors
combined show the effectiveness of the Peer Lesson Strategy as a learner - centered approach in which students gain more autonomy a nd deeper involvement in classroom activities.

Peer Lesson Strategy is one of the techniques that pre - service teachers are trained in and encouraged to employ during their Teaching Practicum. They exercise learner autonomy, creativity,
and classroom management, though autonomy - supportive approaches sti ll predominate. The PLS is likely to foster further comfort around learner - centered techniques. Early targeting of this skill helps
them professionally practice as reflective and innovative educators.
For practicing teachers, using PLS is integrated into the daily teaching schedule to promote learner independence, engagement, and collaboration. PLS works best when integrated into multiple
lesson plans, allowing learners to master the technique and its a pplication with various learners. PLS is considered one of the frameworks in professional development training, focusing on learner -
centered teaching and PLS, so teachers gain the necessary training to practice learner - centered teaching.
Lecturers at the tertiary level, particularly in education departments, are urged to incorporate PLS into their instructional design. Adopting this strategy enhances the degree of engagement and
learning and models exemplary teaching practices for student   teachers. Support in the form of institutional policies, workshops, and materials is required to enable teachers to apply PLS practices
thoroughly and consistently over time.
8.   Research Limitations
This study has several limitations. First, it was carried out with a certain sample and one educational context, which might be a reason to constrain the generalizability of the study. Second,
the   study's limited duration meant that it was not possible to analyze the long - term impact of PLS on student learning . Lastly, the issues regarding the preparedness of the teachers, the available time,
and the institutional support have not yet been investigated in depth. However, these variables might influence the   sustainability of PLS in practice.
