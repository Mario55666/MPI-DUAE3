### Introduction
Science, Technology, Engineering, and Mathematics (STEM) education is essential to K-12 education (National Academies, 2011). It helps students
develop critical thinking, analytical capacity, and problem-solving skills, providing a pathway for students to acquire highly needed skills in the job
market, which are necessary to thrive in the 21st-century workforce.
The   National   Mathematics   and   Science   Initiative   (NMSI)   provided exceptional guidance   concerning   K-12 STEM   Education   (NMSI, 2023;
Saxton E, et al., 2014). Fostering innovation and creativity requires STEM knowledge that encourages students to open their minds, explore new ideas,
and develop inventive solutions to real-world problems. Achieving societal equity requires concerted efforts and activities to inspire students to become
future innovators and change-makers in their communities by fostering a culture of curiosity and experimentation. STEM education may help bridge
the inequality gap between low- and high-income families and regions, so it is critically important for students from underrepresented and low-income
families (Rocha J, et al., 2021). Equipping students with STEM knowledge helps level the playing field and empowers them to compete on equal footing.
The President's Council of Advisors on Science and Technology (PCAST) has identified and published five overarching recommendations to improve
STEM education for all students (PCAST, 2012). By emphasizing STEM education to students from underrepresented families, educators can empower

### Program Description: CSP Chemistry as STEM Case Study
CSP is one of the most significant programs empowering first-generation and low-income public-school students in the Chicagoland area, particularly in the
south side of Chicago, which has long been a region of socioeconomic disadvantage. In an environment where many talented and motivated students
are omitted from deserved opportunities and equitable access to resources and education due to their circumstances, the work of CSP directly addresses this
problem.
For nearly two decades, CSP has uplifted countless aspiring young scholars from disadvantaged backgrounds; in the process, CSP has (1)
produced invaluable human resources and numerous talents for society and the next generation of leaders and (2) reduced inequality in society by
engaging,   encouraging,   and   teaching   students   from   low-income   and underprivileged families and enhancing the confidence and foundation for
continued achievement, e.g., representation and sense of belonging in STEM and other critical fields (Chiu T, 2023).
CSP has provided a stable base for its students to engage with each other, their local community, the university community, and the broader academic
community as a whole; each class of incoming students is a cohort where the students learn together and from each other in a diverse and interdisciplinary
environment for years throughout their time in the program. For the 2023-24 Academic Year, there are currently 153 students enrolled in the Collegiate
Scholars Program, representing 43 high schools from across the district and city .   This dynamic program provides students with a unique environment to
learn from and interact with students from different backgrounds with various perspectives, ultimately offering participants an opportunity that students
would not ordinarily have without the program. In a COVID and post-COVID educational   landscape,   where   many   public   schools   have   suffered   from
canceled or diminished curricula due to strained resources and challenges with virtual learning, CSP leveraged the resources of the University of Chicago at
the disposal of its students and instructors. For example, students in the CSP chemistry classes conducted experiments in a university laboratory; for
students who were previously only exposed to virtual labs, this experience is

we   present   the   applications   of   student-centered   teaching   and   learning pedagogies to class activities, encouraging students to engage in critical
thinking and problem-solving and leading students from passive to active learning.
### Class Structure and Materials
The course includes lectures, discussions, demonstrations, labs, office hours, peer review/assessment, and out-of-classroom activities as a flapped class
practice. The Canvas course management platform is a digital facility used for shared course materials, digital communication and discussions, homework
and lab report submissions, and feedback.
The class materials cover chemistry fundamentals, from data analysis to laboratory safety, an overview of the historical development of science, atoms,
molecules, gas reactions, acid/base chemistry, solid metallurgical chemistry, and electrochemical energy. The goal is for students to establish a solid
chemistry foundation, and we can inspire students to think independently,

### Office Hours and Discussions
The discussion sessions are held weekly by a teaching assistant (TA), and the instructor and the TA hold office hours a couple of times per week, adjusted
if more are needed. These are the perfect times to answer students' questions, lead for problem-solving, and debate the open questions and the end-of-lecture
questions assigned by the instructor.
The   open   questions   and   inquiry-based   questions   are   from   weekly homework assignments. The instructor asks additional open and review
questions at the end of each lecture instead of only summarizing the contents.
The conversational debate style allows students to express themselves freely in a scholarly environment during the discussion and office hours, which is a
culture that enhances the students' critical thinking skills for creativity and builds confidence in the students toward STEM directions. It also makes the
students feel "at home," a profound sense of community and belonging.
We employ the online Canvas Discussion Board to facilitate on-time communication, learning, and answering questions. The instructor and TA
may open discussion for each new subject matter by posting a few open-ended or provocative questions about a chemical subject. Everyone in the class can
contribute and comment. For each new class topic, each student was required to comment on or answer other students' online questions at least three times
and post at least two questions. It has been a great way to facilitate speedy communication, open mind, and meld into the community.
### Laboratory Experiments
Hands-on experiments are a significant class component that students usually love most. The labs include some conventional lab experiments and a project-
based solar cell lab experiment that takes students three weeks to complete.
Table 2 shows a sample lab schedule for a class of six weeks.
The project-based solar cell lab is a real-world problem concerning clean energy and our natural environment. Students are requested to create solar
cells in the lab to generate electric power, i.e., measurable electric voltage and current.
In the first week, students were assigned into groups – four students per group. Generally, they are advised to read several papers from journals and
online articles and study them in the first week. During this week, students can research and get information about the subject matter from anywhere they can

### Assessment Outcomes
The CSP Chemistry curriculum has been part of the program for nearly 20 years. We have continuously improved our teaching and learning pedagogies
to meet students' needs during this period, with particular attention on stimulating students for STEM education.
There are many proposed models for integrated STEM assessment, such as those proposed by Bicer et al. (Bicer A, et al., 2017). Commercial software
for educational class assessment is also available if desired. For our program, we conducted an anonymous survey within our class system to evaluate the
class and avoid bias.
We asked students to answer some questions related to the effectiveness of the class and the influence of this course on their future studies and career
choices. In the following discussion, we selected the 2019 class as an example where 36 students participated in CSP chemistry, which is a large class, while

### Discussions
From the assessment and the quantitatively measured quotients in graphs, the CSP chemistry yielded quite a positive impact on improving   students'
knowledgebase and attitude toward STEM classes, promoting a sense of psychological belonging, community, and potential consideration to pursue
future careers in STEM.
It is incredibly gratifying that CSP chemistry has been successful in helping to develop the next generation of scientists and engineers. However,
we should cool down, reflect on some issues encountered in the class, and make a concerted effort for future improvement. The assessed outcomes
(Figures 2-4) show that the program and the class are mainly positive and successful; roughly 90% of the students evaluated all the measured quantities

---

Inspiring the Next Generation of Scientists and Engineers   19 as positive or very positive, and a few percent positioned themselves as
neutral.
Most students have fulfilled their expectations of the class and enjoyed learning as well as the learning process. Some started asking questions about
STEM works in detail, while others sought opportunities for potential research work at faculty labs and potential career direction. Nevertheless, not all were
positive. There were negative marks on some of the items, as shown as red marks on the figures.
By the end of the class, there were still red marks, 3%-6%, even though it was a small number of the 36 students. Still, it indicates that more effort is
required of us to bring these students to the mainstream of students with confidence (Spezza S, 2023). From the beginning of the class, we noted that
students seemed divided into two camps of attitude toward science classes and STEM as a whole. Some were quite positive and enthusiastic and appeared to
be intentionally enquiring and focused. At the same time, some were passive and lacked confidence. These students were curious and eager and wanted to
try but were somewhat timid, which was precisely the students we wanted to stimulate. These students needed more attention and further help, and some
personal learning pedagogies might have been helpful.
Multiple factors likely affected their progress, at least in belonging. We believe it is still an issue of belonging. They still have that psychological
barrier to pass and are uncertain about their studies and the future. To seek consistent improvement, we must focus more on meeting students with
kindness and concern for their academic progress and mental and physical well-being   while   enhancing   their   learning   and   participating   in   various
activities. It means that we will need more personalized learning activities concerning their educational and mental health progress to successfully inspire
and cultivate them to become the next generation of scientists and engineers.
