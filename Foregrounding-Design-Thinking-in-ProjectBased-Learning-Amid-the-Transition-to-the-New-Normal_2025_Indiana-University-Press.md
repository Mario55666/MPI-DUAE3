**ABSTRACT**
Incorporating design thinking in project - based learning (PjBL) can generate a new praxis to epitomise an ecology of active - learner approaches and embrace the authenticity of collaborative problem - based
learning (PBL). Design - oriented disciplines in higher education are expanding their teaching and research objectives to prepare future designers to confron t the increasingly complex problems arising
in the new normal. This paper discusses the learning outcomes of two joint collaborative PjBL projects by higher education institutes in Hong Kong and Australia. We explore the application of the
theoretical stru cture of design thinking to multidisciplinary and interdisciplinary PjBL design projects where students partner with community stakeholders and professionals to construct new social
innovation. The paper explains how empathetic design can encourage transdi sciplinary student engagement, enhance learners’ motivation, and generate a sustainable impact on society.
Keywords:   project - based learning ,   design thinking ,   experiential learning ,   collaborative design ,   higher education ,   wicked problems

education paradigm — which focused on individuality — is no longer capable of providing 21st century competencies, as designers are expo sed to a much more dynamic environment (Buchanan, 2001). The

Theoretical Implications of Design Thinking in Transdisciplinary Problem - based Learning Design is inherently transdisciplinary and applies to any area of human experience for its
universality (Buchanan, 1992). Although PBL and PjBL are both instructional methods in which students learn through facilitated problem solving, there are subtle nua nces between them.
While both PBL and PjBL support innovative learner - centred approaches by empowering learners to integrate theory to practice (Savery, 2015), there are different pedagogies in relation to how
the problem is defined, the role of the problem, the process of l earning, the roles of tutors, evaluation, and assessment (Hmelo - Silver, 2004; Savery, 2015). According to Savery (2015), the characteristics of
PBL involves the selection of ill - structured problems and a tutor guides the learning process and conducts a tho rough debriefing at the conclusion of the learning experience, while Hmelo - Silver
(2004) describes PBL as an instructional method where students learn through facilitating problem solving that centres around complex issues that might not have a direct answ er. PBL is not designed
for teaching well - defined problems, but ill - structured/ill - defined problems (Hmelo - Silver, 2004), and the learners have to define their interdisciplinary problem statement by integrating viewpoints across
disciplines (Brassler & Det tmers, 2017).

Effective design thinking is used to tackle the complexities of wicked problems. Wicked problems (Rittel & Webber, 1973) can best be described as ill - defined and loosely formulated. They
are subject to redefinition and resolution in different ways over tim e (Coyne, 2005) and can be best addressed by exploring “concrete integrations of knowledge that combine theory with practice for
new productive purposes” (Buchanan, 1992, p. 6). Since PBL is not designed for teaching well - defined problems, but rather ill - s tructured problems, designers are often confronted with the ill - structured
case to define their interdisciplinary problem statement. According to Dorst (2011), designers have been tackling difficult (wicked) problems for a long time, and the designing disc iplines have created
extensive professional methods to help them. Design thinking can be combined with PBL and PjBL to empower learners to integrate stakeholders’ viewpoints in the learning experience to facilitate
interactive engagement with user experien ce, as well as engaging students in learning the expected content (Torp & Sage, 2002) in the processes of empathy and ideation.
Among the different streams of design thinking ideologies (Johansson - Sköldberg et al., 2013), the Hasso Plattner Institute of Design at Stanford, also known as d.school, has proposed a five - stage
model of design thinking that can be effectively implemented   in design education for its high adaptability. In this model, the abstract concept is crystallised as a process where non - linear actions —
namely empathise, define, ideate, prototype, and test — operate in overlapping spaces (Figure 1). These non - linear stage s are best understood as a series of dispositions of systems where higher - order
learning takes place.

The second stage is the define stage, which delineates questions of inquiry to clear learning objectives and strategic goals. In this stage, learners synthesise their empathetic understanding of
users and redefine the driving questions into an actionable d esign statement that guides the later stages. Instructors often find themselves in the dilemma of how to balance the fulfilment of a
predetermined curricular content in the rigid scope of the course versus the students’ freedom, as promised to grant in an   authentic PjBL experience. The driving question of the PjBL should pose
cognitive challenges to elicit students’ curiosity and prevail in an authentic learning experience (Savery & Duffy, 1995). The key goal here is to develop a stronger sense of ownership   and motivate
learners in a more constructive dialogue into the decision - making initiatives (Blumenfeld et al., 1991).
In this stage, students are very likely to be confronted with hurdles and challenges.
The process of ideation in design thinking is not linear but iterative, which could challenge students’ abilities of handling failures and require them to make a design decision. With design
thinking, the understanding of the social interactions is part of   the design process (Dorst, 2006). Ideate is the main production process in which the learners generate conceptual ideas beyond conventional
thinking.   The   learning   should   not   be   circumscribed   by   disciplinary   boundaries.   Through   a participatory platform, st udents cooperate with faculties, external experts, and communities to collate
ideas and knowledge from diverse fields (Sanders & Stappers, 2008). In the later prototype and test stages, learners make experimental artefacts and present possible propositions   such as storyboards,
scaled models, service scenarios, and rapid - prototyped 3D print outputs.
In PjBL, students need to solve a problem connected to the given task by keeping the final product in mind; there is an expectation to produce an authentic, practical, “tangible” solution
(Brassler & Dettmers, 2017). While the ill - structured problem can be   predefined by the instructor, the prototypes are considered part of the learning outcomes aligned with the principle of constructive
alignment (Biggs, 1996; Biggs & Tang, 2011). Prototyping requires students to materialise their solutions and validate the ir hypothesis of the subject matters, whereas testing provides a robust
validation of the quality and integrity of their developed solutions. Low fidelity prototyping or sketch models can present ideas visually and prompt timely feedback for revision. As a n iterative process,
testing solicits stakeholders’ feedback to generate dialogues with users to rectify solutions and validate ideas. These interim comments provide insights for students to advance their solutions
further and build up learning confidence   through embracing mistakes and failures. Studies find that rapid prototyping allows practitioners to better embrace failure as they are more inclined to interpret
failure as an opportunity to learn (Gerber & Carroll, 2012). Therefore, the five stages of de sign thinking help learners build 21st century skills and dispositions, such as critical thinking, creativity, and
collaboration (Rotherham & Willingham, 2009; Trilling & Fadel, 2009), while also nurturing forward - thinking and advocating for the iterative   generations of proposition testing or prototypes for problem -
solving (Brenner et al., 2016).

Identifying the extreme housing shortage as one of the “wicked problems” in Hong Kong, a group of design students in a higher education institute teamed up with a group of professors and
students from the School of Design and Architecture from a university   in Melbourne to work collaboratively on a social design project. A PjBL experience was formed from individuals with
expertise   in   industrial   design,   interior   architecture,   and   architecture   to   devise   solutions   for underprivileged residents living in extreme ly small apartments called subdivided units (SDU) in
Hong Kong. The entire PjBL spanned over a year and a half, encompassing desk - top research on the urban context in these extremely small “nano - flats” which range from 100 to 200 square - feet in Hong

This PjBL aimed to develop built - environment related improvements for children in such a socioeconomically disadvantaged setting. The team engaged with social workers from the local
community centre, primary school teachers, and professionals to explore po ssible interventions that could   help   SDU   children   to   confront   the   challenges   resulting   from   the   overcrowded   living

stakeholders to understand how the well - being of children living in SDUs was impacted by the complexity of Hong Kong’s wicked housing problem. Students were made aware that the inferior
living conditions of SD Us have profound negative consequences for child learning development.

### Discussion   and Implications
Design thinking, as interpreted by Buchanan (1992), is a means of coalescing the “designerly” competencies, skills, and attitudes for confronting complex problems. The applications of abductive
reasoning   and   the   open   attitude   of   embracing   uncertainty   in   de sign   thinking   are   becoming increasingly prominent in the agendas of design education when viewing the many wicked problems
in our society. By adopting a design thinking approach and embracing the poignant challenges, it is proven that design students can o vercome geographical challenges, time constraints, and cultural
boundaries to generate productive learning and teaching outcomes. A major benefit of linking design thinking with PjBL observed in this study is the development of students’ abilities to clear ly define
project parameters in the early stages, which helped significantly when moving towards the ideate stage of the PjBL experience. We conclude that this is largely due to the free exploration of inquiry
focused on understanding the problem from a us er - centred design perspective before any design work takes place. A recent study by Cummings & Yur - Austin (2022) confirms this point by stating,
“the empathy students developed at the initial stage of project development inspired their enthusiasm toward th e project and helped to unleash their natural creativity” (p. 130). This motivational dynamic
helps students endure the ambiguous nature of new product development and ensures successful knowledge exchange with industry partners.
Incorporating design thinking into PjBL enables students to explore a wide range of societal challenges from a human - centred perspective in a collaborative manner. Through the cultivation of a
user experience ethos and acquisition of relevant strategies, s tudents can develop interdisciplinary and cognitive skills essential in the 21st century. When we extend the execution of PjBL in the social
realm, we see the possibilities of sustainable social innovations where students are posed with real - world problems   and work with real actors within the system to seek solutions. These projects — largely
participatory in nature — provide valuable learning opportunities for students to practise design ethics in a real - world context when they are analysing and reflecting on   the lives of others through the design
means. Ezio Manzini, founder of Design for Social Innovation and Sustainability, defined design for social innovation as “a constellation of design initiatives geared toward making social innovation
probable, effectiv e, long - last lasting and apt to spread” (Manzini, 2013, p. 65). We share his vision that design schools can be the pioneers to trigger, enact, and catalyse initiatives to bring forward more
sustainable social transformation (Manzini, 2011). The next steps   are to champion design education | www.ijpbl.org (ISSN 1541 - 5015)   Spring   202 5   | Volume 1 9   | Issue 1

of marginalised citizens in collective decision - making. Through collaborations with communities, NGOs, and other agencies with divergent expertise, design educ ation can leverage PjBL projects to
generate sustainable social impact.
Our approach in linking PjBL with the five - stage design thinking methodology affords a sixth stage of “manufacture” — translating research findings into tangible product outcomes. This is a novel
contribution for multidisciplinary and interdisciplinary PjBL   design projects, where students partner with community stakeholders and/or industry partners. It is at this sixth stage when partners see
explicit value in the engagement, as unrealized ideas are now realised. This progresses the theoretical framework of d esign thinking and its toolkits beyond a methodological theory and realises new ideas
into practice.
Key contributions connecting the actionable steps to support design thinking in PjBL (seen in Figure 2 and Figure 4) highlights the main paradigms of “designerly” ways of approaching a problem
(i.e., being collaborative, practical, human - centred, and creat ing novel solutions that offer value to those who engage). A strong focus at the beginning of the design process in building empathy into
the   problem   statement   assists   in   greater   awareness   of   user   needs   and   ensures   the   concept development stage is well - def ined. Advancing this to the end of the design process, we provide
greater focus on tacit industry knowledge to ensure the developed outcome(s) have commercial relevance with authentic application. By including a “manufacture” stage, tested prototypes can
a dvance into commercial outcomes, bridging the gap between a “good idea” and a realised outcome with commercial distinction. This is the true value of combining design thinking with project - based
learning for both students involved in the learning experienc e, as well as those who engage in the end product outcome.
PjBL could be widely used in fields beyond medicine, architecture, and engineering in higher education. It could also be applied to resolve real - life problems, while also developing students’ skills
of problem - solving, collaboration, negotiating, planning,   and organisation. Research on PjBL could associate with the development of students’ holistic competencies and what teaching approaches
could be employed to enhance the effectiveness of PjBL. The assessment of PjBL will also be a novel topic to explore in   future research.
