Abstract This qualitative case study explored the integration of Multimedia Arts into Science, Technology, Engineering, and Math-
ematics (STEM) education, transforming it into Science, Technology, Engineering, Arts, and Mathematics (STEAM) for special education students. Grounded in the principles of Universal Design for Learning (UDL) and John Dewey’s edu-
cational theories emphasizing experiential learning tailored to students’ interests and abilities, the study investigated
how integrating multimedia arts could enhance the accessibility and engagement of STEM education for students with disabilities. Data was collected from classroom observations to examine student engagement and learning processes,
students’   artwork, reflective journals to capture students’   lived experiences, and semi-structured interviews with students
to gain insights into the cognitive and creative outcomes of arts-integrated STEM learning. By examining the intersec- tion of arts and STEM disciplines through a UDL lens focused on promoting inclusive education, the study found that
multimedia arts integration significantly improved engagement, accessibility, and comprehension of STEM concepts for students with disabilities. Students demonstrated enhanced cognitive and creative outcomes, indicating a deeper
understanding of STEM subjects. This qualitative study contributes to understanding how to design dynamic, personal- ized STEAM learning environments that enable diverse learners, including those with special needs, to deeply engage
with and demonstrate comprehension of STEM concepts. Findings illuminate strategies for making STEM more equitable through arts integration, suggesting that incorporating multimedia arts can effectively promote inclusivity and engage-
ment in STEM education for special education students.
Keywords   John dewey’s educational theories · Multimedia arts · Special education · STEAM education · Universal design for learning
1   Introduction The performance gap in STEM fields between students with disabilities and their peers is not just a statistic; it reflects the
systemic barriers that exist within our educational systems. These barriers include limited access to inclusive resources,
insufficient accommodations, and a lack of tailored pedagogical strategies. Such challenges hinder not only the academic
achievement of students with disabilities but also their potential contributions to innovation and creativity in STEM fields.
Research shows that students with disabilities are vastly underrepresented in STEM careers, a problem exacerbated by the traditional educational models that fail to accommodate diverse learning needs [21]. Addressing this gap is crucial
to ensuring equity in STEM education.

### 2   Research questions
This study addresses two main research questions and two related sub-questions:
1.   How does the integration of multimedia arts into STEM education impact the engagement and learning experiences
of students with disabilities?
2.   Sub-question 1: In what ways does multimedia arts integration influence the comprehension and retention of STEM
concepts among students with disabilities?
3.   How does multimedia arts integration foster creative thinking and problem-solving skills in STEM education for
students with disabilities?
4.   Sub-question 2: What challenges and opportunities arise in implementing multimedia arts-based STEM curricula for
students with disabilities?
### 3   Literature review
The integration of arts into STEM education, transforming it into STEAM, has gained significant attention as a means to enhance learning experiences, making them more engaging, interdisciplinary, and accessible, particularly for students
with disabilities. This literature review explores the theoretical underpinnings, cognitive benefits, and practical imple-
mentations of STEAM education, underscoring its relevance and potential in special education contexts.
3.1   John dewey’s educational theories: crafting meaningful learning through experience Our research is deeply rooted in the educational philosophy of John Dewey’s Experiential Learning Theory, who cham-
pioned the idea that education should be a process of living and not just a preparation for future living [8]. Dewey’s emphasis on experiential learning — where education is crafted around students’ experiences, interests, and abilities —
informs every aspect of our study. This principle serves as the theoretical framework, guiding our approach to creating a
STEM education framework that is as engaging as it is educational, particularly for students with disabilities.

3.2   Universal design for learning: designing for inclusion and engagement in STEAM The Universal Design for Learning (UDL) framework complements Dewey’s theories, which call for curriculum and teach-
ing methods to be designed from the outset to accommodate all learners. UDL’s focus on providing multiple means of representation, expression, and engagement mirrors Dewey’s vision for an education system that adapts to the learner
rather than forcing the learner to adapt to the system [20]. By applying UDL principles, we aimed to ensure that our educational interventions were accessible and beneficial for students with diverse learning needs [3].
Recent studies have shown that combining UDL principles with STEAM creates inclusive and engaging learning envi- ronments, particularly for students with disabilities [27]. This approach allows students to actively participate in their
educational journey through creative and flexible learning methods. The customizable nature of UDL, when integrated with multimedia arts, provides opportunities for students to engage with STEM content in ways that are both accessible
and innovative [15].
### 3.3   Multimedia arts: bridging the gap between creativity and STEM learning
Integrating multimedia arts into STEM education is where Dewey’s and UDL’s paths converge with our research. By utiliz- ing multimedia arts, we tapped into the experiential, reflective, and engaging nature of learning that Dewey envisioned
while adhering to the UDL principles of making education accessible and interactive. This approach allowed us to cre- ate a learning environment where students with disabilities were not just passive recipients of knowledge but active
participants in their educational journey.
Research by Bequette and Bequette [4] supports the idea that the integration of art into STEM education fosters crea- tivity and problem-solving skills. They argue that incorporating the arts encourages students to engage more deeply
with STEM subjects by allowing them to express their learning in diverse ways. This notion is particularly relevant for students with disabilities, who often benefit from non-traditional modes of learning and representation [31].
### 3.4   Special education: empowering learners through arts‑infused STEM
The inclusion of special education students in STEAM programs has shown promising results in terms of engagement, cognitive development, and creative expression [31]. This approach aligns with UDL principles, ensuring that educational
content is accessible to all students [2], and emphasizes that integrating arts into STEM education can support learners
in STEM, thereby promoting cognitive benefits and cultural inclusivity.
Recent research highlights the importance of creating accessible STEAM curricula for students with diverse needs, focusing on equitable access to resources and training for educators [14]. These studies collectively underscore the
multifaceted benefits of STEAM education, particularly for special education students. By integrating arts into STEM, educators can foster a more inclusive, engaging, and cognitively enriching learning environment, ultimately supporting
the holistic development of all students [12].
### 3.5   STEAM education: fostering creativity, innovation, and accessibility
The inclusion of arts in STEM education, transforming it into STEAM, has gained traction to foster creativity and enhance
learning experiences. Land [16] argued that STEAM education enhances students’ cognitive development and fosters higher-order thinking and problem-solving skills. For special education students, integrating the arts into STEM creates
an inclusive learning environment tailored to individual needs [27].

### 4   Research method
Our methodology is the compass that guides our exploration into the enriching intersection of multimedia arts and STEM education for students with disabilities. By adopting a qualitative case study approach, we examine deep into the
nuanced realities of our participants’ educational experiences. This methodology allowed us to capture the complex dynamics of learning environments, pedagogical strategies, and student outcomes in a detailed and holistic manner.
The heart of our study lies in our participants — students navigating the worlds of arts and STEM. This deliberate choice
ensured that our investigation benefited from a rich tapestry of perspectives, highlighting the diverse ways in which multimedia arts can enhance STEM education. Through this, we aimed to uncover patterns and insights that are broadly
applicable yet deeply rooted in individual experiences [33].
An exploratory case study method undergirded this project. Yin [32] defined the case study method as “an approach to research that facilitates exploration of a phenomenon within its context using a variety of data sources” (p. 544). Yin
and much of the documented research on STEAM indicated that an exploratory case study is an appropriate method for answering “how and what” questions [2, 17, 33]. Exploratory case studies illustrate a unique circumstance that needs to
be detailed and described in relation to the larger topic of investigation [7]. The experiences of students with disabilities
with multimedia arts activities in STEM presented just such a unique circumstance.
We employed various methods to gather data, each contributing a unique lens through which to view our research questions. To better organize and present our data sources, we created a table (Table 1) summarizing the methods used,
the timing of data collection, and which research questions each method addressed. Structured observations provided a window into the real-time dynamics of classroom engagement and interaction with multimedia arts. These observations
occurred during STEAM class sessions where multimedia arts tools were integrated, focusing on student interaction and participation. The structured observations aimed to answer research questions related to engagement and accessibility.
By examining students’ artwork, we assessed the tangible outcomes of our pedagogical strategies, seeking evidence of deepened understanding and creativity. Each piece of artwork was linked to specific media arts in the STEM topic and
evaluated based on creativity and comprehension of STEM concepts.
Reflective journals offered an introspective look at the students’   learning journeys, capturing their challenges, epipha-
nies, and the evolution of their thinking. Participants were asked to submit reflective journals at three key points during
the study: before, during, and after their multimedia art projects. Journal prompts included questions such as: How has integrating arts into STEM changed your understanding of this subject? and What challenges did you face when using
multimedia tools? Lastly, semi-structured interviews with students allowed us to collect nuanced, qualitative insights, enriching our understanding of the impact of multimedia arts integration on STEM learning for students with disabilities
[28]. The interviews, which lasted 30–45 min each, were conducted after the completion of the multimedia arts projects.
Questions focused on students’ experiences with arts-based learning, the accessibility of the tools used, and the overall
impact on their understanding of STEM concepts. Data collected for this study included transcripts from the audio of semi-structured interviews with students.
Data for this study also included the researchers’ field notes that were coded throughout the study. Field notes were recorded during observations and immediately after interviews to capture contextual details, reflections, and emerging
questions. Researcher notes included everything that happened in the study, when it happened, thoughts, questions, con- cerns, etc. Students’   artwork was coded for our interpretation. Thematic analysis [5] was used to organize these data’s codes,
themes, and categories meaningfully. Braun and Clarke’s six-step method involved “Step 1: familiarizing yourself with your
data, Step 2: Generating initial codes, Step 3: Searching for themes, Step 4: Reviewing themes, Step 5: Defining and naming

4.1   Setting, participants, and sampling procedure This research was conducted at the University of Southeastern and Northeastern US, providing a diverse and rich setting for
exploring the intersection of multimedia arts and STEM education. The university’s commitment to inclusive education made
it an ideal location for this study, ensuring access to a broad spectrum of resources and support systems for students with
disabilities.
The study was carried out in a hybrid format, combining in-person and online sessions. This format provided flexibility for students with physical or sensory disabilities, ensuring inclusivity. The digital platforms used, such as Adobe Creative
Cloud, were accessible in both settings, enabling all students to participate equally, regardless of the learning environment.
Participants included ten undergraduate students with documented disabilities enrolled in STEM classes. These dis- abilities included physical impairments (e.g., mobility issues), learning disabilities (e.g., dyslexia), and sensory impair-
ments (e.g., hearing or visual impairments). By focusing on students with diverse disabilities, we examined how multime-
dia tools such as Adobe Creative Cloud and Code Scratch addressed their specific learning needs. For example, students with visual impairments utilized voice-over tools in Adobe Voice Animator to interact with STEM concepts, while students
with physical impairments benefited from the accessibility features of these digital platforms, allowing them to engage fully with the tasks. Out of the ten participants, five were selected for detailed data analysis. These five participants, all
referred to by pseudonyms to protect their identities, provided a diverse representation of disabilities, including physi-
cal, learning, and sensory impairments. This deliberate selection process was intended to facilitate a detailed, in-depth
qualitative analysis, aligning with the exploratory nature of the case study methodology, which seeks to provide rich, contextualized understandings rather than broad generalizations [7, 33].
By focusing on this specific group of students, our research aimed to uncover the nuanced ways in which multimedia arts can enhance the educational experiences of students with disabilities. This deliberate focus on a small, diverse sample
allowed us to explore students’ individual and collective experiences, providing a comprehensive understanding of the benefits and challenges of integrating multimedia arts into STEM education.
4.2   Data analysis We adopted a holistic approach to analyze our data within the methodological frame of a case study. We used thematic
analysis [5] to organize and code data meaningfully. Initial codes were generated to capture aspects of the data that were
deemed interesting and germane to our research questions and theoretical stance, allowing for subsequent organization of these data. This enabled us to categorize emerging themes and make connections among the research questions.
After completing this analysis, we conducted a deductive analysis using the assigned codes, allowing for increased dependability. We continued refining our themes, eliminating overlap, to express the essence of each identified theme.
The thematic analysis was aligned with our theoretical frameworks, including UDL and Dewey’s experiential learning, to examine how these frameworks were reflected in students’ experiences and outcomes. We used qualitative data analysis
software (QDAS) such as ATLAS.ti or Nvivo in this process. The researchers’ field notes and students’ artwork maintained
by us were included for analysis.

### 6   Findings
6.1   Cognitive enhancements through STEAM Our study’s exploration into the integration of STEAM (Science, Technology, Engineering, Arts, and Mathematics) edu-
cation for students with disabilities has unveiled significant cognitive benefits, affirming the value of this approach in special education contexts.
Firstly, we observed considerable cognitive gains among special education students immersed in STEAM curricula.
These improvements were not limited to academic achievements; they also included fundamental enhancements in critical cognitive functions such as memory retention, problem-solving, and critical thinking. For example, obser-
vations revealed that Nathaniel’s ability to recall the steps of chemical reactions improved significantly after creat- ing animations through the Wick Editor. He reflected that “creating animations through the Wick Editor helped me
remember the steps of a chemical reaction much better than just reading about them.” This observation highlights the role of multimedia tools in fostering deeper cognitive engagement and comprehension. Additionally, analysis of
Nathaniel’s animations demonstrated his ability to sequence chemical reactions effectively, further validating these cognitive gains (see Fig. 3). This finding supports our claim that integrating arts into STEM lessons can significantly
enhance cognitive engagement for students with disabilities.
Regarding problem-solving skills, Carlos demonstrated marked improvement during the project when he used AutoDraw to create real-time diagrams that visualized complex chemical concepts. Initially struggling with abstract
ideas, Carlos stated that using visual tools “made it easier to see how things fit together in chemistry.” Observational
data showed his growing confidence in breaking down tasks and systematically solving problems during the process, which exemplifies enhanced problem-solving capabilities.
Critical analytical thinking was evident in Tanner’s projects, where he created animations to represent molecular structures. Observers noted Tanner’s ability to analyze the relationships between components of chemical structures

Secondly, personalized learning experiences provided through STEAM, enriched with multimedia arts, have proven essential in addressing the diverse cognitive needs of special education students. By tailoring learning experiences to the
individual, these students have accessed new ways to process, understand, and apply knowledge, breaking down barriers to cognitive accessibility and fostering a deeper, more inclusive learning environment. For instance, Carlos initially struggled

how things fit together in chemistry." This observation highlights the effectiveness of customized visual aids in promoting
understanding for students with learning disabilities, as further supported by his accurately detailed diagrams submitted
during the study (see Fig. 6). This highlights the importance of customizing tools to support individual learning styles [24].
Thirdly, the role of creative engagement within STEAM acted as a catalyst for cognitive flexibility and innovative think-
ing. Projects requiring creative thought, such as creating characters in Piskel App (see Fig. 5), stimulated neural connec-
tions and fostered a more engaging and inclusive learning environment. For example, Tanner mentioned that creating animations allowed him to see stories come to life, which he found “incredible” and indicative of enhanced cognitive
flexibility and creative thinking [9, 25]. For instance, our study found that Tanner’s experience with creating animations
demonstrated how STEAM projects can enhance cognitive flexibility and creative thinking. Moreover, Sherry’s involve- ment in digital media arts demonstrated how these tools supported creative expression and comprehension of STEM
concepts. He explained, “Working with Code Scratch (see Fig. 4) allowed me to break down a problem into smaller steps.
I understood the science better because I had to think about each step creatively.” Sherry’s project exemplified how computational tools promote both stepwise thinking and problem-solving, which is crucial for STEM comprehension.
Lastly, an unexpected yet impactful finding has been the role of metacognition and self-reflection within the STEAM framework. Students have developed a deeper understanding of their thought patterns and learning preferences by
engaging in projects encouraging reflection on their learning processes and cognitive strategies. This metacognitive approach has empowered students, giving them tools to adapt and thrive in various learning situations. Vincent shared,
“STEAM showed me that art is a big part of understanding science. I didn’t realize how much drawing and reflecting on what I created could help me learn better.”   This was further supported by his reflective journal entries, where he described
how visualizing concepts through art helped him structure his study methods more effectively. His testimony highlights the importance of self-awareness in learning, particularly for students with disabilities, and how STEAM encourages
metacognitive practices that improve learning outcomes [34].
The creative aspects of STEAM education can help special education students develop critical thinking and problem- solving skills [4]. The arts provide opportunities for students to explore diverse perspectives, experiment with new
ideas, and develop higher-order thinking skills [23]. However, successful implementation of STEAM for special education students requires careful consideration of factors such as resource availability, specialized educator training, and the
development of inclusive assessment methods [12, 19, 26].
These individual testimonies and project analyses provide clear evidence of the cognitive benefits of STEAM educa- tion, reinforcing its role as a transformative educational approach. Integrating STEAM for students with disabilities is
not just about academic achievement; it’s about fostering cognitive growth and development. Through this approach, we educate students and equip them with the cognitive tools and skills necessary for lifelong learning and success.
This comprehensive understanding of the cognitive benefits of STEAM reinforces its importance as a transformative educational approach.

### 8   Conclusions
As we stand at the threshold of a new era in educational pedagogy, the integration of multimedia arts into STEM — transforming it into STEAM — represents a pivotal shift in how we approach teaching and learning, particularly for
special education students. Our exploration into this interdisciplinary framework has illuminated the vast potential of multimedia arts to enrich and enhance the educational landscape, making complex concepts more accessible, engaging,
and meaningful for students with diverse learning needs.
The journey of integrating media arts with science in the context of special education has underscored the profound impact such a curriculum can have on student engagement, cognitive development, and creative expression. Through
this innovative approach, we have observed improvements in memory retention, problem-solving capabilities, and critical thinking among students. Nathaniel’s and Carlos’s experiences, for example, highlight how STEAM can make
learning more engaging and accessible, transforming abstract concepts into tangible, interactive learning experiences.
However, the evolution of STEAM education does not end with its implementation. As our findings indicate, several challenges remain in ensuring that STEAM education reaches its full potential, particularly for students with disabilities.
These include resource availability, educator training, and equitable access, which are critical for the successful integra-
tion of multimedia arts into STEM. For instance, as observed in our study, students like Nathaniel and Carlos benefited from digital tools, but ensuring access to such resources across all educational settings remains a challenge. To address
these challenges, we propose the following recommendations: 1   Resource Availability   Adequate funding and resources are crucial to providing the necessary technological tools and
materials for a successful STEAM curriculum. This includes ensuring that schools have access to multimedia platforms that cater to the diverse needs of special education students.
2   Educator Training   Professional development for educators is essential to equip them with the skills needed to integrate
arts into STEM and apply inclusive teaching methods. Ongoing support and training will ensure that teachers can adapt to the interdisciplinary nature of STEAM and make STEM concepts accessible and engaging for all learners.
3   Equitable Access   Schools must address systemic barriers that limit access to STEAM programs for students with dis-
abilities. This includes implementing policies that promote equal access to technological resources, adaptive tools, and inclusive practices that cater to diverse learning needs.
Innovative assessment models are also necessary to capture the diverse expressions of understanding among special education students. Traditional assessments may not fully reflect the cognitive and creative growth fostered by STEAM
education. As seen in Vincent’s and Tanner’s experiences, reflective practices and creative outputs can provide more comprehensive evaluations of student learning.
In closing, the future of STEAM education lies not just in the integration of arts with science but in our collective willingness to reimagine what education can be. By fostering an environment that values creativity, inclusivity, and
lifelong learning, we can empower every student to reach their fullest potential. By addressing the challenges identified
in our study — such as resource availability, educator training, and equitable access — and implementing the proposed recommendations, we can create a robust STEAM curriculum that benefits all students. This commitment to innovation
and inclusivity will not only enhance the educational experiences of students with disabilities but also pave the way for
a more holistic and enriched approach to learning. The integration of STEAM, with its emphasis on creativity and inter- disciplinary learning, holds the promise of preparing students for an increasingly complex and interconnected world,
equipping them with the skills and knowledge necessary for lifelong success.
