1. I ntroduction
Islamic Religious Education (IRE) at the higher education level is increasingly recognized as a crucial vehicle for shaping morally grounded, spiritually reflective, and intellectually capable students.
In the context of Indonesia and other Muslim - majority   societies, this educational field is tasked not only with transmitting classical religious knowledge but also with preparing learners to navigate the
complexities of contemporary life while upholding Islamic values (Baharuddin et al., 2024). However, curr ent pedagogical practices often fall short of this transformative mission. Traditional lecture - based
methods continue to dominate, favoring one - way knowledge transmission and rote memorization over reflective engagement and critical inquiry (Ayyad, 2022; B ensaid & Machouche, 2017; Omar,
2022; Widyawati & Nurhayati, 2023). This approach, while historically and culturally significant, has demonstrated limited success in cultivating students' ability to internalize values or apply Islamic
teachings to modern e thical dilemmas (Ayyad, 2022; Felsenthal & Agbaria, 2025).
Recent literature highlights that Islamic Religious Education faces multi - layered challenges.
These include curricular fragmentation, lack of contextual relevance, and pedagogical stagnation in addressing students' cognitive and affective needs (Abdul Khal il et al., 2024; Ulfat, 2020). In regions
such as Aceh, the challenge is further intensified by the need to balance national educational standards   with   local   religio - cultural   traditions   (Razali   et   al.,   2024).   Meanwhile,   inconsistent
integration of national   values and religious moderation across universities results in uneven graduate competencies (Faisal et al., 2021; Yani et al., 2023). Teaching methods also remain largely traditional
and insufficiently responsive to digital transformation and global educa tional trends (Amaly et al., 2023; Hashim, 2017; Ismail et al., 2024; Nurhayati et al., 2024). Together, these factors underscore
the urgent need for pedagogical reform in Islamic higher education.
The core problem addressed by this study is the limited effectiveness of conventional instructional strategies in fostering deep comprehension, value internalization, and critical thinking
in Islamic Religious Education. Although lecture - based approaches h ave succeeded in delivering factual knowledge, they often disengage students and hinder the development of higher - order
cognitive and ethical reasoning skills (Felsenthal & Agbaria, 2023; Omar, 2022). These shortcomings become particularly evident when stu dents struggle to relate abstract religious doctrines to everyday
ethical challenges. Additionally, the reliance on memorization — while culturally rooted — tends to reduce learning to passive recall, marginalizing the reflective and dialogical dimensions of f aith - based
education (Ayyad, 2022). In response to these limitations, a range of innovative teaching strategies has been proposed. Contextual Teaching and Learning (CTL), for example, links religious content with
real - life scenarios to enhance relevance an d comprehension (Parhan et al., 2024). Similarly, the Bahtsul Masa'il method engages students in interpreting classical texts through collaborative
discourse, enhancing both reading comprehension and applied ethical reasoning (Hudri & Al Haj Zaini, 2023).   These approaches indicate a growing recognition that effective Islamic education must
move beyond knowledge transmission toward reflective and contextualized learning. Among the emerging strategies, Problem - Based Learning (PBL) stands out for its strong th eoretical foundations
and demonstrated impact on learning outcomes. Grounded in constructivist and transformative learning theories, PBL emphasizes student - driven inquiry, critical thinking, and real - world problem
solving (García - Planas et al., 2021; Hung   & Amida, 2020). In moral and values education, PBL fosters ethical reasoning by encouraging learners to engage with complex issues, formulate judgments, and
collaboratively seek solutions (Bessa et al., 2017). Furthermore, it aligns well with Islamic pedag ogical goals, promoting not only intellectual development but also character formation and social
responsibility (Khatiban et al., 2019; Khazen, 2024).
Empirical studies have shown that PBL can enhance religious comprehension and moral development more effectively than traditional methods (Khatiban et al., 2019; Mao, 2021). In
comparative research, PBL has been found to outperform lecture - based instructio n in fostering https://doi.org/10.22521/edupij.2025.1 8 . 412   Published online by Universitepark Press

### 2 .   Literature Review
2.1. Theoretical Foundation of Problem - Based Learning (PBL) Problem - Based Learning Problem - Based Learning (PBL) signifies a fundamental transformation in pedagogical paradigms,
representing a shift from transmission - oriented, instructor - led modalities to learner - centered approaches grounded in authentic engagement and contextual relevanc e. Rooted in a sophisticated
amalgam of learning theories, PBL promotes inquiry - driven, collaborative, and reflective learning processes that align with the epistemological demands of 21st - century education (Akçay, 2009;
Storey & Schiavo, 2013). At its cor e, PBL is informed by constructivist theory, which posits that learners actively construct knowledge through meaningful engagement with their environment. This
constructivist orientation redefines the learner’s role from passive recipient to active knowled ge builder, wherein students interrogate complex, ill - structured problems and synthesize multi - sourced
information to derive actionable solutions. Such cognitively demanding engagement fosters deeper conceptual understanding and enhances the transferabilit y and sustainability of learning (Zhao et al.,
2024).
Complementing this is social constructivism, which accentuates the intersubjective nature of learning through collaborative dialogue. Within PBL frameworks, learners co - construct knowledge in
small group settings by articulating hypotheses, evaluating pers pectives, and negotiating shared meanings. These socially mediated interactions enhance not only individual cognitive development
but   also   cultivate   interpersonal   competencies   critical   for   professional   practice,   including communication, empathy, and cooper ative problem - solving (Safitri & Nurhayati, 2023; Shimizu et al.,
2021; Wu et al., 2018). The theoretical scaffolding of PBL is further enriched by insights from cognitive psychology. This perspective underscores how learners organize, store, and retrieve   information,
emphasizing mental processes such as abstraction, reasoning, and reflection. PBL facilitates the activation of prior knowledge, promotes hypothesis testing, and encourages iterative refinement of

### 2.2. PBL in Higher Education
Problem - Based Learning (PBL) has emerged as a transformative pedagogy within higher education, originating in medical training and progressively adopted across disciplines such as
engineering, architecture, science, and business. At its core, PBL fosters a   student - centered learning environment where learners engage collaboratively in solving real - world, ill - structured problems.
This approach promotes the development of essential academic and professional competencies, including critical thinking, self - direc ted learning, and collaborative problem - solving (Hung & Amida,
2020). Underpinned by constructivist principles, PBL encourages deep cognitive engagement by positioning students as active agents in constructing knowledge. By applying learned concepts to
aut hentic scenarios, students not only retain information more effectively but also cultivate adaptability   and   analytical   skills.   Furthermore,   PBL   nurtures   soft   skills — such   as   teamwork,
professional communication, and leadership — that are increasingly critical   in contemporary work environments (Muhd Darus et al., 2016).
Empirical   studies   confirm   that   students   engaged   in   PBL   demonstrate   higher   academic achievement, improved conceptual understanding, and stronger motivation, particularly when
instruction is aligned with real - life relevance and professional contexts (Adamuth e & Mane, 2020; Nemakhavhani, 2024). Nevertheless, successful implementation is not without obstacles. Key
challenges include resource limitations, faculty preparedness, time constraints, and curricular rigidity, all of which may hinder PBL’s institutional   integration (Borges et al., 2014; Salvador et al.,
2023). Best practices underscore the importance of sustained institutional support, innovative assessment   strategies,   and   interdisciplinary   collaboration   in   course   design.   Moreover,   the
effectiveness of P BL is closely tied to its alignment with cultural and contextual realities. In non - Western academic settings, adapting PBL to local discourse norms and learning expectations is critical
for fostering engagement and inclusivity (Imafuku, 2012; Savin - Baden,   2016). PBL represents a powerful instructional model capable of preparing students for the intellectual and interpersonal
demands of the 21st - century workforce.

### 2.4. PBL Efficacy in Improving Students’ Comprehension
Problem - Based Learning (PBL) has emerged as a transformative pedagogical model that significantly enhances students’ comprehension and learning outcomes across educational levels and
disciplines. At its core, PBL cultivates critical thinking, facilitates d eep conceptual understanding, and promotes long - term knowledge retention by engaging students in inquiry - driven, real - world
problem - solving. Empirical findings consistently demonstrate that PBL elevates students’ higher - order cognitive skills, particularly   among learners with strong academic orientation and consistent
participation (Lee, 2025). The incorporation of concept mapping into PBL frameworks further amplifies engagement and reinforces learning gains across cognitive and behavioral dimensions
(Mesba h et al., 2025). These outcomes are echoed in subject - specific applications; for instance, PBL has been shown to improve achievement and scientific attitudes in physics education (Isna &
Masykuri, 2018). Similarly, integrating gamification tools such as Ka hoot! within PBL contexts has been found to heighten academic performance and learner satisfaction (Taesotikul et al., 2021).
From a cognitive standpoint, PBL fosters durable knowledge retention and facilitates transfer of learning to unfamiliar contexts, largely due to its emphasis on active, self - directed learning (Yew &
Goh, 2016). It also promotes intrinsic motivation and sus tained engagement, evidenced by improved attendance patterns and positive student feedback (De Witte & Rogge, 2016; Naik et al., 2024). The
use of authentic, real - life problems and cooperative learning structures sustains learner interest and contributes t o deeper, more meaningful educational experiences (Badjie & Velankar, 2023). Beyond
cognitive outcomes, PBL supports the development of essential twenty - first - century skills — including collaboration,   communication,   leadership,   and   adaptability — that   are   vita l   for   success   in
contemporary professional environments (Naik et al., 2024). It enhances students’ sense of agency and academic self - efficacy, empowering them to take ownership of their learning processes.   A
broader theoretical lens reveals that PBL not o nly supports immediate comprehension but also enhances knowledge transfer, reasoning, and cognitive flexibility. Students engaged in PBL frequently
demonstrate stronger self - regulatory learning strategies and are more adept at applying their knowledge to c omplex, novel problems (Lu et al., 2014). These effects are magnified in settings where

3.   Methodology
### 3.1. Study design
This study employed a Sequential Explanatory Mixed Methods approach, integrating quantitative and qualitative data collection and analysis in two strategically ordered phases(Creswell & Creswell,
2017). The quantitative strand followed a quasi - experimental   design using a single - group pre - test and post - test procedure without a control group, which is acknowledged as a limitation of the study.
The qualitative strand adopted a basic interpretive qualitative design to explore students’ learning perceptions and   experiences. Data integration followed a sequential explanatory strategy, in which
qualitative findings were used to interpret and enrich the quantitative results. The initial quantitative phase served to establish empirical evidence concerning the pedagog ical effectiveness of Problem -
Based Learning (PBL) in enhancing students’ comprehension of Islamic Religious Education (IRE) content. The subsequent qualitative phase was designed to elucidate and contextualize these results
through the in - depth exploratio n of student perspectives. This design was selected to afford both analytical breadth and explanatory depth. While the quantitative component provided statistically
measurable insights, the qualitative dimension enabled a richer interpretation of how stude nts cognitively and affectively engaged with PBL as a pedagogical strategy. The use of methodical
triangulation across phases contributed to the robustness of internal validity and strengthened the interpretative credibility of the study.
3.2. Participants The target population consisted of fourth - semester undergraduate students enrolled in the Islamic Education Study Program at Singaperbangsa University, Karawang, which was chosen due to
its ongoing curricular reform efforts integrating Islamic values with   student - centered learning models, making it a suitable site to examine the transformative impact of Problem - Based Learning.
The total population comprised 222 students, all of whom had completed the Philosophy of Islamic Education course — a foundational req uirement for   participation.   A simple random sampling
technique was employed to minimize selection bias and ensure representativeness. Utilizing the Slovin formula at a 5% margin of error, a sample of 143 students was obtained. This sampling strategy
facili tated generalizability within the defined population while ensuring analytical rigor. Eligibility criteria included course completion and lack of prior exposure to PBL - based instruction, thereby
mitigating confounding influences on outcome measures.
3.3. Data collection tools Data collection was aligned with the mixed - methods framework, comprising both standardized cognitive assessments and semi - structured qualitative interviews.

### 3.4. Validity of data collection tools
To ensure both construct and content validity, the cognitive assessment underwent rigorous scrutiny by two academic experts specializing in Islamic Religious Education and instructional
evaluation. Each item was systematically reviewed for theoretical alig nment, semantic clarity, and pedagogical relevance to the targeted learning outcomes. Recommendations from the validation
process were meticulously integrated to refine the instrument prior to its deployment. Reliability was established through the computa tion of Cronbach’s Alpha, which produced a coefficient of 0.85,
indicating high internal consistency across test items. To mitigate confounding variables and ensure procedural uniformity, the cognitive assessments were administered under standardized condi tions.
Synchronization of the intervention and post - assessment schedule further enhanced the internal validity of the findings.
Regarding qualitative data, the study upheld methodological rigor through the implementation of triangulation, peer debriefing, and participant member checking. To bolster trustworthiness,
selected interview transcripts were returned to participants for ve rification, ensuring accurate representation of their perspectives. A comprehensive audit trail was maintained throughout the
analysis phase, which included the use of codebooks, documented theme development processes, and reflexive analytic memos to enhan ce transparency and reproducibility of qualitative inferences
### 3.5. Data analysis procedure
Quantitative data were analyzed using IBM SPSS Statistics, applying both descriptive and inferential statistical techniques. Descriptive statistics, including means, standard deviations, and
frequency distributions, provided a foundational overview of stud ent performance. A paired samples t - test was subsequently employed to assess statistically significant differences between pre -   and
post - intervention scores, thereby quantifying the effectiveness of the PBL intervention on students’ comprehension of Islami c Religious Education content.

theme definition, and synthesis of findings. Coding was exe cuted manually and cross - verified through peer debriefing to bolster analytical reliability. The process emphasized thematic saturation, ensuring

1. Improved Understanding of IRE Concepts
Students reported enhanced conceptual understanding, particularly in grasping the spiritual significance of worship. For instance, Student A articulated a deeper devotion in prayer, while Student

2. Enhanced Critical Thinking Skills
The qualitative data revealed that PBL fostered analytical reasoning and value - based decision - making. Students such as E and F described learning to identify root causes of problems and critically
assess relevant information. Others, like G and H, emphasiz ed their capacity to formulate ethically grounded solutions, guided by Islamic principles like justice and integrity. The narratives suggest that
PBL   environments   challenge   students   to   engage   in   deeper   reasoning   that   transcends   rote memorization.
3. Increased Engagement and Motivation
Participants expressed heightened interest and participation in IRE classes. Students I and J remarked on increased motivation and reduced boredom, while K and L highlighted elevated
classroom interactivity and confidence in articulating their views. The e xperiential, student - centered nature of PBL appeared to cultivate intrinsic motivation, thereby enhancing overall learning
engagement.
4. Implementation Challenges
Despite the benefits, students also reported barriers to effective PBL implementation. The most cited challenges were time constraints and the need for more lecturer support. Students M and N

6. Limitations and future research
Despite the significant contributions of this study, several limitations should be acknowledged that delimit the generalizability and scope of the findings. First, the participant sample was drawn
exclusively from a single Islamic higher education institut ion, potentially narrowing the external validity across diverse educational ecosystems. The unique pedagogical practices, sociocultural
norms, and institutional ethos specific to this setting may have shaped students’ experiences of PBL in ways that are no t universally replicable. Second, the study relied heavily on self - reported
qualitative data, which, although rich and contextually grounded, introduces potential biases including social desirability, recall inaccuracies, and interpretive subjectivity. Whi le the use of
thematic analysis provides valuable insight into student perspectives, the absence of complementary quantitative measures limits causal inference and restricts our ability to quantify learning outcomes.
Future research should consider longitu dinal mixed - method designs, integrating psychometric assessments, learning analytics, and observational data to triangulate findings and enhance empirical
robustness. Third, the role of instructional variables — such as teacher facilitation quality, PBL impl ementation fidelity, and curricular integration — was not systematically investigated. Further
inquiry should examine how educator competence, professional development, and institutional alignment influence PBL outcomes in religious education contexts. Addit ionally, broader comparative
studies are needed to explore PBL’s pedagogical resonance across interfaith and intercultural educational settings. Fourth, although the study utilized a pre - test and post - test design to measure
the impact of the intervention,   it did not include a control group. The absence of a control group is a methodological limitation, as it restricts the ability to rule out alternative explanations for observed
improvements and limits the strength of causal claims. Future research should   consider employing a quasi - experimental design with a control group to provide more robust evidence of intervention
effects. Finally, intervention - based research is recommended to evaluate the impact of PBL models specifically tailored to Islamic pedagogy.   These efforts should include the design and testing of
structured curricula that integrate Islamic ethical constructs with inquiry - based learning, supported by rigorous, culturally responsive evaluation frameworks. By addressing these methodological,
inst ructional, and contextual gaps, future scholarship can offer a more nuanced and generalizable understanding of how PBL can be strategically implemented to enrich both the cognitive and spiritual
dimensions of learning in religious education.
7. Suggestions and practical implications
The findings of this study present critical pedagogical and institutional implications that warrant immediate attention. First, the demonstrated cognitive and affective benefits of Project - Based

institutions must initiate systemic reforms to embed PBL within the core of IRE curricula. This involves ensuring alignment w ith theological imperatives, fostering epistemological depth, and respecting the
spiritual values that underpin religious instruction.

8. Conclusion
This study addressed two main research questions regarding the impact of Problem - Based Learning (PBL) in Islamic Religious Education (IRE). First, the implementation of PBL led to a
statistically significant improvement in students’ comprehension of core I RE concepts, as indicated by the increase in post - test scores across domains such as ibadah, aqidah, akhlaq, and muamalah.
Second, qualitative findings revealed that students perceived PBL as fostering deeper conceptual understanding, critical reasoning, a nd the ability to apply Islamic teachings to real - life situations. By
facilitating the convergence of cognitive, affective, and ethical learning dimensions, PBL enables students to move beyond surface - level comprehension toward a more profound, integrative
engagement   with   Islamic   teachings.   The   model   fosters   critical   reasoning,   enhances   moral discernment, and nurtures emotional resonance — core objectives of religious education. These
outcomes demonstrate that PBL, when appropriately contextualized, offers a   viable alternative to traditional didactic instruction, aligning with contemporary educational imperatives while preserving
spiritual integrity. However, several challenges were identified in the PBL implementation process, including time constraints for g roup work and discussion, as well as the need for more intensive
instructor support and feedback. These issues, compounded by the absence of a control group in this quasi - experimental study, should be acknowledged as methodological and practical limitation s.
Addressing these structural constraints requires institutional commitment to reimagining curriculum frameworks, assessment models, and faculty competencies. Moreover, embedding PBL within
culturally responsive and community - integrated pedagogical ecosys tems can further enhance its transformative   potential.   In   light   of   these   findings,   this   study   recommends   the   strategic
institutionalization   of   PBL   in   Islamic   higher   education,   accompanied   by   sustained   faculty development and structural support. Through thi s lens, Islamic education can cultivate ethically
grounded, intellectually agile, and socially responsive learners equipped to navigate the complexities of the modern world.
