### Podcasts in higher education
Podcasts have become an increasingly popular medium of communication and knowl - edge exchange. According to Lee et al. (2008), the word “podcast” was coined in 2004 as
a combination of the words iPod and Broadcast. Podcasts are audio (or video) broadcasts that can be downloaded or streamed via personal digital devices, such as smartphones,
digital media players, personal computers, tablets, etc. Podcasts are broadcast to an audience. As Evans (2008, p. 491) noted, “the use of digital media players, popularised
by Apple’s iPod, is widespread amongst undergraduate students”. As a result of the increasing popularity of podcasts, there is a growing body of literature on the use of
podcasts in higher education (see Cooper et al., 2009; Copley, 2007; Dale & Hassanien, 2008; Dale & Pymm, 2009). From a review of literature on podcasting in higher education
in the UK, Harris and Park (2008) categorised the types of uses of podcasting within education into four groupings: teaching-driven, service-driven, marketing-driven and
technology-driven. According to Harris and Park (2008) teaching-driven uses have considered the potential of technology to improve quality of teaching or allow more
effective teaching to ultimately improve the learning experience, and such podcasts might

students with learning difficulties (Harris & Park, 2008, p. 550). Marketing-driven podcasts are aimed at recruitment of students or campus tours, while technology-

which might then promote the use of podcasts within courses (see Harris & Park, 2008, p. 550). Offering a slightly different take on the extant literature, McGarr (2009)

students to review; supplementary podcasting, involving additional materials to deepen understanding; and, creative podcasting, which involves students creating their own
podcasts to aid their knowledge and understanding of a topic. Pegrum et al. (2015) note that creative podcasts are the least common form of podcasting in higher education.
There is emerging debate around the possibility for improved learning outcomes from student-generated or creative podcasts and while there is limited evidence, some suggest
that turning students into “knowledge creators” (McGarr, 2009; quoted in Pegrum et al.,
2015) can help develop a range of competencies, including knowledge-building and
critical thinking skills (see Pegrum et al., 2015). Much of the wider literature on educa - tional uses of podcasting explores instructor or lecturer generated podcasts for teaching –
some use podcasts for revision (Evans, 2008), presentation of lecture materials and content (Salmon & Edirisingha, 2011), reflexivity (Fischer and Baird, 2006), presentation

mation on various resources within a university (Berk et al., 2007). Audio recordings are also a popular mechanism for feedback, rather than written comments, and advance -
ments in virtual learning environments within HEIs have enabled this shift (Salmon & Edirisingha, 2011). Explorations of the use of podcasts for active and deep learning are
limited (Lee et al., 2008; Salmon & Edirisingha, 2011) and this has been identified as an under-researched field by Abt and Barry (2007, p. 8) who noted that “research needs to
examine   the   involvement   of   students   themselves   in   the   creation   of   podcasts”.
Importantly, much of the literature on podcasting in higher education notes the need for research on podcast creation by students and podcasts as assessment (Kemp et al.,
2012), though this type of podcast in higher education is yet to be rigorously assessed, especially within geography. It is this void that this paper offers a contribution on:
### student generated podcasts for deep learning in geography.
### Podcasts in geography
There is a small, but not insignificant, set of literature exploring the utility of podcasts for teaching and learning geography. Within third level geography, podcasts are used in
a variety of contexts, including the delivery of lectures, feedback on assessment, informa - tion guides for assessment, to support guided walks around campus, to present assess -
ment or other practical information to save time in lectures for thematic content (Kemp et al., 2012). Many of the podcasts are provided to students via virtual learning

support independent fieldwork, such as guided walks around a particular location (see Downward, 2008). Hill et al. (2012) also report on the use of video podcasts from field
locations to support thematic lecture content. Essentially, podcasts are seen as a way of extending technical supports for field work in geography (see also Hill & Nelson, 2011).
Beyond this, France and Wheeler (2007) examined the utility of podcasting assignment
### feedback in geography. They argued that the new approach to feedback offered
“enhanced opportunities to deliver both generic and individualised feedback to students, and an improved student learning experience” (p.9; see also France & Wheeler, 2007).
Further, Winterbottom (2007) explored the utility of delivering virtual lectures in geography using podcasting technologies. In their research, students noted that flexibility
and the ability to replay or revisit lectures was the main advantage of podcasting (Winterbottom, 2007). Winterbottom cautioned against using podcasting to replace face-
to-face teaching, arguing instead that it freed up time to allow for detailed discussions or other in-class learning options. As noted above, most research notes the advancements in
virtual learning environments as having aided the possibilities around podcasting and the
### delivery of podcasts in geography modules.
Research by Kemp et al. (2012) is one of the few papers within geography exploring the use of student-generated podcasts for assessment in geomorphology (see also Kemp
et   al.,   2013).   Kemp   et   al.   (2012)   used   student-produced   podcasts   as   part   of a geomorphology module from 2008–2010. Students worked in teams of 5–6 people to
produce a radio show style of podcast aimed at communicating geomorphological data to a general audience. Students were required to complete data collection and analysis in the
field and laboratory and communicate their findings by creating a 10-minute podcast using the software programme Audacity. Many students reported development of tech -
nical, group-working and communication skills and generally enjoyed the creativity of the exercise. Challenges noted included data, technology, scripting of podcasts, time, and
issues with the creative process, though students were motivated by the novelty of the assessment method (see Kemp et al., 2012). In all, Kemp et al. (2012) found difficulty in
assessing the depth of learning from the podcast assessment, though they did note clear evidence of students learning to communicate science effectively. This point is especially
### important for ESD.

### Deep learning, podcasts and geography
Approaches to learning are often viewed on a spectrum, with surface learning at one end and deep learning at the other. According to Houghton (2004, p. 9), surface learning is
viewed as “the tacit acceptance of information and memorization as isolated and unlinked facts”. It is often argued that this approach to learning “leads to superficial
retention of material for examinations and does not promote understanding or long- term retention of knowledge and information” (Houghton, 2004, p. 9). Surface learning
might generally be associated with teaching practices and pedagogies whereby learning is instructor-led and often involves teachers imparting knowledge onto students (e.g.
traditional lecture format) and then reproducing that information via a written exam - ination, for example. By contrast, Houghton (2004, p. 9; emphasis added) noted that deep
learning: involves the critical analysis of new ideas, linking them to already known concepts and principles, and leads to understanding and long-term retention of concepts so that they can
be used for problem solving in unfamiliar contexts.   Deep learning promotes understanding and application for life .
This tends to follow more action-oriented and learning-centred approaches to learning.
For Hill and Woodland (2002, p. 540) deep learning is “the acquisition of higher order skills such as analysing, interpreting and evaluating information rather than simply
amassing, reproducing and describing it”. Importantly, they argue that deep learning is holistic , involving “an integration of facts to produce understanding, rather than atomis -
tic, characterised by the accumulation of disparate facts” (p.540). In a similar vein, Ryan and Aasetre (2021, p. 380) argued that “deep learning indicates understanding and
creative use of knowledge in new settings”. In relation to the benefits of deep learning, Pegrum et al. (2015, p. 142) noted that “educational researchers have established that
a deep approach to learning that promotes active understanding of meaning can lead to better student outcomes, higher grades and superior retention of knowledge over time”,
arguing that a deep approach to learning involves “active understanding of meaning” (p.144). In much of the literature on deep learning approaches, there is a strong emphasis
on understanding, rather than memorising, as being key to deep learning.

There is emerging consensus on the ways in which podcasts can be used for assess - ment to allow for active understanding of meaning to enable deeper learning. Biggs and
Tang (2011, p. 85) noted that students had identified that a good practical test of their understanding was “being able to explain to someone else or to be able to adapt and to
use what had been understood.” This creates an important role for podcasting in deep learning as the method of assessment requires communication of knowledge and under -
standing, as well as the application of their knowledge to their own research, data gathering or case studies. In relation to deep learning, Pegrum et al. (2015, p. 144) argued
that contextualisation was critical for deep learning – that is, “forging of connections between pre-existing and new knowledge, which in turn improves understanding, reten -
tion, and application of learning to real-world situations”. In relation to podcasts and deep learning, Dale and Povey (2009) argued that there was a deeper level of learning
from the creation of learner-generated podcasts as students had to read the theoretical materials and indeed read more widely on the topic to be able to apply it to the subject

One final component of deep learning worth reflecting on is the structure and design of the assessment task itself. Pegrum et al. (2015) argued that it is important for educators
to create conditions that maximise the likelihood of students engaging in a deep learning approach through carefully designed assessment. They note, however, that given the lack
of empirical research on this topic, more research is required to understand how deep learning can occur through creative, or student-generated, podcasts. Thus, it is an aim of
this paper to use a case study to explore the ways in which an approach to deep learning can be fostered through a clearly defined task for students involving active learning
through case study research, academic contextualisation, and communication through oral/aural methods, specifically podcasting. Indeed, many authors have noted the poten -
tial of podcasts for a deeper mode of learning (see Jarvis and Dickie, 2010; Kemp et al., 2012), though are yet to generate data to demonstrate this potential.
There are a small number of contributions in geography seeking to explore means to promote deep learning within the discipline (see Dummer et al., 2008; Golightly & Raath,
2015; Hope, 2009; Ryan & Aasetre, 2021; Schultz & DeMers, 2020). Dummer et al. (2008) explore the use of reflective field diaries as a tool for deep learning during geography
fieldwork. They argue that reflective diaries allow for enhancement of critical self- reflection and communication skills and this, combined with an active learning approach
to field work, allows for deep learning. They also argue that clear assessment guidelines and assessment criteria are essential for deep learning (Dummer et al., 2008). Similarly,
Ryan and Aasetre (2021, p. 384) argued that to achieve deep learning in geography, the teacher must “facilitate the learning environment, content and assessment in a deliberate
way. Activities must be constructed to encourage reflection”. Golightly and Raath (2015) argued that one of the key components to deep learning in geography was the student’s
intrinsic motivation – their willingness to engage in learning and assessment tasks and not see it as simply a means to an end (i.e. getting a grade and passing a module). The
study presented in this paper aims to contribute to this body of work by exploring the possibilities of podcasts for enabling deep learning in geography, noting in particular that
less has been said about the ways of communicating new knowledge to further enhance deep learning. I argue that podcasts offer a novel assessment method, using oral/aural
components, to further our knowledge of the ways to achieve deep learning.

understand the process of dialogue through which knowledge is created, and they examine the logic of an argument critically . This module drew upon ideas of the postcolonial city
and urban theories beyond the west. Here, students were challenged to consider the ways

### Student reflections on learning
To assess the utility of student-generated podcasts as a form of deep learning, I analysed reflective statements produced by the students. As part of the assignment, students were
asked to complete a short reflective statement on their learning experiences. This reflective exercise was aimed at getting students to reflect on   what   they had learnt, to
become more aware of the skills and knowledge they developed, together with the ways they learn, to allow students greater cognisance of   how   they learn. There were 78 students
who completed the module and I draw on a sample of 67 reflective statements where permission had been given to use for research purposes. The reflective statements were
analysed using thematic content analysis and I reflect here on the core themes that were identified from this analysis relating to the depth of learning, oral/aural versus written
assessment, independent construction of knowledge, technology, and creativity.
Deep learning using oral and aural assessment The most frequently occurring reflection on learning centred on the oral/aural approach,
rather than written or text-based assessment, for the ability to allow a deeper learning of the themes and a greater retention of the information. It was not only the spoken word
(oral), but also the listening to, or hearing of, their voice (aural), which aided with information retention and deeper learning. Some of these reflections included:
I took in more information after saying it out loud a couple of times. Usually, I would write an assignment and forget what I wrote a couple of weeks on but, this was enjoyable, and I took
a lot in. (#23) Hearing yourself saying it over again versus just typing the information is a new experience that you are re-learning the information in a way which is something I haven’t experienced
before. (#7) I felt I was actively learning about new topics by listening to myself talk. (#8) I learned that there is a major difference in just typing information you have read and
explaining verbally the information you have read. I feel personally, one gains a greater understanding of this information. (#21)
I think this way of assessment was really good for understanding and retaining information because . . . we were saying the information out loud and more than once, so it made it a really
good way of retaining information. (#25)

terms of depth of learning, it would seem imperative that we explore further the utility of diverse oral/aural methods and include diverse oral/aural assessments into geography
curricula where appropriate. Further, the format of assessment outputs requires deeper consideration in the literature on deep learning, as oral/aural methods offer a new

principles of diversity and inclusion within UDL, where different assessment formats are encouraged. Finally, deep learning also has benefits in terms of student performance

### Reflections and revisions: workload and UDL
Following the initial implementation of, and reflection on, this approach to learning and assessment, two areas emerged for revision: workload and inclusivity (UDL). Developing
a podcast series does generate multiple demands on the students and can increase the workload of the assessment. The workload for this assessment, at least initially, is also on
the higher side for staff compared to other, more traditional forms of assessment,

---

548   T. KENNA especially given the need for clear and structured guidelines to encourage deep learning, which often need to be reiterated and discussed with the students in detail. Although, in
### this module, audio and video recordings with assignment guidelines, background,
justification, etc., were made available for the students to consult as many times as required. Upon reflection, the workload was on the higher side for both staff and
students. Throughout the semester, there was no greater workload than other forms of assessment. Students had been encouraged to contact the lecturer and check that they
were on track with the assessment, especially case study selection. The in-semester workload was like other assessment methods and the students did not report technical
difficulties, so generally speaking just got on with it. However, the requirements for the students were high. The effort to research case studies, make 5 episodes (3 research-
based), sustain the discussion, tie together with introductions and conclusions, was high.
The marking load, while more enjoyable than other assessment formats, was also higher with each podcast series taking about an hour to assess. Given the size of the class, this
was challenging. I do, however, agree with many of the students that the benefits gained outweighed this extra “load” both for staff and students. Listening to podcasts was more
enjoyable than, say, reading essays. Students were creative, different personalities emerged, making the work more engaging to assess, though it longer. Importantly,
student performance was better than previous years where written forms of assessment had been used. In all, 78 students completed the module, no student failed and 64% of
### students received a 2:1 grade or higher (50 out of 78).
For future iterations of this assessment, it has been redesigned to attend to workload issues. Future students will be required to develop a single podcast episode, of
a maximum 20-minute duration. They will be required to select an overall theme for their podcast episode related to Global Urbanism and will still be required to select three
themes from the modules, research independent case studies, and present their research, together with an accessible discussion of the literature and relevant urban theories in
their podcast episode. The required work will be proportionate to the revised length of the podcast episode. This revised version will be a much more manageable task for staff
and students, whilst still retaining the necessary components for deep learning. For others looking to introduce podcasts into their own modules, Kemp et al. (2012) offer
an example of the way podcasts are used in the group setting, accounting for a smaller component of the overall module assessment. Like Kemp et al., others could use podcasts
for learning and assessment of a particular thematic area within a module, asking students to research a particular case study area or theme and produce shorter episodes,
maybe 5–10 minutes in length. Podcasts could be based on primary research (e.g. inter - views) to engage a more conversational tone and thus might be useful in research
methods modules, whereby the outputs of research could be incorporated into podcast
### episodes.
In relation to the principles of inclusive assessment for UDL, there are limitations to the assessment reported in this paper. To attend to the principles of inclusive assessment
in UDL, future iterations of this assessment task will be altered to allow multiple submission formats. Before this module had commenced, I received an enquiry from
### a student interested in undertaking the module who had a speech impediment and
wondered about alternatives to the oral components of the assessment. In an ideal

creativity, public communication and engagement, etc., with the main difference being oral/audio versus written/visual presentation. Students will be able to choose the sub -

### Conclusions
It is evident that there are a combination of factors making the reported assessment task successful for deep learning in urban geography, including a structured assessment task
with clear and detailed guidelines, a requirement for academic contextualisation, the use of oral/aural methods for assessment, the requirement for independent research and new
knowledge construction, linked to academic contextualisation (relevant theories and literature), a high degree of choice and creativity (encouraging intrinsic motivation),
and the development of new skills, especially diverse communication of knowledge. The combination of these factors allowed for the podcast assessment to be a holistic process,
integrating several aspects of learning. The podcast series was the only form of assess - ment in this module, and it could be argued that the length and breadth of the task
demanded sustained and immersive engagement and that this was also critical for deep learning. In all, this study demonstrates that podcasts can enhance deep learning in
geography under carefully and thoughtfully crafted conditions. Producing a podcast series appears to be enjoyable, creative, and empowering. The study shows that podcasts
can be a useful tool for assessment that can offer unique contributions to the field of deep learning in geography, especially deep learning of geographic concepts. This study also
highlights the role of considering diverse assessment formats, especially those involving oral/aural methods, for deep learning to enhance our knowledge of the ways in which
deep learning can occur or be facilitated. Podcasts also have the potential to raise the profile and popularity of the discipline within and beyond institutes of higher education,
as well as offer a novel and transformative approach to learning and education for sustainable development.
