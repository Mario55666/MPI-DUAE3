© The Author(s) 2025 Abstract Active learning and inquiry-based learning in higher education foster critical think- ing and engagement, preparing students to become lifelong learners and adaptable
problem solvers. While intensive delivery modes often incorporate these pedago- gies, research has predominantly focused on outcomes rather than the pedagogi-
cal approaches themselves. This study addresses this gap by exploring academics’ experiences with active and inquiry-based learning methodologies in the VU Block
Model®. Through semi-structured interviews with nine academics, thematic analy- sis identified five themes illustrating the implementation of these methodologies in
intensive delivery modes. Academics described employing active learning practices such as peer teaching, collaboration, hands-on activities and real-world projects in
their university classroom. However, the participants’ views of the implementation of inquiry-based learning methodologies proved problematic in intensive delivery
contexts, often being reduced to posing questions and overlooking its broader scope and potential. The utilisation of active learning and inquiry-based approaches in the
context of VU Block Model® presented an opportunity for pedagogical innovation deeply rooted in Dewey’s philosophy of learning through participative and collabo-
rative experience. This study highlights the need for continued exploration of inno- vative pedagogies in intensive teaching to enhance teaching capacities of academic
staff.

### Introduction
Previous commentaries overviewing the future of higher education have rein- forced the necessity to embrace innovation in pedagogical delivery (Haque,
2023). In higher education, pedagogical innovations encompass diverse descrip- tions and approaches, all aimed at enhancing student experience and their over-
all outcomes (Major et al., 2020), such as promoting “educational equity, student retention or persistence, sense of belonging, learning or engagement, or instruc-
tor engagement or efficiency” (Palmer & Giering, 2024, p.454). It is argued that changing the mode of course delivery (e.g., intensive teaching) creates spaces
for pedagogical innovation in higher education (Chau et   al., 2023). Changing the mode of course delivery within a university (e.g., semester, intensive) can
be driven by a range of factors including student academic outcomes (Kift et al., 2010), student engagement (Krause & Coates, 2008), global impacts such as
COVID- 19 (Peimani & Kamalipour, 2021) and the financial circumstances of an institution (Huls, 2022). Weldon (2024) commented on a global push within
higher education for institutions to progress towards student centered models of delivery by adopting shorter, intensive course modalities that move away from
traditional semester-based structures and demonstrate “a commitment to critical and practical self-reflection essential to effective learning and teaching” (p. 2). As
a consequence of change, the efficacy of learning and teaching approaches within higher education courses is framed by the institution’s successful implementation
of an appropriate delivery mode (Imran et al., 2023). This shift can also trigger positive transformations, such as increased openness among academics to inno-
vate within new spaces (Lundberg & Stigmar, 2024). A key outcome of overall institutional success is bound by its capacity to remain innovative and the success
of those innovations in supporting the learning and teaching achievements of its students.
Unlike traditional courses, which typically adhere to a semester-long structure with regular weekly meetings, intensive teaching courses offer a more concen-
trated educational experience. The landscape of intensive teaching varies signif- icantly, as reflected in the numerous terms used to describe it, including   time-
shortened courses   (Daniel, 2000),   block courses   (Burton & Nesbit, 2008),   VU Block Model®   (Loton et al., 2022; Thomas et al., 2024), and   compressed courses
(Kops, 2014). This array of terminology underscores the diverse approaches in play; nevertheless, the common hallmark of these courses is their compact and
condensed format for instructional sessions. However, as Lodge and Ashford- Rowe (2024) note, there is an “underemphasis on… the process” of teaching and
learning within higher education policy and practice (p. 1). Typically, learning outcomes (or products) receive more focus, while the “mechanisms behind learn-
ing in intensive modes” often remain less defined (Lodge & Ashford-Rowe, 2024, p. 1). This underexplored area suggests a need for deeper investigation into how
intensive modes shape learning dynamics and processes in higher education.

### Dewey’s Contributions to Active Learning and Inquiry‑Based
### Learning
There are many definitions of active learning and IBL approaches within the edu- cational landscape that encompass a spectrum of perspectives. Defining the rela-
tionship between IBL approaches and active learning is difficult and there seems to be an overlap regarding how these approaches are defined and discussed in the
literature (Prince, 2004). Both approaches are considered pedagogies that effectively immerse students in the processes of knowledge creation, as opposed to receiving
passive content dissemination from the teacher or what Freire (1970) would call the banking mode of education. In this paper, we draw from a Deweyan perspective to
frame our understanding of active learning and IBL to comprehend the theoretical underpinnings of these methodologies.
Dewey’s (1938) work laid the theoretical groundwork for constructivist peda- gogy and his impact on educational philosophy extends notably into higher educa-
tion. Dewey’s theoretical contributions focused on educators leveraging the learn- er’s lived experiences to facilitate meaning-making from the surroundings, aiding
in problem-solving and knowledge construction (Dewey, 1916). Emphasising the interplay between individuals or groups and their environment, Dewey highlighted
that learning fundamentally emerges from this dynamic experience. Dewey’s advo- cacy for experiential learning highlights the importance of learning through direct
experiences which is prominently featured in his seminal work titled “Experience and Education” (Dewey, 1938). The aim of any educational endeavour, according
to Dewey, is to provide “the enterprise of supplying the conditions which insure growth” (Dewey, 1938, p.51). Dewey argued the fundamental role of the educator
is to assist the learner’s growth through the progressive development and expansion of experience. He asserts that experience arouses a learner’s curiosity, strengthens
initiative, and sets up a desire and purpose to grow and learn. Dewey’s work aligns closely with both active learning and IBL by emphasising the importance of experi-
ential learning and the learner’s active engagement in constructing knowledge.

student engagement, motivation and cognitive involvement. Bonwell and Eison (1991) succinctly define active learning as “anything that involves students in

### act of active engagement through conscious connection with knowledge, students

through problem-solving, exploration or other active learning activities. Hence,
### active learning is deeply rooted in constructivist pedagogy, emphasising the
### active construction of knowledge by learners (Anthony, 1996; Dewey, 1938).
### Active learning methods have acquired considerable attention and research
### within educational discourse. Despite documented positive impacts on learning
### outcomes, student engagement, and improved academic outcomes (Beichner,
### 2014; Freeman et al., 2014; Lumpkin et al., 2015), there exists a gap in under-
### standing of the nuanced ways in which these methods are adopted and integrated
### across diverse disciplines and institutional contexts. Research has highlighted
the benefits of active learning in promoting inclusive practices (Haak et   al.,

### However, there is a notable literature gap concerning the role and outcomes
### of active learning within intensive delivery modes. As higher education faces

### effective pedagogical strategies becomes even more critical (Devlin & Samara-

IBL involves the development of the sense of inquiry and investigation in which questions or problems are explored (Spronken-Smith et al., 2007). Whilst the pro-
cess may vary it typically involves stages such as ask, investigate, create, discuss, and reflect. When articulating characteristics of IBL, Oliver (2008) states “learning
comes as a consequence of the information processing that occurs as students work to explore the problem setting and to seek a solution” (p. 88). Spronken-Smith et al.
(2007) outline fundamental components of IBL, positing that it ought to be question or problem-driven, based within the pursuit of knowledge creation and comprehen-
sion, and characterised by self-guided approaches, with instructors assuming a facil- itative role. Adhering to Dewey’s philosophical standpoint, reflection has a pivotal
role within IBL. For Dewey, reflection is essential for meaningful learning to occur as he states, “we learn by thinking reflectively about the perplexing problems, issues
and actions that we experience” (p.7).
Whilst inquiry pedagogy is widely advocated, researched, and implemented in primary and secondary school contexts, “there is currently little systematic knowl-
edge about the practice of inquiry-based learning (IBL) in higher education” (Adi- tomo et al., 2013, p.1239). When applied effectively in higher educational contexts
IBL-based pedagogies have demonstrated measurable enhancements in student aca- demic performance (Taasoobshirazi et al., 2006).
Despite active learning and IBL approaches being highly advocated in educa- tional arenas, there is a lack of research regarding the various approaches to imple-
mentation in higher educational settings (Aditomo et al., 2013). To date, existent scholarship has yet to examine the nexus between active learning and IBL and inten-
sive teaching formats. Therefore, this research aims to explore academics’ percep- tions of their active and IBL practices within intensive teaching.
### Method
A generic qualitative research approach (Caelli et   al., 2003; Kahlke, 2014) was chosen as the methodological framework for this study, aligning with the goal of
uncovering and understanding the perspectives of experiences of education academ- ics regarding teaching and learning practices within an intensive delivery model.
Generic qualitative research is ideal for studies focused on exploring phenomenon, process, or perspectives of individuals (Caelli et al., 2003). This approach does not
adhere to a single, universally accepted stance; rather, it enables flexibility in captur- ing the multifaceted nature of participant experiences without the constraints of a
more rigid methodological paradigm (Caelli et al., 2003). Grounded in constructiv- ist epistemology (Creswell & Poth, 2017), due to its alignment with Dewey’s educa-
tional philosophy, which emphasise the importance of understanding the experience, reflection, and the active construction of knowledge by participants in educational
settings (Dewey, 1916, 1938).

### Context
### Victoria University introduced the VU Block Model® in 2018 as part of an edu-
cational reform agenda to enhance student engagement, satisfaction, and academic
### outcomes (Ambler et   al., 2021, McCluskey et   al., 2019). The model, delivers
immersive, single-unit courses over four weeks, reflecting global trends in inten- sive and block teaching. However, it stands out through its institution-wide adop-
tion (Samarawickrema & Cleary, 2021), providing smaller class sizes and facilitat- ing stronger bonds between students and educators by allowing exclusive focus on
one subject at a time (Weldon & Konjarski, 2024). Initial evidence revealed posi-
### tive impacts on student engagement and performance (Loton et al., 2022; McClus-
key et al., 2019; Samarawickrema & Cleary, 2021), yet further research is needed to understand academics’ perspectives and refine teaching practices within the VU
### Block Model®.
### The key focus underpinning all aspects of VU Block Model® delivery principles
is inclusive teaching, as a means to support student learning, success, and the stu-
### dent experience (Samarawickrema & Cleary, 2021). Active and IBL are fundamen-
### tal components of these delivery principles, emphasising immersive experiences
### and opportunities for ongoing feedback (Samarawickrema & Cleary, 2021). Within
### the VU Block Model® context, academics responsible for designing units follow a
### rigorous design process that includes engaging with the VU Block Model® prin-
ciples, collaborating with colleagues from different facilities and learning design- ers to ensure high-quality learning experiences for students and undergoing a peer
review prior to delivery. This paper concentrates on the delivery principles of active
### learning and IBL, recognising them as crucial elements with inherent depth and
### complexity.

### Data Collection
### Semi-structured interviews, 30–50 min in duration, were conducted over the Zoom
### video conference platform and voice recorded with participants’ permission. The
interview questions focused on various aspects, including participants’experiences in Block Model teaching, the alignment of this teaching approach with their peda-

### interest in having observational data collected on their teaching practices.
### Data Analysis
Reflexive thematic analysis methods outlined by Braun and Clarke (2022) that incor-
### porated rigorous and systematic procedures were implemented. Researchers inde-
pendently created open codes on two participant transcriptions, then met to discuss initial ideas and relationships between codes. This process was repeated with other
transcriptions, refining codes collaboratively. The research team regularly met to systematically organise codes into meaningful themes. Once consensus was reached,
three researchers conducted inter-rater reliability to ensure the consistency and accu-
### racy of the coding process by cross-checking independently coded transcripts.
The research team brought prior experience in school and higher education teach- ing, including familiarity with active and IBL, which informed the interpretation
of participants’ pedagogical descriptions. Both participants’ explicit language and researchers’ inferences were used to identify active and IBL practices. Participants
were initially asked to define these approaches by describing what they look like in the VU Block Model® classrooms and detailing their strategies. In many cases, par-
ticipants used the language of active and IBL explicitly. However, in later stages of the interview, where questions did not necessarily directly reference these terms, the
research team made inferences based on participants’ descriptions of their pedagogi- cal practices. These combined approaches allowed the research team to ground the
themes in both the participants’ expressed understanding and the broader pedagogi- cal principles underlying the study. Themes are presented in the findings below.

### Findings
The   analyses   from   the   semi-structured   interviews   with   academic   participants revealed insights into their perspectives of active learning and IBL practices in the
Block Model. The academics extensively discussed the use of active learning strat- egies, highlighting strong examples in their implementation. Active learning was
described as a multifaceted approach, encompassing peer teaching and collabora- tion, hands-on activities, and connected to authentic contexts such as real-world
problems and placement. However, participants grappled with the conceptualisa- tion of IBL in the Block Model. Two themes emerged from academic perceptions
of IBL: (a) the reduction of IBL to posing questions, and (b) IBL as more than ques- tioning to embrace collection and analysis of data.
Active Learning as Peer‑Teaching and Collaboration There were elements of collaboration and peer-teaching that were intertwined with
active learning. Participants emphasised the importance of this social interaction component to not only deepen understanding of concepts, but also cultivate a learn-
ing community where students construct knowledge together.
Small group tasks were featured in many participant reflections on engagement in the learning process. Participants encouraged learners to collectively explore, dis-
cuss and apply concepts. This collaborative approach was discussed together with aspects of critical thinking and problem-solving, but also through promotion of peer
interaction and consideration of diverse perspectives. Participant 6 valued moments where students contribute spontaneously, moving away from a teacher-centric model
towards a peer-led dynamic. This shift, particularly observed in Block Mode, exem- plifies successful active learning, where students co-create ideas and engage in col-
laborative problem-solving: An effective task is a task that a small group can work on, and create some aspect of that task, whether they come back with a performance or presenta-
tion or, or a situation where the whole small group has contributed, and is able to successfully do their, their whatever it is, and others in the group, partici-
pate and respond to them. I love it when the students actually [get] stuck when something happens. And then someone says, a comment, and another person
adds in… I find that is [the] most successful learning, because the students are moving away from the me, the lecturer, or the teacher…they’re actually
contributing. That’s the magical moment. And I guess that happens more in Block, I think, than it did in the in the in the other ways, mainly because you
do have a quite intense time (Participant 6).
Often, participants shared constructivist pedagogy of active learning through embedding questioning, discussions, and various other interaction modes through-

### Discussion
The landscape of higher education is continually evolving, with a growing emphasis on pedagogical innovation to enhance learning outcomes. Within this context, there
arises a need to delve deeper into understanding pedagogical practices, particu- larly in intensive teaching settings (Lodge & Ashford-Rowe, 2024). It is essential
to explore how these practices align with robust theoretical frameworks to ensure effective educational delivery. In their commentary of effective teaching, Devlin and
Samarawickrema (2022) highlight the significance of embracing   new pedagogies that foster active, participatory approaches in higher education. As such, there is a
need to investigate how pedagogical innovations can be effectively integrated into intensive teaching environments to enhance the learning experiences of students and
consider what these teaching methodologies look like in practice.
While there is a wealth of research supporting the effectiveness of active learning and IBL in traditional classroom settings, their integration into intensive delivery
modes remains relatively unexplored. While research has investigated the VU Block Model® design principles (Kuiper et al., 2015), there are limited theoretical connec-
tions to the delivery principles of active learning and IBL in the intensive delivery mode despite the potential benefits these pedagogical approaches offer. Understand-
ing academics’ perceptions of implementing these strategies within the constraints of intensive delivery modes is crucial. By shedding light on the pedagogical beliefs
and perceived practices shaping student learning experiences, research in this area can provide valuable insights into optimising teaching methodologies in intensive
settings. To begin exploring how academics perceive the implementation of active and IBL strategies in intensive delivery modes, our study provides insight into the
conceptual nature of active and IBL in the university classroom.
Examining the academics’ perspectives of active learning in this research reveals a clear resonance with Dewey’s concept of dynamic learning experiences that equip
students for active engagement with the complexities of the world (Dewey, 1938).
Academics consistently described active learning as immersive, hands-on activities with real-world applications, providing numerous examples that illustrate its align-
ment with the objectives of intensive teaching. Participant responses reinforced active learning as a core delivery principle, seen as a valuable approach for quickly
engaging students with complex content (Al-Rawi & Lazonby, 2017). However, as Lutes and Davies (2013) note, the condensed timelines in intensive programs may
limit students’time for deep content assimilation, which, coupled with a heavy

---

Innovative Higher Education workload, can impact course satisfaction (Lee & Horsfall, 2010). While these chal- lenges may be inherent in intensive delivery modes, it is important to recognise that
### learning is an ongoing process, and students may deepen their understanding and
integrate complex concepts into their practice after the unit and/or course concludes.
The concentrated pace of intensive delivery modes thus highlights the importance of equipping students with foundational knowledge and skills during the course that
they can reflect on and reapply in praxis over time. Active learning strategies are particularly effective in fostering immediate and meaningful engagement, creating
### learning experiences that not only address immediate outcomes but also provide
a basis for sustained growth. Engaging in active learning experiences in intensive modes is crucial for student-focused learning, echoing Dewey’s advocacy for learn-
ing environments where students actively solve problems and take responsibility for
### their learning (Dewey, 1938).
Certainly, curriculum design plays a pivotal role in shaping the adoption of active learning within the intensive delivery mode (Konjarski et al., 2023). As discussed
by Konjarski et al., the emphasis on a flipped classroom approach inherent in VU
### Block Model® accentuates the importance of pre-class engagement with content,
allowing face-to-face class time to be dedicated to active learning strategies. Par- ticipant 7 highlighted, “I usually teach… it’s that idea of a flipped classroom… if
you’re coming together for three hours, you want to be engaged, you want people to be in groups and talking.” In the context of this study, participants described deliber-
ate efforts in crafting learning experiences that immersed students in active learn- ing. Some participants confirmed how the intensive delivery mode itself facilitated
opportunities for them, as academics, to plan and implement these immersive expe- riences. The narratives from participants established the significance of purpose-
ful curriculum design in creating an ecosystem that supported students towards the
### dynamic processes of knowledge creation, aligning with Dewey’s (1938) conceptu-
### alisation of learning.
Participants in this research emphasised active learning as learner-centered and engaging in authentic contexts. By placing the learner at the center of the learning
process, there is a deep consideration of pedagogical practices aimed at enhancing deep approaches to learning (Entwistle, 2009). Additionally, teaching approaches
that prioritise a student-centred approach and support the learning process positively impact the student experience and satisfaction (Tan et al., 2016). This additional
emphasis on providing learning experiences that parallel real-world environments
### and situations resonates with Dewey’s theory of learning. Deep learning occurs
### through the experience of interaction with others and the environment (Dewey,
1938), which was described by participants in this study through immersive activi- ties, peer teaching and collaborative learning, as well as situated authentic contexts
### in school settings such as placements.
### Varying levels of comprehension of IBL were demonstrated by participants in
this study, with a notable scarcity of examples within VU Block Model®. The lim- ited examples may be attributed to the compressed nature of the delivery mode, con-
straining the time needed for students to effectively engage in the cyclical process of action and reflection integral to IBL (Spronken-Smith, 2012). Cohesive course
design emerges as a critical factor in IBL, differentiating it from active learning.

principles which could serve as a bridge, fostering stronger connections between
### design intent and instructional implementation of IBL learning experiences. The
implementation of IBL requires careful and cohesive design at a unit or course level
### to allow for classroom implementation. The identified barriers to implementing
IBL in school settings, including the lack of training for teachers unfamiliar with IBL, extensive resources required and perceived time constraints (Dorier & García,
2013), can similarly impede the successful integration of IBL in higher education
### settings.
### The challenges of implementing IBL in intensive delivery contexts may arise
from the time-intensive nature of the planning and implementation cycles of IBL.
Time can be perceived as a challenge for academics preparing for teaching in the
### intensive delivery mode (Thomas et al., 2024), which may impact the adoption of
IBL due to the considerable amount of time required for designing and executing the cyclical model. In addition, IBL can be considered resource intense due to the diver-
sity of materials and resources required and the level of flexibility and responsive- ness required to fully address student’s needs, interests and questions. Further, this
approach requires skilled facilitators who have the pedagogical expertise to effec- tively facilitate and guide inquiry-based projects requiring an investment of facilita-
tor time and expertise (Fitzgerald et al., 2019). Kirschner et al. (2006) noted dis-
### comfort and reluctance by academic staff when implementing IBL due to the shift
required in the academic moving from the role of the teacher to the facilitator.
### Given these challenges, active learning may serve as a practical and sufficient
pedagogical approach in intensive teaching contexts, to engage students in meaning- ful learning opportunities that align well with the demands of intensive delivery.
While IBL may offer a potentially deeper opportunity for exploration and reflec- tion, the logistical constraints of intensive teaching may make active learning a more
feasible and impactful choice for addressing students’learning needs. However, fur- ther research is needed to explore how elements of IBL can be further incorporated
### within the dynamics of intensive delivery modes, to perhaps complement active
### learning and enhance student learning outcomes.
### Incorporating IBL methods into learning experiences nurtures a mindset of
curiosity, continual inquiry, and lifelong learning, closely aligning with Dewey’s
### concept of education as an ongoing and reflective process. This alignment with
### Dewey’s vision was evident in this research through academics’ comments, which
highlighted knowledge “co-construction”, the role of “questioning”, and reflexive learning practices such as “drawing on things that have happened” and cyclically
“connecting what you’ll learn and what you know”. In the context of IBL, students
### are positioned as active agents, encouraged to question and explore boundaries
through problem solving (Aditomo et al., 2013). This represents a paradigm shift in the power dynamics of education, with autonomy and agency shifting progressively
### towards the students as a foundation of future education (Charteris & Smardon,
### 2017). The evolving role of academics within this framework highlights the need
for a more facilitative and guiding design rather than traditional didactic approaches, supporting Dewey’s (1938) notion of empowering students in the learning process.

ing and mastery of concepts. Research into intensive delivery modes also supports
### depth of learning as feasible and effective (Austin & Gustafson, 2006).
### To operationalise a more comprehensive integration of these principles, it is
imperative that universities establish dedicated spaces for professional learning cen- tered on these pedagogical principles (Samarawickrema et al., 2024). Establishing
a learning community where educators can collaboratively delve into these princi- ples, observe one another’s practices, and engage in peer teaching activities would
### contribute to a more cohesive and informed adoption of IBL and active learning
strategies. The facilitation of IBL requires a reflective, action-research approach, and educators may need professional learning to engage effectively in this process. The
collaborative nature of the learning communities can enable a collective negotiation of cultural, values, beliefs, and professional backgrounds, fostering a shared under-
standing that contributes to the evolution of pedagogical practices (Freire, 1970; Luguetti et al., 2018). This collective approach to professional development aligns
### with contemporary notions of teaching as a communal and evolving practice.
### Limitations and Future Research
While this current study has provided valuable insights into academics’ perspectives of IBL and active learning in an intensive delivery mode of higher education, it is
important to acknowledge its limitations. One limitation of the study is that data were collected through academics’interviews regarding their practices rather than
through observation of those practices. This reliance on self-reported data is limited, as faculty members might provide responses influenced by their perceptions or inter-
pretations of their actions, rather than an objective account of their actual practices.
In designing this research, a deliberate decision was made to focus exclusively on the perspectives of academics within the discipline to provide a nuanced explora-
tion of their unique insights and professional experiences. The purposive sampling technique employed sought to capture participants from the education discipline,
emphasising their pedagogical expertise and professional backgrounds. By narrow- ing the scope to academics, the study aimed to address gaps in the literature on aca-
demics’ perceptions of intensive delivery modes, such as the Block Model, and their application to active learning and IBL. Progression of research beyond interviews
could be achieved by incorporating classroom observations and other data collection forms (e.g., academics field notes, interviews post lesson, photovoice and others)
### in future studies supporting a more nuanced understanding of how these pedago-
gies are enacted in practice. To deepen the understanding of pedagogical practices, employing case studies, ethnographic or action research methodologies could also
be of benefit to understand contextual factors influencing instructional decisions.
### Furthermore, future research could explore how advancements in Learning Man-
### agement Systems (LMS) align with and support intensive delivery modes, provid-
ing insights into how technology facilitates and enhances pedagogical innovation in these contexts. Finally, a further crucial dimension to consider for future studies is

---

Innovative Higher Education the inclusion of the student experience. Understanding how students perceive and engage with these pedagogies is essential for a comprehensive evaluation of their
effectiveness. Integrating student perspectives in future research would provide valu- able insights into the impact of active learning and IBL on learning outcomes and
the overall educational experience.
Second, a more thorough exploration of the academics’prior knowledge and pro- fessional development regarding active learning and IBL is warranted. In our study,
we concentrated on the academics’perceptions of these pedagogies without delving deeply into their previous experiences. While it was assumed that participants, as
teacher educators, possessed substantial pedagogical knowledge due to their pro- fessional roles, their perceptions of IBL varied, reflecting diverse interpretations
and applications of the approach. While we acknowledge that their understanding of active learning and IBL influences how they implement these approaches, this
aspect was not examined in our research. At Victoria University, professional devel- opment on high-impact teaching within the VU Block Model® is available to staff,
with a particular focus on active learning strategies; however, it is also not known whether participants engaged with this training. Future studies should investigate
how academics’prior experiences and professional development shape their enact- ment of these pedagogies in practice. Such studies could include an intervention
component, such as providing professional development on IBL strategies, followed by a subsequent evaluation period to assess how academics incorporate these strate-
gies into their teaching over time. This approach may offer valuable insights into the long-term impacts of targeted professional development and the sustainability of
pedagogical innovations in higher education contexts.
### Conclusion
Echoing Lodge and Ashford-Rowe’s (2024) call for a deeper understanding of learn- ing processes within intensive delivery modes, this research underscores the critical
role of examining innovative pedagogical approaches in such contexts and consid- ering their implementation in a university classroom. The shift towards learner-
centred approaches, as conceptualised by Dewey’s (1938) notion of dynamic learn- ing experiences, was explored through active learning, and IBL teaching methods.
While academics expressed active learning practices were evident and some even enhanced by the structure of the VU Block Model®, the integration of IBL was
described less frequently. This raises important questions about whether the limited perceived use of IBL stemmed from the intensive course structure, academics’ lim-
ited knowledge of IBL principles, or the reduction of IBL to surface-level practices, such as incorporating questioning in class exercises. Furthermore, active learning
may even serve as a practical and sufficient approach to meet the immediate learning needs of students in intensive teaching contexts. These findings point to the need for
targeted operational support and professional learning to enhance understanding and effective implementation of active and IBL principles.
Future research should explore how theoretical frameworks, such as Deweyan learning, can support faculty in embedding deeper inquiry-based practices across

---

Innovative Higher Education diverse delivery modes. It is essential to provide academics with professional learn- ing spaces where they can not only develop new pedagogical strategies but also inte-
grate these approaches into both intensive and regular courses in meaningful ways.
Without intentional support, learner-centred teaching may be inconsistent, with some approaches like IBL remaining underutilised. As this study indicates, foster-
ing pedagogical innovation requires more than simply adopting intensive course for- mats—it demands a continuous interplay between theory, practice, and structured
opportunities for academic reflection and growth.
