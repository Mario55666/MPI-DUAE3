Abstract Student-created videos engage students’ interests, creativity, and content knowledge and enrich collaborative learning
in STEM education. These videos enhance critical thinking and analytical skills, which are essential tools in the fields of
science, technology, engineering, and mathematics (STEM). This study presents the results of such an assignment across several STEM areas (biology, chemistry, exercise science, information technology, and mathematics) at a minority-serving,
liberal arts higher education institution in the southeast region of the United States. Undergraduate students ( n   =   557)
across varied, online course modalities (synchronous and asynchronous) were required to create four problem-solving videos 3–8 min in duration. Assessment tools included a self-assessment of learning gains survey given to control and
experimental groups and a post-video creation survey given only to experimental groups. Grade data was also collected from all sections. Comparing the experimental and control groups, students showed a statistically significant gain in
their ability to give oral presentations, create videos, and edit videos. Qualitative data from free-response questions corroborate these gains and suggest that students also grew in content knowledge and conceptual understanding
through these assignments. Our study implements a multimedia theoretical framework which suggests students learn more effectively from consuming presentations with both auditory and visual components. Our results suggest students
see similar gains from producing presentations with both auditory and visual components. Further our results suggest that multimedia production enhances students’   presentation skills. From a practical perspective, this study suggests that
faculty should incorporate student-created videos in online classes, which typically require oral presentations in person.
Faculty are also advised to require oral and visual components within these videos to maximize learning gains from the perspective of a multimedia theoretical framework.

### 1   Introduction
With the rise of online courses following the pandemic, the importance of authentic assignments is ever-increasing.
Students have a wealth of resources available to them while completing online assignments and assessments, so incorporating verbal explanations of students’ methods and thoughts helps to ensure a higher degree of academic
integrity. One way to accomplish this is to utilize short student-created video solutions to individual problems mul- tiple times throughout a semester.
1.1   Background Student-created videos have gained significant attention in higher education as a powerful tool for content genera-
tion and active learning [1–3]. When undergraduate students are asked to create and produce video presentations, they are immersed in a valuable opportunity that allows meaningful learning and creative expression [4]. The ben-
efits of student-created videos in higher education are numerous. Speed and his colleagues [5] have shown that student-created videos promote deeper learning and foster critical thinking skills. Additionally, collaborative video
creation projects enhance student engagement and encourage active participation [6]. These findings underscore the transformative potential of student-created videos.
Student-created videos are intrinsically an interdisciplinary assignment but are especially suitable for STEM courses which often require complex problem-solving [7]. Additionally, these videos are ideal for visualizing and demonstrat-
ing STEM ideas. Student-created videos in STEM courses promote higher levels of understanding and knowledge retention than traditional instructional methods [8]. The evidence highlights the significant role that student-created
videos play in enhancing learning outcomes within STEM education. Student-created videos also provide an ideal alternative to in-person oral presentations which are not available for fully online classes. Campbell and colleagues
implemented student-created videos in fully online STEM courses for pre-service teachers and found that student- created videos contributed to an increase in students’ perceived STEM content knowledge, improved perceptions of
self-efficacy, and students’ engagement in the behavioral, affective, and cognitive domains [1].
Student-created videos may also aid in reducing the stress induced by oral presentations, as this assignment type can be particularly challenging compared to other assignments [9, 10]. In addition, students with a fear of public
speaking associate oral presentations with negative college experiences [11]. Student-created videos avoid these pitfalls by allowing students to record their videos multiple times and review their presentations before submitting
them, thus decreasing the fear of making mistakes or forgetting information in front of a live audience. Students can also record the videos in a comfortable environment and, as a result, improve their performance when conveying
information.
1.2   Theoretical framework A variety of theoretical frameworks have been used in the investigation of student-created videos such as con-
structivist [12], experiential learning [13], and multimedia theory[14]. Constructivist frameworks have also been used with other distance learning approaches, such as virtual reality environments [15]. In our work, we consider
student-created videos from a multimedia theoretical framework. Multimedia learning theory provides an essential framework for understanding how individuals learn from multimedia content. The theory suggests that learners
process information through two channels: one for verbal material and one for visual material. By leveraging both channels simultaneously, learning can be enhanced through the integration of auditory (spoken) and visual (image,
diagram) representations. In the context of student-created videos, this framework aligns well, as the process of creat-
ing videos requires students to articulate their thoughts verbally while simultaneously presenting visual information, such as diagrams, animations, or slide content [15]. According to multimedia theory, the separation of content into
two domains also reduces cognitive load thus enhancing learning outcomes. This process helps facilitate a deeper understanding of the subject matter, as it engages multiple modes of learning, reinforcing key concepts through
varied representation [16, 17].

### 1.3   The knowledge gap and the present study
The reviewed literature highlights the promise of student-created videos; however, several limitations exist in the current body of knowledge. First, the sample sizes of these studies are typically quite small. For the studies above in
which sample size was reported, it ranged from   n   =   74 to   n   =   113 [5, 6]. To our knowledge, no studies larger than 113
participants have been reported. Second, the studies typically investigate just one academic discipline, limiting the applicability of their results. Thirdly, they typically lack a control group; of the surveyed literature, only Stanley et al.
incorporated a control group [6]. To address these shortcomings, the present work investigated student-created videos in a large number of STEM-related courses with a randomized controlled trial design. This study design will
provide the basis for robust practical recommendations on the utility of student-created videos.
Previously, multimedia theory has been examined in the context of content knowledge acquired through an instruc- tor’s multimedia presentation. For example, a multimedia presentation on STEM content teaches students more effec-
tively about this content. Our study is theoretically novel in two ways: first—will the creation of multimedia content, rather than its consumption, improve learning outcomes? (i.e., will   giving   a multimedia presentation on STEM content
effectively teach students about this content?). Second—will a multimedia presentation on a specific content area improve students’ presentation skills? (i.e., will giving a multimedia presentation on STEM content effectively teach
student about oral presentations?).
### 1.4   The present study, research questions and key variables
The present study implemented student-created video assignments in 24 sections of fully online undergraduate courses across many STEM disciplines (biology, chemistry, exercise science, information technology (IT), and mathematics). The
course modalities for this study included 13 synchronous sections, as well as 11 asynchronous sections, with 557 stu- dents in total enrolled in these courses. In addition, each section was randomly assigned to an experimental or control
group to study the impact of these assignments. Within the experimental sections, students were individually required to create a total of four problem-solving videos which ran approximately 3–8 min each. The efficacy of these assign-
ments was assessed by the following measures: (1) a self-assessment of learning gains (SALG) survey given to control and experimental sections, and (2) a post-video creation survey given to experimental sections only. All courses took
place at Georgia Gwinnett College (GGC), a minority-serving institution located in the southeast region of the United States with a total enrollment of approximately 11,000 students, at the time of this study.
In our study, students are required to solve qualitative and quantitative problems and present the results in a video.
Across all multidisciplinary courses, there are common educational objectives for the institution. These essential skills
are termed Integrated Educational Experience Student Learning Outcomes which include (but are not limited to): (1)   Clearly communicate ideas in written and oral form.
(2)   Demonstrate creativity and critical thinking in inter- and multidisciplinary contexts.
(3)   Demonstrate effective use of information technology.
Each of the above IEE student learning objectives overlap with key variables that will be explored in this study. Those variables include problem-solving, critical thinking, oral communication and the usage of technology. In our study, stu-
dents are required to solve qualitative and quantitative problems and to present the results in a video. Our key research
questions are: (1)   What impact do student-created videos have on problem-solving abilities and critical thinking?
(2)   What impact do student-created videos have on oral presentation skills?
(3)   How do students perceive this assignment in their learning experience?
These research questions involve three key variables – problem solving, critical thinking and oral presentation skills.
These key variables are defined below.
Mourtos et al. define problem-solving “as a process, used to obtain a best answer to an unknown, or a decision sub- ject to some constraints” [18]. This process should not be confused with exercise solving—which is, for example, solving
instances of textbook problems. True problem-solving abilities should be transferable to other domains of knowledge [19].

of successfully achieving some goal [20]. The general nature of this definition makes it plain that this is an essential skill
in education, the workplace, and life in general. Critical thinking in the context of scientific inquiry can also be broken
down into component skills such as interpreting, evaluating, explaining, and others as explained by Utami et al. [21].
Problem-solving can be thought of as a specific application of critical thinking where the desired goal is determining a solution to an important problem.
Effective oral presentation arises from a combination of complex skills which includes clearly understanding content, effectively organizing content both cognitively and practically, and engaging speaking strategies (volume, pace, tone,
eye contact, etc.) [22]. Critically, the requirement to clearly understand content connects this variable to problem-solving
and critical thinking within our study.
The key variables under examination are informed by multimedia theory. According to established theory, students should make gains in problem-solving and critical thinking based on creating and synthesizing audio and visual rep-
resentations of key STEM content. The final variable, oral presentation skills, extends multimedia theory to developing presentation skills through the construction and synthesis of audio and visual components of the presentation itself.
### 2   Methods
2.1   Assignment description Students were required to produce four problem-solving-oriented videos over the semester, comprising 15% of their
overall course grade. Students were given instructions that stated the problem they were required to solve, a time requirement (3–8 min long), a rubric, and some general guidelines on how to submit their video. These documents are
provided in the supporting information. Students were given no formal requirements on   how   each video was to be filmed
or produced, giving them the freedom to choose which platforms they would want to use.
2.2   Description of survey tools The principal measures were post-video creation surveys, a SALG survey, and grade data. This tool provides qualitative
data. Students in the experimental sections were surveyed after the completion of their videos (post-video creation survey) to assess gains and frustration levels from the assignment. This survey was given twice, once after the first video
deadline and again after the fourth video deadline to assess any changes in gains with successive videos. Two experimen-
tal sections completed less than four videos and so their responses were only included in the first survey. No incentive
was given to students for responding to this survey, which led to a relatively low response rate [99 students (50%) for the
first survey and 69 (35%) for the second survey]. A SALG survey, which involved quantitative responses asking students to
rate their gain in a variety of areas from 1 (no gain) to 5 (great gain), was given at the end of the semester. SALG surveys
are an effective means to measure students’ self-reported sense of progress in a particular learning outcome [23]. While
self-assessment surveys are typically observed to be positively correlated with other measures, they do typically overes-
timate gains [24]. This overestimation is examined in greater detail in the discussion section of our paper. Lastly, students
were also asked a variety of free-response questions. This data was collected from both the control and experimental sections. This survey had a significantly higher number of responses since both control and experimental sections were
surveyed (198 total, 36%). For the SALG, students were offered three bonus points on their final exam as an incentive.
(Note: Students were able to earn these bonus points by answering the survey questions or explicitly opting out, so participation was not required). An example of each survey tool used is included in the supporting information. Lastly,
grade data was collected from all sections including the course final grade and the grade on each video assignment.
Unfortunately, no statistically significant differences in grade data were found between the experimental and control groups and this grade data is not reported. These survey tools and their relationship to the control and experimental
groups are summarized in Fig. 1.

Responses to the SALG survey ranged from 1 (no gain) to 5 (great gain) which readily enabled a computation of the mean and standard deviation of each response. The results for the experimental and control groups were then compared using
an independent samples   t -test to determine if statistically significant differences were observed. An identical procedure
was adopted for the post-video creation survey. In this case, the results after the first and fourth videos were compared
to determine if a statistically significant difference was present. They were not compared to the control group since the
control group did not complete the post-video creation survey.
The qualitative questions on the SALG survey were open-ended and did not specifically prompt students to discuss the video assignments. This means that relatively few responses mentioned videos. Every instance where a student used the
word   “video”   or   “videos”   about their   own   videos is included in the results below. This enables the reader of the manuscript
to directly observe the content of these qualitative responses. Given the relatively low number of responses on a given theme, these should be taken as an anecdotal confirmation of results that emerged quantitatively from the surveys.

### 4   Discussion
4.1   Student‑created video examples The diversity of these creations, and the array of others not shown, showcase the interdisciplinary and creative aspects
of student-created videos. This assignment type provides a unique opportunity for students to tailor their submissions according to their unique set of talents and experiences. This demonstrates a surprising level of technical expertise on
the part of students given the lack of specific instructions on format.
Our work aligns with the multimedia learning theory, as integrating visual and verbal elements leads to more effective learning outcomes by engaging learners through dual auditory and visual channels. The dual approach of this theory
likely explains the improvements observed in students’ STEM content knowledge as reflected in their video projects. The enhanced presentation skills and content mastery observed in this study align directly with the multimedia theoretical
framework, suggesting that managing cognitive load through verbal explanations and visual representations allows students to process information more effectively. The results from this study could indicate that the active integration
of visual and auditory components could help learners make meaningful connections between complex STEM concepts and real-world applications. The findings likely suggest that student-created videos are an effective pedagogical tool
for online STEM courses, enabling students to leverage multimedia learning principles to enhance critical thinking and develop technical skills. By offering content through multiple modalities in STEM courses, as the multimedia framework
suggests, students can engage in deeper processing, which supports long-term retention and mastery of complex subject matter. However, it is important to note that students who submitted video recordings without incorporating
digital media components missed critical opportunities for enhanced learning. Those students who merely speak to the camera only construct an auditory representation of their content and miss out on gains that could be observed from
visual representation [25].
Future investigations should directly compare student-created videos that require an oral speech to those that require a visual representation in addition to an oral speech (for example, using PowerPoint). Practically, instructors assigning
student-created videos should require students to present their content using both modalities.
4.2   The nature of self‑assessment The important results of the present study are derived from student self-assessment. To properly understand these
results its critical to examine the nature and accuracy of this assessment type. The body of literature on self-assessment
is immense and the interested reader is referred to the three meta-analyses performed on this topic [26–28]. An excellent
summary statement of the accuracy of self-assessment is found in León et al.: “Previous empirical evidence has found a clear relationship between scoring self-assessment and teachers’ grade and that students tended to overestimate their
performance [28].” In the meta-analysis by the same author, the magnitude of this overestimation corresponds to a Hedge’s   g   value of 0.2 and a   z -score overestimate of 0.472.[28, 29]. In addition, Leòn et al. found that specific moderating
effects can reduce or amplify the overestimation. For example, providing feedback to students on their assignments or, even better, on their self-assessment, typically reduces their overestimation while formative assessments tend to have
less accurate results. Discussing of a particular study that compares self-reported gains to an objective measure of student
learning may be helpful. For example, Noroozi et al. measured improvements in writing abilities via a rubric and by self-
reported perceptions after students completed a three-week online module [30]. While self-reported gains correlated positively with improvements as measured by the rubric, the correlation was not statistically significant. Additionally,

In the context of the present study, the literature on self-assessment suggests that our students overestimated their true gains in each of the skills assessed. It is also essential to consider the moderating effects discussed above that may
be present in our study. Fortunately, students were given feedback on their video assignments which would suggest a reduction in this overestimation [28]. Unfortunately, our survey tools are probably best categorized as a formative
assessment since they were not completed for a grade and, in some cases, were given across the semester [31]. Since formative assessments typically amplify overestimation, this would count in the opposite direction. These two effects are
roughly similar in magnitude with feedback reducing overestimation ( z   =   0.559) while formative assessments increasing
overestimation ( z   =   0.550) [28]. It is plausible, therefore, that our results have a relatively typical overestimate size even
given these moderators. The overestimate of student knowledge, and its variation from study to study, introduce some uncertainty in the survey results. The nature of this overestimate will be considered in each survey tool in the discussion
below.
### 4.3   Post‑video creation survey
Students reported that the video creation assignment led to gains in the abilities to produce videos, an increase in their knowledge of class content and that these assignments were more helpful than typical assignments in the course.
These self-assessments, however, are subject to the overestimation effect discussed in Sect. 4.3. Assuming our results involve the typical overestimate ( z   =   0.472) the results can be corrected for this bias by decreasing the reported gains
by approximately half a standard deviation. Reducing each gain by the standard deviation times the z-score of a typical overestimate yields the corrected values in Table 6.
The gains across each category are now more modest given students’ typical overestimation of their gains with the average in each case falling slightly below a score of three which was labeled as a “moderate gain” on the survey tool.
These results help to answer our third research question: (3) How do students perceive this assignment in their learning experience?
The students perceive student-created videos to be moderately helpful. A finding which will be further discussed in the following sections. This provides some support for the claim the multimedia presentations are effective for students
to acquire subject specific knowledge. Future studies should randomly assign students presentations with or without visual components to further investigate the relevance of multimedia theory to these gains.
This survey tool was given after both the first and fourth video, however, the gains reported in the ability to produce videos and in knowledge learned did not significantly change between the first and final video produced by students.
Additionally, the time spent on the first and fourth videos did not significantly change. Taken together, this suggests that
instructors should feel free to assign any number of video assignments to their students without concern of a decrease in learning with each subsequent video.

### (2) What impact do student-created videos have on oral presentation skills?
The most robust finding of the present study is that student-created videos serve as an effective means to develop oral presentation skills. This is especially important since oral presentation abilities are a critical skill and one that can
be challenging to develop in online courses [22, 32]. Van Ginkel et al. proposed another approach to this shortcoming addressed in the literature is the possibility of implementing virtual reality (VR) presentations with automated feedback
[33]. The outcomes were directly compared to face-to-face presentations and no statistically significant differences were
observed, suggesting VR presentations are also a suitable form of presentations for online courses. McDougall devel- oped an alternative approach to developing oral presentation skills in distance learning classes by requiring students
to schedule an in-person presentation at a venue and time of their choosing [32]. Since the presentation could not be directly evaluated by instructors, students completed a self-reflection activity and reported the presentation experience
activity to be a significant “even transformative” experience. While this approach places a logistical burden on students
to schedule their own talk, it does provide a robust in-person oral presentation within the context of a distance learning
class. Lastly, it is also possible to provide students with instruction in presentation skills even in the absence of presenting
in-person themselves [34]. While this strategy does not give students a comparable experience to in-person presenta- tions, it highlights an important means to develop this critical skill in a distance learning environment.
The quantitative SALG survey also provides the most robust basis for assessing improvements in critical thinking and problem-solving skills (our first research question). In the context of our study, outcomes related to critical thinking and
problem-solving showed no statistically significant differences, but a related category of questions showed an interesting
outcome. For questions that assessed students’ perceived gains in content knowledge and, especially, their confidence and comfort with the class content the experimental group rated their gains as   less   than the control group. For example,
students who created videos rated their gain in comfort levels with complex ideas at just 3.94 compared to a gain of 4.10
for the control group ( p   =   0.12). While these results individually are not statistically significant, it is surprising that each

This may be related to a well-known effect in the psychological literature called the   “illusion of explanatory depth”   [35].
Essentially, individuals typically show over-confidence in explanatory knowledge which leads them to overestimate their ability to explain the interrelationships between complex ideas. This suggests that students in STEM may overestimate
their understanding of this form of knowledge within their courses. For example, students may report a great gain in their confidence that they understand the material, but in reality, the gain is much less.
Interestingly, this illusion is weakened when individuals attempt to explain the content they supposedly understand so well [36, 37]. This may explain the decrease in confidence experienced by the students who are forced to explain complex
ideas through videos. While obviously, we desire students to make significant gains in confidence and knowledge, we also
desire that they can accurately assess the depth of their knowledge. Substantiating this claim would require additional research directly assessing this effect, something previously uninvestigated in STEM education.
This result translates directly to our understanding of the reliability of self-assessment. It appears that giving a pres-
entation on a specific subject area serves to reduce the overconfidence typically reported by students who assess their own gains. Giving oral presentations, then, may serve as a moderator of this overconfidence in a manner similar to faculty
feedback as discussed above. This is also a fruitful area for further investigation.

students did indeed acquire key 21st-century skills from this assessment. This suggests that student-created videos are an ideal assignment to integrate curriculum with key technological skills, a critical challenge in higher education [38].

Lastly, the qualitative results within the SALG survey corroborate the quantitative results from the surveys above con- cerning gains in the ability to create videos, edit videos, and give oral presentations. The qualitative results also suggest
that student videos led to gains in content knowledge and conceptual understanding. The results section lists every instance where a student mentioned “video” or “videos” in their comments. The low number of responses that discuss
these assignments suggests caution in interpreting these results. However, it is favorable that zero students suggest the
videos were not a helpful assignment.
These student comments are also helpful in answering our three research questions. Two relevant comments are discussed below with respect to each research question.
(1) What impact do student-created videos have on problem-solving abilities and critical thinking?
“The videos helped me understand the subject more because we had to record ourselves explaining the steps and how to work the problem.”
This student comment supports the claim that student-created videos aided students in problem-solving and criti- cal thinking, two of our key variables. Interestingly the above comment specifically mentions the visual ( recording ) and
audio ( explaining the steps ) as helpful for understanding the subject. This is perhaps the strongest qualitative result with
respect to our theoretical framework suggesting that this student was aided by synthesizing multiple modes of learning as multimedia theory suggests.
“I received helpful feedback on my video projects. I liked the video projects for a grade because they were helpful in my
learning.” This comment is illustrative of the importance of feedback and grades to learning. This student specifically states that
the comments received, and the grades received aided in learning.

“Better [speaking] skills, thanks to videos.” “Making videos and oral speech.” These students both explicitly connect the student-created videos to their improvements in oral presentation skills

4.6   Limitations This study was performed at a single institution which may limit the applicability of its findings to contexts with sig-
nificantly different demographics. This study also includes a variety of different online modalities (synchronous and asynchronous) this may have introduced variability in how the video assignments were given and assessed. As discussed

### 5   Conclusion
In responses to the SALG survey, students reported statistically significant increases in the ability to create and to edit
videos as well as a statistically significant gain in their ability to give oral presentations. These quantitative results are con-
sistent with the qualitative responses given by students on the SALG survey. These qualitative results indicated the videos
helped them: (a) understand course content, (b) enhance technical skills related to video production, and (c) improve their
oral communication. The post-video creation survey indicated these assignments improved their knowledge of course content and involved minimal student frustration. Despite these gains, no statistically significant differences in success-
ful course completion were observed due to the student-created video assignment. Of the key variables investigated in this study (problem-solving, critical thinking, and presentation skills) presentation skills showed a significant positive
outcome. The utility of student-created videos for growth in content knowledge is well established [1, 8]. Previous work
has also shown the video creation project as helpful in developing digital literacy—such as the ability to produce and edit videos [39–41]. The literature on student video creation and oral presentations primarily focuses on the develop-
ment of fluency in foreign language education [42–44]. Previous work has also been completed showing that student viewing   of their own presentations helped develop oral presentation abilities [45]. To our knowledge, the present study
is the first that directly looks at gains to oral presentation abilities from the production of student-created videos more
generally. Additionally, the present work highlights a potential avenue of future investigation—the illusion of explana-
tory depth within the context of STEM education. Future work should examine closely the changes in oral presentation quality before and after the production of repeated student-created videos and directly compare these gains to those
achieved through in-person presentations.
Traditionally, the multimedia theoretical framework suggests students learn from   consuming   multimedia content, our results suggest that gains can similarly be observed by   producing   multimedia content. Our results further suggest
that multimedia assignments aid students’ growth in oral presentation abilities. Instructors wishing to adopt this assign-
ment type within their online courses are advised to encourage students to include oral and multimedia components in their presentations consistent with a multimedia theoretical framework. From a practical perspective, instructors
should strongly consider this assignment type in their online courses when their corresponding in-person class typically
requires oral presentations.
