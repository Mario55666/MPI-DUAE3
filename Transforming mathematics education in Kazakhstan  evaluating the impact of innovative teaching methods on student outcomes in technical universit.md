1. Introduction
As a rapidly developing nation, Kazakhstan has placed significant emphasis on education as a key driver of economic growth and societal progress (OECD, 2023). The country ’ s strategic development plans,
including the Kazakhstan – 2050 Strategy, highlight the critical role of human capital development in achieving long-term economic diversification and technological advancement (Hor, 2021). In this context,
proficiency in mathematics is particularly crucial, as it underpins many of the skills required for innov- ation, scientific research, and technological development (Agaidarov et al., 2020).
However,   recent   assessments   have   revealed   trends   in   mathematics   performance   among   the Kazakhstani students. The 2022 Programme for International Student Assessment (PISA) results showed
that Kazakhstan ’ s 15-year-olds scored 425 points on mathematics, which is significantly below the OECD average of 472 points (OECD, 2024). This performance gap persists in higher education, with employers
reporting difficulties finding graduates with adequate quantitative skills (Hartley et al., 2016).
Kazakhstan faces issues such as outdated curricula, traditional teaching methods, and a digital divide, which   are   common   obstacles   in   many   developing   nations   striving   to   enhance   STEM   education

2. Research background
The transformation of educational practices in Kazakhstan has resulted in the adoption of various innovative teaching methods beyond traditional lecture-based instruction, each with its own merits and
challenges in the local context. Among these approaches, flipped classroom methodology has gained particular attention in Kazakhstani universities. Bekbolganova (2022) demonstrated that implementing
flipped classroom techniques in mathematics courses led to improved student engagement and concep- tual understanding, with a significant increase in academic performance compared to traditional teach-
ing methods. Similarly, eLearning initiatives have become increasingly prominent, particularly following the COVID – 19 pandemic. A comprehensive study of Kazakhstani universities by Semenov et al. (2024)
revealed that incorporating digital learning platforms and online interactive tools in mathematics educa- tion resulted in enhanced student participation and better learning outcomes, particularly in abstract
mathematical concepts.
Problem-based learning (PBL) has emerged as a significant pedagogical approach in Kazakhstan ’ s mathematics education. Unlike project-based learning, which focuses on creating a specific end product,
problem-based learning emphasizes the process of solving complex open-ended problems. Tursynkulova et al. (2023) found that mathematics students exposed to PBL demonstrated stronger analytical skills
and   better   retention   of   theoretical   concepts   than   those   in   traditional   learning   environments.
Additionally,   game-based   learning   and   adaptive   learning   technologies   have   shown   promise   in Kazakhstani classrooms, with Sansyzbayev (2023) reporting increased student motivation and improved
problem-solving abilities when gamification elements are integrated into calculus courses.
2.1. Project-based learning (PjBL) Project-based learning (PjBL) is an innovative pedagogical approach that has gained significant attention
in educational research and practice in the past few decades. Rooted in constructivist learning theory, PjBL is characterized by student-centered, inquiry-driven learning experiences that revolve around com-
plex, authentic problems or questions (Krajcik et al., 2023). The core principles of PjBL include student autonomy, collaborative learning, and creation of tangible artifacts that demonstrate understanding
(Larmer & Mergendoller, 2010).
The theoretical   foundations of PjBL   have been   drawn   from   several influential   learning theories.
Constructivism, as articulated by Piaget and Vygotsky, emphasizes the active role of learners in con- structing knowledge through experience and social interaction (Sancar-Tokmak & Dogusoy, 2023). The
experiential learning theory, developed by Kolb (1984), aligns closely with PjBL ’ s emphasis on hands-on

In higher education, PjBL has been implemented across various disciplines with notable success and challenges. Compared with traditional lecture-based instruction, PjBL has shown promise in fostering

ment across STEM disciplines. Beyond academic performance, PjBL has been associated with increased student motivation and engagement (Wijnia et al., 2024). Moreover, PjBL has been shown to foster the
development   of   21st-century   skills,   such   as   critical   thinking,   collaboration,   and   communication (Almazroui, 2023).
PjBL has been shown to be effective in enhancing mathematical proficiency in mathematics educa- tion. Boaler (2002) conducted a longitudinal study comparing PjBL and traditional approaches to math-
ematics   education,   and   found   that   students   in   PjBL   environments   developed   more   flexible   and applicable mathematical knowledge. Similarly, Faulkner et al. (2023) argued that PjBL approaches in
mathematics foster a deeper conceptual understanding by allowing students to construct mathematical meanings through problem-solving experiences.
The body of research on the effectiveness of PjBL has grown substantially by employing various methodological approaches. Meta-analyses and systematic reviews have provided valuable insights into
the overall impact of PjBL across educational contexts (Zhang & Ma, 2023). However, researchers have noted gaps in current PjBL research, particularly in mathematics education at the tertiary level. There is
a need for more rigorous, large-scale studies that examine the long-term effects of PjBL on mathematical proficiency and the transfer of learning (Loyens et al., 2023).
### 2.2. Literature solutions for mathematics education
In response to persistent challenges in mathematics education, researchers and educators have pro- posed various solutions to enhance student learning and engagement. One prominent approach is the
implementation of innovative pedagogical strategies. Active learning methods that engage students dir- ectly in the learning process have shown significant promise in improving their mathematical under-
standing and retention (Ting et al., 2023). The flipped classroom model, in which students engage with content outside class and use class time for problem-solving and discussion, has demonstrated positive
effects on mathematics achievement and student attitudes (Li et al., 2024).
Technological integration is becoming increasingly crucial in mathematics education. Digital tools and software, such as dynamic geometry environments and computer algebra systems, have been shown to
enhance students ’   understanding of abstract mathematical concepts (Susanto et al., 2023). Online learn- ing platforms and adaptive learning systems offer personalized learning experiences, allowing students
to progress at their own pace and receive targeted feedback (Fern  andez-Herrero, 2024).
Curriculum design and development have also been focal points for improvement. There is a growing emphasis on curricula that focus on real-world applications of mathematics, helping students see the
relevance of mathematical concepts in their lives (Da, 2023). Interdisciplinary approaches that integrate mathematics with other subjects have shown promise in enhancing student engagement and under-
standing (Zhan & Niu, 2023).

relevant and meaningful to students, thereby increasing their motivation and engagement in the sub- ject. Additionally, PjBL fosters the development of critical thinking and analytical skills, as students must

approach leads to a deeper, longer-lasting understanding than traditional instructional methods (Walker & Leary, 2023). Moreover, PjBL has been associated with more positive attitudes towards mathematics
among students, potentially reducing math anxiety and increasing students ’   confidence in their mathem- atical abilities (Chen & Yang, 2019). In higher education settings, PjBL has proven to be especially valu-
able in helping students connect theoretical mathematical concepts with practical applications in fields such as engineering and science (Krajcik et al., 2023). This integration not only enhances students ’   math-
ematical proficiency, but also their overall competence in their chosen fields of study. Furthermore, in mathematics   education,   PjBL   has   demonstrated   the   ability   to   develop ’   higher-order   thinking   skills,
including analysis, synthesis, and evaluation. These skills are essential for advanced mathematical reason- ing and problem solving, and their development through PjBL prepares students for more complex
mathematical challenges they may encounter in future academic pursuits or professional careers. The effectiveness of PjBL in mathematics education is not limited to academic achievement; it also cultivates
important 21st-century skills such as creativity, innovation, and adaptability, which are increasingly val- ued in the modern workforce (Chen & Yang, 2019; Zhang & Ma, 2023).

3. Research methodology
3.1. Research design Quasi-experimental studies are research designs that aim to evaluate causal relationships without ran-
dom assignment of participants to treatment and control groups (Cook & Campbell, 2007). In this study, we employed a   non-equivalent group   design, a   common type of quasi-experiment in   educational
research (Reichardt, 2019). This design involves comparing outcomes between naturally occurring groups exposed to different conditions, typically including pre- and post-tests (Reichardt, 2009). We chose this
approach because of the practical and ethical constraints in university settings (Gribbons & Herman, 2019). The non-equivalent group design allowed us to assess the impact of project-based learning while
respecting existing class structures. This design suits educational contexts in which complete randomiza- tion is unfeasible (Schneider, 2007). Using pre-test scores and multilevel modeling, we accounted for ini-
tial group differences and nested data structures, enhancing causal inference validity in a naturalistic environment (Raudenbush & Bryk, 2002).

### 3.2. Validity and reliability
The assessment instruments demonstrated strong psychometric properties. The pre-course assessment and final exam were developed and validated by experienced mathematics professors at both participat-
ing universities, ensuring robust content validity and alignment with course objectives. The assessment tools showed high internal consistency reliability with a Cronbach ’ s alpha of 0.89 for the pre-test and
0.92 for the post-test. A panel of six subject matter experts evaluated the content validity using a struc- tured review process, yielding a Content Validity Index (CVI) of 0.87, well above the recommended
threshold of 0.78. Construct validity was supported through factor analysis, with all items loading appro- priately on their intended constructs (factor loadings ranging from 0.72 to 0.88). Additionally, criterion-
related validity was established through significant correlations ( r   ¼   0.76,   p   <   0.001) between test scores and students ’   previous mathematics performance. The inter-rater reliability for the project evaluation
components was also strong, with an intraclass correlation coefficient (ICC) of 0.85, indicating consistent scoring across different evaluators.

### 3.4. Procedures
In this study, we adopted the Gold Standard PjBL model as recommended by Larmer et al. (2015) to design and implement our intervention in Calculus I courses. This comprehensive framework ensures a
high-quality PjBL experience that enhances student engagement and learning outcomes. The gold- standard PjBL model comprises seven essential design elements, each of which plays a crucial role in
creating an effective learning environment. These elements are: (1) A Challenging Problem or Question, which serves as the heart of the project and drives student learning; (2) Sustained Inquiry, involving an
active, in-depth process of asking questions, finding resources, and applying information; (3) authenticity, ensuring that the project involves real-world context, tasks, tools, quality standards, or impact; (4)
Student Voice and Choice, allowing students to make decisions about the project, fostering ownership and engagement; (5) reflection, encouraging ongoing thought about the learning process and its appli-
cations; (6) Critique and Revision, emphasizing the importance of feedback and product improvement; and (7) Public Product, where students make their project work public by sharing it with people beyond
the classroom.
The PjBL intervention designed for Calculus I TG was implemented during two academic years: 2022 – 2023 and 2023 – 2024 at both universities. Following the Gold Standard PjBL model (Larmer et al., 2015),
the intervention centered on a challenging optimization problem that served as the core of student learning, with each component systematically implemented across both institutions.
The core of our implementation is a precisely defined, challenging problem. Students worked in teams to optimize the dimensions of a cylindrical container to achieve the maximum volume with the
minimum surface area. The project specifications required container volumes between 250 and 1000 ml, with material costs set at $0.05 per square centimeter of surface area. Teams worked within a maximum
budget of $25 per container and maintained height-to-radius ratios between 2:1 and 4:1.
The sustained inquiry process spanned 15 weeks and was divided into four distinct phases. The first three weeks focused on mathematical modeling using calculus concepts, particularly derivatives and
optimization. Weeks four-seven were dedicated to developing volume and surface area functions. The next four weeks involved the application of optimization techniques, followed by a final phase of testing
solutions against constraints. Throughout this process, students submitted weekly progress reports of two to three pages, detailing their calculations, assumptions, and revisions.
To ensure authenticity, the teams worked with manufacturing constraints throughout the project.
They utilized real material specifications from local container manufacturers and applied industry stand- ard safety margins with ±2% tolerance. Teams incorporated practical manufacturing capabilities into
their designs and calculated production costs using current market prices.
Students ’   voices and choices were facilitated through a structured team formation process. Teams of three to four students were created based on balanced pre-test scores, with students ranking their pre-
ferred roles as mathematical modelers, cost analysts, and design specialists. Each team selected a specific container type from the options including beverages, food storage, or chemical storage containers.
Teams established their own weekly meeting schedules, with a minimum requirement of 2 h per week.
The reflection component is implemented through multiple structured activities. Students maintained individual weekly learning logs of at least 500 words and participated in bi-weekly team reflection ses-
sions lasting 30 min each. Monthly progress presentations of 15 min per team provided opportunities for broader reflection, and the process culminated in a 2000-word final reflection paper analyzing their
learning journey.

### 3.5. Data analysis
This study employed a multilevel modeling (MLM) approach to analyze the effects of a project-based learning intervention on students ’   calculus problem-solving proficiency. MLM, also known as hierarchical
linear modeling (HLM) or mixed-effects modeling, is a statistical method designed to handle data with nested or hierarchical structures, in which observations are grouped at different levels (Raudenbush &
Bryk, 2002; Snijders & Bosker, 2011). The selection of the MLM for this study is justified by the inherent hierarchical nature of educational data, where students are nested within groups, which, in turn, are
nested within cohorts.
The use of the MLM offers several advantages over traditional single-level regression techniques. First, it accounts for the non-independence of observations within clusters, which, if ignored, can lead to
biased standard errors and increased type I error rates (Hox et al., 2017). Second, MLM allows for the simultaneous examination of effects at multiple levels, enabling researchers to disentangle the impact of
individual, group, and higher-level factors on the outcome of interest (Woltman et al., 2012). This is par- ticularly crucial in educational research, where outcomes are often influenced by factors at various levels
of the educational system.
To test the differences between the TG and CG, we employed a multilevel model. This model was designed to account for the nested structure of our data while allowing us to examine the effects of the
intervention. In our analysis, group membership (TG versus CG) was the primary predictor of interest.
We included pre-test Score, Cohort, and University as fixed effects in the model. This approach allowed us to assess the impact of the PjBL intervention on post-test scores while accounting for initial perform-
ance levels and potential differences across cohorts and universities. By incorporating these factors as

5. Findings and discussion
The findings of this study provide compelling evidence for the effectiveness of project-based learning (PjBL) in enhancing students ’   mathematical proficiency in Kazakhstan ’ s higher education context while
also contributing to the broader international discourse on innovative pedagogical approaches. The sub- stantial improvement observed in the Target Group aligns with both local and international research on
active learning methods in mathematics. Within Kazakhstan ’ s educational landscape, these findings extend Tursynkulova et al. ’ s (2023) work on problem-based learning, which demonstrates enhanced ana-
lytical skills and conceptual retention among mathematics students. Although Tursynkulova et al. (2023) focused on geometric construction problems, our findings suggest that PjBL ’ s effectiveness extends to
calculus education, indicating broader applicability across mathematical domains in Kazakhstan ’ s educa- tional system.
The multilevel model results, indicating a 9.15-point increase associated with the PjBL intervention, corroborate Barak and Asad ’ s (2012) findings on improved problem-solving skills, while addressing spe-
cific challenges identified in Kazakhstan ’ s mathematics education system. Sapazhanov et al. (2020) high- lighted various factors affecting mathematics achievement in Kazakhstan ’ s specialized universities, and
our   findings   provide   a   concrete   pedagogical   solution   for   these   challenges.   The   large   effect   sizes
( d   ¼   0.85   overall)   not   only   exceeded   typical   educational   interventions   (Hattie,   2023)   but   also

The consistency of these large effect sizes across cohorts ( d   ¼   0.82 and   d   ¼   0.89) strengthens the valid- ity of our findings and addresses concerns raised by Kosybaeva et al. (2017) regarding the need for

trajectories in Kazakhstan ’ s mathematics education. The project-based approach, which requires students to apply mathematical concepts to real-world optimization problems, addresses the need for practical
application skills identified in both international literature (Blumenfeld et al., 1991) and Kazakhstan ’ s edu- cational objectives (Burkhalter & Shegebayev, 2012).
The   results   of   this   study   have   significant   implications   for   mathematics   education   reform   in Kazakhstan. Aligning with Kazakhstan ’ s strategic educational goals (OECD 2014), our findings provide
empirical support for integrating PjBL into mathematics curricula. The success of PjBL in enhancing both mathematical proficiency and student engagement addresses concerns about declining interest in STEM
subjects (Potvin & Hasni, 2014) while supporting Kazakhstan ’ s ambitions for economic diversification and innovation (Koshim et al., 2018).
Moreover, the collaborative nature of our PjBL intervention aligns with international trends in work- force preparation (Berdibekov et al., 2020) and Kazakhstan ’ s educational modernization efforts. By engag-
ing students in group projects that require the application of mathematics to practical problems, this approach develops both technical expertise and interpersonal skills, addressing employer demands in
Kazakhstan ’ s evolving job market (Berdibekov et al., 2020).
Although our findings demonstrate the strong positive effects of PjBL, several factors affect their gen- eralizability. The significant disparity in representation between South Kazakhstan University (88%) and
Peoples ’   Friendship University (12%), combined with the marginally significant university effect (coeffi- cient   ¼   – 2.31, SE   ¼   1.18,   p   ¼   0.051), suggests that our results may better reflect experiences at larger
institutions. Additionally, our quasi-experimental design and the slight imbalance between the target (44.9%) and control (55.1%) groups may have introduced selection biases. The specific cultural and edu-
cational context of Kazakhstan, along with the resource requirements for PjBL implementation, including appropriate facilities and faculty training, means that outcomes may vary in different educational sys-
tems or resource environments.
6. Conclusion
This study tested the hypothesis that PjBL has a significantly positive effect on calculus problem-solving proficiency among university students in Kazakhstan. Based on our findings, we conclude that this
hypothesis is supported.
The results of our quasi-experimental study provided strong evidence to support this hypothesis. The multilevel model analysis revealed a significant positive effect of the PjBL intervention on students ’   cal-
culus problem-solving proficiency. This effect was consistent across the two academic cohorts with large effect sizes. These findings indicate that PjBL not only improves students ’   performance statistically but
also has a substantial practical impact on their mathematical skills.
The consistency of these results across different cohorts and universities strengthens our confidence in the effectiveness of PjBL as an intervention in Kazakhstani higher education. Students who partici-
pated in the PjBL intervention demonstrated significantly higher post-test scores than those in the con- trol group, even after accounting for pre-test performance, cohort differences, and university effects.
Although our study provides robust support for the efficacy of PjBL in enhancing calculus problem- solving proficiency, it also revealed areas for further investigation. Future research could explore the
