provide an adequate background. Considering this, the first reference dates back to the 17th century, when in 1613, the term ‘didactics’ was introduced by the German educa -

Jan Amos Komensky with his work “The Great Didactics” [ 2 ]. Didactics will be better

There is vast amount of literature available about education. When it comes to adult education or helping adults to learn, the term ‘andragogy’ comes to place. It was origi -
nally introduced with a publication in 1833, under the title “ Platons Erziehungslehre ,   als Pädagogik für die Einzelnen und als Staatspädagogik. Oder dessen praktische Philoso -
phie ” (in English: Plato’s theory of education, as pedagogy for individuals and as state pedagogy. Or his practical philosophy), by the German educator Alexander Kapp [ 3 ].
He presented andragogy as an educational stage after pedagogy, and also published in 1837 the article “ Aristoteles´ Staatspädagogik ,   als Erziehungslehre für den Staat und die
Einzelnen ” (in English: Aristotle’s state pedagogy, as an educational theory for the state and individuals), alluding the aim of education as derived from the purpose of the state;
overall, in view of the andragogic and pedagogic dimensions between politics and edu -
### cation [ 4 ].
The 19th century brought the establishment of various programs for adults, while andragogy and adult education were used synonymously. However, the use of the term
‘andragogy’ became more popular internationally during the 20th century. The Ger - man sociologist Eugen Rosenstock reintroduced it in 1924, claiming that it was “insuf -
ficient to translate pedagogic concepts into an adult situation” and encouraging the use of a separate method and philosophy for adult education. Soon after, adult education
### increased as a field of study and practice [ 5 ].
A common interest on improving the education of humanity has been in focus for a long time. It is mentioned, for instance, in the United Nations Educational, Scientific
and Cultural Organization’s (UNESCO) Constitution signed in 1945, and later, in 1947, adult education was already part of the program in the UNESCO’s General Conference
in Mexico [ 6 ]. In the 1950s and 1960s, adult education became more important with the decolonization of many lands worldwide. Now, in the 21st century, the quality of adult
learning and education (ALE) is still in focus: fact evidenced with the implementation of
### the Belém Framework for Action (BFA) which was adopted by 144 UNESCO member
states in 2009 with the commitment of following up and monitoring ALE. The continu - ous development of quality in adult education has allowed, for example, improvements
in curricula in 77% of the countries, assessment in 75% of the countries and analysis of
### learning outcomes in 68% of the countries since 2018 [ 7 ].

value of their offers. As opposed to general perception, the curriculum is not limited to the academic content taught, but includes learning standards, objectives, instructional
techniques, projects, assignments, presentations, assessment methods, literature, mul - timedia [ 8 ], serving as an organizer of learning. Moreover, in this 3rd decade of the 21st
century, it is impacted in great measure by how the information and communication technologies (ICT) have developed and are being adopted [ 9 ], such as the accessibility to
new research and innovation through the Internet, the usage of artificial intelligence [ 10 ] and how common the live interaction and synchronous exchange of information have
### become.
Designing a curriculum starts at the macro level (macro-curriculum), and is grounded on national plans and policies, local regulations, research and international trends.
Although this sounds like the responsibility of a Ministry of Education, there may be different governing organizations depending on the country or state law. The second
level of curricular specification is the meso-curriculum, where the academic program is designed and planned under context-specific considerations: field or discipline, the
graduate profile, the professional to be trained, the requirements of the auditing and accreditation bodies, and the market demands among others. The third level is the
micro-curriculum [ 11 ], where classroom intervention is managed in its three stages: start, development and closure, including objectives, content, learning strategies and
didactic activities, resources, evaluation and bibliography [ 12 ]. Each level of the curricu -
### lum is designed in articulation with its higher level [ 13 ].
Here is where the term ‘didactics’ fits more appropriately. The professor, at the micro- curriculum level, should provide the student with the relevant tools to stimulate and
develop metacognitive skills for learning. Since there is no unique teaching method that can guarantee an optimum learning for all the students, then planning and adapt -
ing didactics models and strategies is necessary. Certain factors such as the physical and psychological characteristics of the students, interpersonal relationships of the group,
and the relation between the professor and the group, can have an impact on the learn - ing outcomes [ 14 ]; so it is crucial that the professor counts with autonomy, prepared -
ness and will to adapt the activities to the changing context and sustain higher education
### quality [ 8 ].
From a global perspective, most countries are keeping a common focus until the end of this decade on 17 Sustainable Developments Goals (SDG) with their respective tar -
gets agreed as a basis to the 2030 Agenda [ 15 ]. It is recognized that “ higher education is an essential and integral part of the SDG 4 Education 2030 agenda ”, along with sus -
tainability and cooperation which are two of the promoted principles to reinvent higher education and address the global challenges [ 16 ]. This research project corresponds to
the SDG 4 – Quality Education, on the targets 4.4 and 4.7, by fostering on: the relevant skills of adults for employment, decent jobs and entrepreneurship, as well as the knowl -
edge and continuous development of the curriculum, professor education and student
### assessment.

### 3   Methods
This research has a mixed-methods sequential design with two phases. The first phase is exploratory: it involves participant observation in courses of the Master’s program in
Higher Education and categorization of qualitative data on student satisfaction to then test the association with variables from sentiment analysis as a trigger to the subsequent

---

Lasso-Rodríguez and Melgar-Bieberach   Discover Education   (2025) 4:527 Page 7 of 22 research phase. In qualitative research, participant observation is used to collect data
about people in real-world settings whilst being part of the scene observed, recording what they do, rather than what they profess to do [ 24 ], and it may involve informal con -
versations [ 25 ]; it enables the researcher to learn about the people, processes and cul - ture under study through involvement in day-to-day natural activities over an extended
period of time to develop a narrative explaining various aspects [ 26 ].
Initial learning from the first phase makes it necessary to explore further with a differ - ent data source and technique to validate reliability and the consistency of preliminary
findings. Artificial intelligence is used thereafter, specifically sentiment analysis, to reach findings that allow to proceed with a broader exercise in the institution.
The second phase of the research is descriptive: it mainly takes a quantitative, non- experimental, and cross-sectional or transversal approach to collect and process quan -
titative data from a survey with Likert scales, although also including qualitative data from open questions. At this point, the scope increases to include all the students from
undergraduate and postgraduate programs of ULATINA at national level in 2024. A study is descriptive when it seeks to specify important properties, characteristics and
profiles of people, groups, processes or phenomena under analysis, pretending to collect or measure data in an independent or joint manner about concepts or variables, and its
objective is not to indicate how these are related, nor to explain the reason of the find - ings [ 27 ].
A mixed-methods approach can be used when the phenomenon or problem is from a complex nature, meaning that a single approach like quantitative or qualitative would
not appropriately lead to an answer. So, one method complements the findings from the other. The project comprises an objective part with physical elements, like: university,
number of students, professors and administrative personnel, but also a subjective part with varied realities, like: student perceptions, interactions, experiences, sentiments and
emotions, all leading to an intersubjective reality. In addition, the design of the research is sequential, because the first component or phase needs to be executed first and, if
applicable, allow the second phase to start with the other method [ 27 ].

### 6   Discussion
There is no point of reference available or numbers from another institution in a simi - lar context to be able to compare the results. In spite of that, the results are useful for
the university to compare the Likert scores between questions. Further analyses would reveal, for example, that the most significant difference was 0.597 points between q08
(1.910 pts) and q09 (2.507 pts), giving a higher relevance to q08 when compared to the rest. Such gap is followed by a difference of 0.53 points between q11 (2.828 pts) and q15
(3.358 pts), separating the ULATINA scores below (4 indicators) and over (9 indicators) the Likert scale average of 3 pts.
Even though this research takes Panama and ULATINA as the context of study, the mixed-methods sequential design applied is of a generic nature. It means that a simi -
lar approach can be followed for any other country and university to discover oppor - tunities to improve quality at the micro-curriculum level. A key enabler to transfer this
approach to other institutions is the support offered by the students, professors and HEI personnel; there are cultural and statutory factors that may hinder the project, such as
unwillingness to take part in the study, legal constraints, or a prolonged paperwork for approvals. In addition, depending on the characteristics of the specific context (e.g.,
means of communication, language, data restrictions), different data collection and pro - cessing software tools or instruments may be more suitable.
The micro-curricular planning includes the organization of activities to be performed throughout an academic course. It is the responsibility of the professor to pay attention
to students’ concerns and establish teaching strategies and methodologies to resolve those issues during classes [ 34 ]. This can only be achieved when the feedback from stu -
dents is received and, as came to light in the results section, most of the points identi - fied for improvement at ULATINA are related to simplifying the communication from
students.
While the curriculum design, documentation and challenges may not be exactly the same in all countries, the work sessions performed by the individual educator in class
are considered part of the micro level. Such micro design can be influenced through co- planning interventions from students [ 35 ]. The professor should consider adapting the
planned activities based on students’ knowledge and suggestions. In the case of ULA - TINA, this statement relates directly to the fourth key action identified for improve -
ment: adapting the Moodle learning modules’ evaluation form to improve its usefulness.
Having a course evaluation form available on a weekly basis, which has been the case for the learning modules at ULATINA, represents an appreciable frequency to support
the micro-curriculum didactics. To leverage this instrument, as learned during the proj - ect, it is convenient to guarantee the anonymity of participants and count with pertinent
questions in order to receive transparent and opportune feedback.
A curricular evaluation investigates how the curriculum has been implemented, its achievements, deficiencies and problems, as well as the perception of managers, admin -
istrative personnel, professors, students, graduates and employers among other aspects.
This evaluation has to be assumed as a systematic and feedback-based activity that con - tributes to the continuous improvement of the curriculum, considering the quality of the
andragogic process developed.
Furthermore, andragogy not only requires relevant theory and practice of teaching, but specially trained adult education personnel [ 36 ]. This has been a key strength of

---

Lasso-Rodríguez and Melgar-Bieberach   Discover Education   (2025) 4:527 Page 17 of 22 ULATINA according to the results, where, as indicated by students, the professors stand
out in multiple aspects positively. These results are likely partly attributed to the institu - tional requirement that faculty members have a postgraduate Specialization in Higher
Education. Educators benefit from mastering instructional methods because this way they can ensure that they are using appropriate techniques to teach adult learners effec -
tively [ 37 ].
In andragogy—even if applying pedagogic practices, heutagogy, or a blend—it is not surprising to receive varying and unexpected feedback, since each student is relatively
mature, counts with its own experiences and developed believes. The fact that critical thinking is a common skill reinforced in higher education, also promotes questioning of
the   status quo   and constructive criticism, which are ideal for continuous improvement.
Closely monitoring the sentiment of students in a classroom allows to act early, having a higher chance to control a situation that may run out of hand; however, the means to
grab such sentiment may lead to data privacy and legal implications if the information is not handled with confidentiality. A mood state, good or bad, might have originated in a
previous course, gradually scale in the current course, and even endure to the next one.
Knowledge in psychology is beneficial for a professor to manage distinct scenarios and individuals. The mood of students affects their cognition and behavior, it is not inten -
tional but dependent on the environment [ 38 ].
### 7   Conclusion
This study has relied on more than one method and technological means to find out, through sequential phases, the measures that a university can take to improve the qual -
ity of the curriculum evaluation process of its undergraduate and postgraduate pro - grams. The focus has been on the micro-curriculum, recognizing the agility of this
curricular level, for example, to continuously adapt the classroom didactics to the actual environment.
In the specific case of ULATINA in 2024, it was discovered that its professors are the major strength, due to the quality of knowledge facilitation, the evaluation tools and
instruments they use, as well as their accessibility and interest in the development of students. On the other hand, the students believe that the key points to be strengthen
comprise the communication about mechanisms offered to suggest improvements, will - ingness of students to share feedback, accessibility of the administrative personnel and
the usefulness of the current course evaluation form. It is confirmed that a more trans - parent and opportune feedback can be achieved by adapting the evaluation instruments
employed in the curriculum evaluation process.
The numbers obtained in this research triggered by AI give an intriguing indication about how the university is performing and which are its key opportunities for improve -
ment. The resulting datasets can be leveraged by the institution to perform deeper anal - yses towards focus areas (Online Material 2). In addition, the items measured serve as
indicators to be compared in future periods for tracking the outcome of actions taken to improve.
While in this case the professors turned out to be the strength of the university, other HEIs may require measures directed to improve the performance of professors in the
classroom, the instruments they use or other aspects. An institution may already count with multiple mechanisms to facilitate the interaction with students; however, a standard

---

Lasso-Rodríguez and Melgar-Bieberach   Discover Education   (2025) 4:527 Page 18 of 22 process benefits from being simple, transparent, monitored actively, and counting with
anonymous and quick communication alternatives considering the criticality. Receiving bottom-up evaluation and feedback to learn from other perspectives is valuable for any
organization.
The teaching-learning strategies, for instance, analyzing and discussing business cases in teams, gamifying some course content, organizing information visually with diagrams
like concept maps, or running projects based on newly acquired knowledge, hold an important part in the micro-curriculum; nevertheless, these can also be impacted by the
institutional system as a whole, which includes the administrative personnel and related student services.
### 8   Limitations
It was not possible to include or comment in this article about the feedback on open- ended questions due to the sensitivity and confidentiality of the topics mentioned within
the respective answers. Despite the validity of the chosen models to perform the NLP analyses, there is always an inherent level of subjectivity when using AI, which is influ -
enced by the design as well as cultural biases on data chosen by humans. It could also be alleged that a bigger sample including students that did not volunteer to participate
would have returned different results.
### 9   Directions for future research
More research would be advantageous regarding other aspects to improve the micro- curriculum, additional to transparency and timeliness in the evaluation process. Getting
to know how students from other universities assess the quality of their institutions, as well as the resulting actions to improve and how they progress, would also be valuable.
