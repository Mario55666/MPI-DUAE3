Capstone design projects and cornerstone design courses are deliberately crafted curricular spaces aiming   to   foster   these   processes   of   experiential   learning   of   engineering   design   skills   (Fletcher   &
Harrington, 2018; Meah et al., 2020). However, curricular scenarios are not the only spaces where active design learning can take place. Co-curricular or extra-curricular settings offer supplementary avenues for

Bowie & Cassim, 2016; Tsang et al., 2001). Additionally, a variety of challenge-based and competitive design activities, emerging from these extra-curricular contexts, have also gained attention for their
potential to engage students in real-world problem-solving scenarios (Van den Beemt et al., 2023).
Among these, hackathons have become particularly prominent as intensive, collaborative environments where participants design innovative solutions under time constraints.
Hackathons are competitions where participants work in teams to design and build prototypes of sol- utions to solve challenges provided by companies or organizations, embracing the principles of open
innovation. Through their proposals, the groups strive to meet the needs and interests of business or social stakeholders by fostering collaboration and innovative thinking. Competitors typically work 24 to
48 hours to create their solutions, mainly developing software prototypes, but they may also incorporate hardware or physical elements. During a hackathon, contestants also get the opportunity to attend
workshops and interact with technology and application domain experts and other participants.
During the decade of 2010-2019, the phenomenon of the hackathons grew. One evidence of this growth was the fact that the Major League of Hacking, the principal coordinating organization of these
events in North America, facilitated 229 hackathons in 2022 (MLH, 2022). Most of these competitions were organized by universities. These extra-curricular activities bring together more and more univer-
sities, students, and companies with the purpose of creating prototypes of computing or engineering solutions.
Participation in a hackathon presents students with a unique opportunity to tackle business or com- munity challenges within a condensed timeframe. It also offers a platform to engage with technology
experts across various domains, collaborating in teams to build prototypes that require the application of design skills. Given these traits, hackathons have become valuable environments for experiential
learning in engineering. This article delves into the hackathon phenomenon and its meaning in engin- eering education, focusing specifically on how these events can foster the development of engineering
design skills.
2. Background and related work
In recent years, universities around the world have shown a growing interest in organizing hackathons, which has led to a corresponding rise in research exploring their educational potential. Early investiga-
tions have begun to highlight the unique learning opportunities that hackathons offer. These analyses have   typically   applied   two   educational   frameworks:   Problem-Based   Learning   (PBL)   and   Experiential
Learning.
From the perspective of Problem-based Learning (PBL), these events foster the acquisition of skills that enable students to assess the complexity of problems, identify multiple strategies for understanding
information, and evaluate their progress in acquiring knowledge and solving problems (Christie & de Graaff, 2017; Szymanska et al., 2020; Wallwey et al., 2022). Kolmos et al. (2024) recently expanded this
perspective to highlight the need for interdisciplinarity in PBL settings, specifically within what they call mixed micro-projects, which are projects in the style of hackathons that motivate students to transcend
their individual disciplines to respond to the overall project objectives.
While Problem-Based Learning offers valuable insights into the educational potential of hackathons, it is the framework of Experiential Learning that resonates more closely with our focus on engineering
design competencies. This approach emphasizes the crucial connection between real-world interaction and the development of practical skills, making it particularly relevant when examining how hackathons
can foster engineering design abilities. In recent years, several studies have explored hackathons through

In any experiential learning activity, learners are directly involved and interact with the phenomenon under study (Abdulwahed & Nagy, 2009; Kolb & Kolb, 2005; Yardley et al., 2012). However, experience
alone does not guarantee learning; it must be accompanied by personal reflection that enables individu- als to derive meaning from their lived experiences. This reflection is where interaction with real-world
contexts has the potential to cultivate new knowledge and skills (Raelin, 1998). By incorporating these experiential aspects, learning environments can lead to deeper, more comprehensive, and long-lasting
learning outcomes. As Raelin (1998) points out, deep cognitive processes are activated when students tackle complex, multidisciplinary problems that require innovative solutions.
The experiential learning perspective offers a relevant framework for studying hackathons as settings for the development of engineering design skills. However, before reviewing some recent related stud-
ies, it is necessary to establish our conceptual framework on engineering design and competency-based education in this field.
In a seminal paper, Dym et al. (2005) define Engineering Design as follows: Engineering design is a systematic, intelligent process in which designers generate, evaluate, and specify
concepts for devices, systems, or processes whose form and function achieve clients ’   objectives or users ’ needs while satisfying a specified set of constraints. (Dym et al., 2005, p. 104, added emphasis).
The terms highlighted in the definition above make it clear that in engineering design, the context has a significant role to play. To effectively develop engineering design skills, students must engage in
activities that closely resemble real-world experiences. This need aligns directly with the principles of experiential learning, where interaction with authentic contexts is fundamental for deeper learning and
skill development.
ABET reinforces the importance of engineering design through its guidelines for accrediting engineer- ing programs (ABET, 2023). These guidelines emphasize the development of key design skills in their
definition of student outcomes. Specifically, student outcome number 2 highlights the necessity for stu- dents to acquire the ability to design solutions that meet specified needs while considering real-world
constraints.
While ABET ’ s guidelines emphasize the importance of engineering design skills, these are typically implemented within structured, curricular settings rather than extracurricular activities like hackathons.
Two of the most widely adopted curricular formats for developing these skills are capstone design proj- ects and cornerstone design courses (Dym et al., 2005). These scenarios are common practice not only
in North America but also in engineering programs around the world, providing students with formal opportunities to engage in real-world design challenges within their academic programs.
Despite the existence of these curricular spaces, the   Engineer 2020   project progress report concludes that the amount of experiential learning present in engineering courses varies widely (Lattuca et al.,
2010). Moreover, tenured faculty often do not include strong involvement with   ‘ real clients ’   in their classes. On the other hand, a higher level of student contact with real-world constraints and needs
occurs in other co-curricular and extra-curricular contexts (Lattuca et al., 2010). This happens in formats such as community projects, internships, and other service-learning experiences (Bielefeldt et al., 2010;
Castro et al., 2020; Huff et al., 2016; Tsang et al., 2001), student clubs, and design competitions. Among these, hackathons are specifically recognized as a prominent form of design competition in the   Engineer
2020   report.
At this point, with the use of experiential learning and engineering design frameworks, it becomes clearer that hackathons can be viewed as educational settings where participants ’   design skills are devel-
oped through experiences that closely resemble real-world scenarios. Therefore, it is relevant to focus on the learning processes activated in participants during these events. To this end, we will examine signifi-
cant insights from recent research on this topic.

‘ educational effectiveness ’ , and that one contributing factor may be the   ‘ artificial pressure ’   environment, which encourages participants to push their limits and expand their capabilities (Duhring, 2014). This

Building on this call for deeper research, the project by Byrne et al. (2017) conducted measurements among participants in an IoT Hackathon. The aim was to assess how such activities could influence

A handful of recent studies examined learning processes in hackathons with university students.
Horton et al. (2018) goes beyond Byrne by incorporating a structured framework — specifically Project- Based Learning (PBL) — to better assess the educational value of hackathons. Horton used an interview
protocol with three teams at a university hackathon, comparing its structure to PBL standards. The study found that most PBL components   ‘ make an explicit or implicit appearance in the hackathon setting ’ .
Notably, Horton emphasizes that   ‘ hackathons should continue to be analyzed in a more structured man- ner ’ , and suggests future studies could focus on   ‘ generating hackathons with specific learning goals ’ .
Although PBL includes design-related elements, this research did not directly explore the development of design skills, highlighting an opportunity for further investigation.
Place et al. (2017) focused their study on the self-regulated learning process of participants. Based on interviews   during   a   Major   League   Hacking   (MLH)   event   and   applying   Winne ’ s   model   ‘ Improving
Measurements of Self-Regulated Learning ’ , they identified a key factor in the educational effectiveness of the hackathon: participants   ‘ came to the hackathon to act upon their desire to learn ’ . This attitude
greatly influences the speed of the learning process. They suggest further validation is needed to deter- mine whether these findings can be applied to engineering courses.
Gama et al. (2018) continue the investigation into the educational use of hackathons, this time in an event like a hackathon but conducted within an IoT course. While the study does not directly analyze
the results through the lens of Experiential Learning, it introduces this framework as a relevant approach for understanding the educational potential of hackathons. The experience did not include a predefined
challenge or an external group of actors, such as mentors or judges, as is typical in a proper hackathon.
Data were collected from 22 participating students through surveys and individual interviews. The results emphasized the value of   ‘ learn-by-doing ’ , frequently mentioned by students, and the strong cor-
relation between participation in hackathons and increased motivation, engagement, and enjoyment.
Students   ‘ tended to agree that they learned more in the hackathon and practical classes than in theoret- ical ones ’ . However, the study did not delve into these new learnings or whether they relate to the
development of skills among participants. Additionally, the paper highlighted the impact of the short time frame, which pressured students to quickly   ‘ deliver intermediate milestones ’ .
Uys (2019) conducted a similar experience to that of Gama et al., involving a hackathon-like activity within a course. However, the conclusions are limited due to the lack of interview or survey data from
participants, relying instead on the professor ’ s observation of the experience. The author agrees with Gama that students found   ‘ the hackathon was an extremely valuable activity that prepares them for the
demands of the working environment ’ . Buchner ’ s (2017) working paper also supports this educational aspect, based on the author ’ s observation and reflection from a specific hackathon.
Finally, Kang et al. (2022) conducted a scoping review of 45 articles (2000-2021) to provide an over- view of how experiential learning is used in Human-Computer Interaction (HCI) education, identifying
design hackathons as one of 12 types employed, and reporting on student outcomes such as enhanced UX knowledge, applied skills, and increased job marketability.
In summary, recent research on hackathons has revealed their educational impact, highlighting the need to better understand the learning process experienced by participants. Authors such as Duhring
(2014) and Byrne et al. (2017) emphasized the need for studies conducted directly within university hackathons. Horton et al. (2018) then suggested the importance of structured studies using frameworks
like Project-Based Learning (PBL) or Experiential Learning to better understand the rapid learning that occurs during these events, driven by participants ’   desire for self-learning, as noted by Place et al.
(2017).   Lattuca   et   al.   (2010)   had   previously   stressed   the   need   to   increase   experiential   learning   in

This paper addresses key gaps in the educational study of hackathons. While Gama et al. (2018) made an initial attempt to apply Experiential Learning, their study was limited to a hackathon-like event in a class-
room setting. There remains a need to explore real hackathons, where the added elements of real-world contact, pressure, and immersion may offer new insights into student learning. Additionally, as Byrne et al.
(2017) pointed out, there is little research focused on skill development, and no previous research has spe- cifically addressed the learning of design skills in engineering students. Investigating this in a hackathon
context could deepen understanding of the development of design skills, which are so crucial in engineer- ing education, and provide insights for enhancing these processes within curricular environments.
3. Research design
3.1. Goals Our objective was to explore how hackathons, as intensive, time-bound collaborative environments, con- tribute to the development of essential engineering design skills. We aimed to understand the specific
elements of hackathons that facilitate rapid skill acquisition.
The following research questions guided our investigation:
1.   Do participants develop engineering design skills during a Hackathon?
2.   What factors drive the rapid learning of skills in a Hackathon, according to the participating
students?
In addition to the research findings obtained for these questions, we will reflect on how the insights gained could be applied in pedagogical practices within engineering education. Specifically, we will aim
to establish the relationship between hackathons and the curricular settings in which engineering design skills are taught.
3.2. Methodology Data   was   collected   at   an   annual   Hackathon,   which   took   place   at   the   School   of   Engineering   at
Universidad de La Sabana in Colombia. It was a nationwide event with 127 undergraduate students from 16 universities competing. There were 96 male students and 31 female students between the ages
of 18 and 22. Of these, 98 were engineering students. The event ’ s scale and the variety of universities involved provided an opportunity to observe a diverse range of experiences and outcomes, allowing us
to gather insights that reflect typical hackathon dynamics.
The students were organized into 24 teams, each consisting of 4 to 5 members, with engineering stu- dents present in every team. Teams worked on challenges provided by one non-profit organization and
three multinationals known as the sponsors. These challenges were revealed to the participants only at the beginning of the Hackathon. So, the students were not familiar with them beforehand. The partici-
pants signed up with their teams before the event. They had to pass through a screening process by the university organizers. It was a rigorous selection, where only 24 teams were selected from a larger
number of registered participants.
Another essential role during the event were the mentors. They were professionals assigned by the companies and the organizing university, specialized in particular technologies and with a clear under-
standing of the needs of the context. Their function was to keep the teams focused on the challenges and to solve doubts about tools and platforms being used. This mentorship was designed to simulate
real-world engineering scenarios where professionals often rely on collaboration and expert guidance to overcome challenges. The interaction with mentors not only aided in the immediate task completion
but also contributed significantly to the students ’   learning process by exposing them to industry practi- ces and professional standards.

### 3.3. Data collection
The data collection was carried out through a survey using the Forms service of Microsoft Office 365.
One week after the Hackathon, a link to the survey was sent to all 127 participating students. Over the following two weeks, reminder emails were sent to encourage completion. Participation in the survey
was voluntary, and by the end of the period, 22 undergraduate students had filled out the survey, repre- senting 17.3% of the total event participants.
Among the 22 students who completed the survey, 81.8% (18 individuals) were engineering students, while the rest were from Business Administration (3) and Psychology (1). Given that one of the research
questions focused on identifying factors influencing rapid learning in a hackathon, all completed surveys were included in the analysis, regardless of the respondents ’   field of study. This decision was further
supported by the fact that non-engineering students worked alongside engineering students during the event. The data consisted of responses from 8 female and 14 male students, with 13 of the 22 partici-
pants experiencing their first hackathon.

### 4.2. Distinctive features of the learning process in a hackathon
In hackathons, the teams are driven to learn new concepts, techniques, and tools to solve the proposed challenges (Byrne et al., 2017; Kienzler & Fontanesi, 2017). Question nr. 4 provides information about the
motivation of students to learn in a short interval of time and with a high enough level to apply what they have learned to their prototype.
The results provide insights into the factors driving rapid learning during the hackathon. As seen in Figure 4, the most frequently cited motivator was the competitive nature of the event, with 86.4% of
respondents (19 students) indicating that competing with other teams played a key role in their learn- ing. Other significant factors included the intense hackathon atmosphere and the time constraints, both
noted by 68.2% of students (15 responses), as well as the necessity to learn in order to complete the task, highlighted by 59.1% (13 participants).
Surprisingly, external rewards such as prizes were reported as less influential, with only four students (18.2%) mentioning them. This suggests that while external rewards might be expected to incentivize
participation, the intrinsic challenge and urgency of the hackathon format may foster a more immediate and powerful form of motivation. Interaction with expert mentors was also a secondary factor, with
31.8% (7 responses) considering it important.
Some   of   these   findings   confirm   conclusions   from   previous   studies,   as   will   be   reviewed   in   the
Discussion section, while others represent new insights compared to those studies.
These results can lead to insights about features that distinguish hackathons from curricular learning environments, and which generate exceptional learning processes in some participants.
The final question of the survey addressed another typical feature of hackathons: the possibilities that participants have to be in contact with the context. These opportunities come in many ways: challenges
posed by real companies and organizations; talks and direct advice from expert professionals, mainly from experienced engineers; datasets and information from real situations; presentations to and feed-
back from judges who are usually executives from the sponsoring firms.
Figure 5 shows that the hackathon provided a rich experience of interaction with the context for most of the students in the sample. The two segments on the right side of the graph represent this
group of participants: 59.1%, 13 students, responded that during the event they had more contact with the real world than in their previous university courses, and 13.6% (3 participants) even expressed that
before the hackathon they had not had experience of interacting with the context throughout their pro- fessional studies. Hence, 72.7% of the answers (16 out of 22) are in this range.

5. Discussion
The survey results indicate that attending teams successfully performed essential engineering design tasks, such as identifying user needs and prototyping solutions, with 90.9% of the 22 students reporting
execution of all four key activities outlined in the engineering design framework (Dym et al., 2005).
Additionally, the products generated during the hackathon — lists of design ideas (90.9%), mockups (72.7%), and programming prototypes (63.6%) — align with typical outputs of engineering design activ-
ities, reinforcing the central role of prototyping in such events, as all of the respondents reported pro- ducing at least one prototype. Collectively, these findings suggest that the hackathon provided an
enabling environment for participants to actively apply their engineering design skills while utilizing relevant techniques throughout the design process.
But additionally, the event not only puts into practice existing knowledge and skills in design. The results also support the idea that students are encouraged to pursue new learnings related to engineer-
ing design. They developed new levels of mastery in identifying customers ’   and users ’   needs and objec- tives, and in what Dym et al. (2005) refer to as   ‘ specifying concepts for devices, systems, or processes ’
through the prototype they built. Moreover, the findings reveal that seventy-five percent of respondents reported acquiring knowledge about techniques and technologies for designing their solutions, which
may indicate that they expanded their engineering design skills during this activity.
On the other hand, another result from the survey shows that the majority of participants (72.7%) felt that the hackathon provided them with more exposure to real-world scenarios than they had previously
experienced in their courses. This aligns with a key aspect highlighted in the conceptual framework on engineering design, which emphasized that activities aimed at developing engineering design skills
should closely resemble real-world experiences. Therefore, this characteristic was effectively achieved through the hackathon.
These last three points, which refer to the activities and design outputs generated during the event, the learning related to design skills, and the direct contact with real-world information, experiences, and
stakeholders, address the first research question. Not only does a hackathon match the core attributes of a setting meant to foster engineering design skills (ABET, 2023; Dym et al., 2005)., but it also, as per-
ceived by the students surveyed, stimulates the development of these skills through the acquisition of design and prototyping techniques in an environment that closely mirrors real-world engineering chal-
lenges. However, it is important to note that the sample size of 22 students may limit the generalizabil- ity of these findings to broader contexts. Further studies with larger cohorts are recommended to
validate these observations.
To explore the second research question, which focuses on the factors driving rapid skill acquisition in the hackathon, one of our key findings aligns with previous studies. Duhring (2014) and Gama et al.
(2018) had both identified the pressure of limited time as a crucial factor in promoting fast learning among   students.   Similarly,   our   results,   particularly   from   question   4,   show   that   68.2%   of   surveyed

findings in the literature, now backed by firsthand data from participants in a real hackathon, unlike the prior studies which lacked this direct input.
Place et al. (2017) highlighted the significance of self-regulated learning, suggesting that a key factor in hackathon educational effectiveness was participants ’   intrinsic motivation, as they   ‘ came to the hacka-
thon to act upon their desire to learn ’ . Our findings are partially consistent with this observation. In our study, 59.1% of respondents identified   ‘ Learning by Need ’   as a factor contributing to rapid skill acquisi-
tion, but it was not the most prominent factor. Competition with others (86.4%) ranked highest, fol- lowed by Limited Time and the Hackathon Environment, both at 68.2%. The difference in our results
compared to Place ’ s study may be explained by the different conceptual frameworks: while Place used PBL, our study is based on Experiential Learning, which may have emphasized that external dynamics
like competition and time constraints play an even more influential role in driving rapid learning within the hackathon setting.
Additionally, our results reveal a key finding not emphasized in previous studies: for the majority of our sample (86.4% or 19 out of 22 students), the primary factor driving rapid learning was   ‘ Competition
with others ’ . When combined with the next two factors — Limited Time and the Hackathon Environment, both identified by 68.2% of respondents — a distinctive pattern emerges. These three elements together
form a unique setting that appears to create a powerful combination of external pressures and motiv- ation. This combination acts as a catalyst for accelerated learning, underscoring the importance of
designing hackathons that foster competition, urgency, and an immersive, dynamic atmosphere for par- ticipants. This provides an answer to the second research question based on the results.
Next, based on the insights gained, we can reflect on the implications for pedagogical practices in engineering, particularly regarding the integration of hackathons into the curriculum. While hackathons
do not encompass all aspects of traditional Capstone projects — such as extended analysis, synthesis of solutions, and detailed technical designs project (Fletcher & Harrington, 2018; Meah et al., 2020; Uys,
2019) — they   effectively   highlight   several   essential   components   relevant   to   engineering   design
experiences.
Importantly, hackathons should not be viewed as replacements for comprehensive learning processes like Capstone projects or internships, which require more time for in-depth exploration. Instead, the
short-term, high-pressure environment of a hackathon may offer specific benefits that could contribute to student engagement and skill development, according to the results observed in the event. Our find-
ings highlight the key factors that stimulated rapid learning in the studied event.
Moreover, hackathons provide an opportunity for students to engage with real-world challenges and stakeholders, an experience that many participants (72.7%) reported as unprecedented in their educa-
tion. This immersive exposure allows students to collaborate with domain experts and address pressing engineering design tasks, creating a vibrant experiential learning environment.
Thus, incorporating hackathons into engineering curricula can enrich the learning landscape, either as supplementary extracurricular activities or as part of in-class experiences, as has already been proposed
(Gama et al., 2018; Horton et al., 2018). This approach could enable educators to leverage the dynamic pace and stimulating conditions of hackathons, enhancing the development of engineering design skills.
By redefining hackathons as integral components of the educational framework, engineering schools can better prepare students to design innovative solutions that meet real-world demands.
6. Limitations
One limitation of this study relates to the specific hackathon analyzed. While the event shares key char- acteristics with many others — including its structure based on real-world company challenges, an open
format for participants from various institutions, awards, and a two-day timeframe — the findings were derived from this particular case. Hackathons are not standardized across all settings, and this variability
may influence the generalizability of the results. However, the alignment of this hackathon ’ s design with common practices enhances the relevance of the findings to similar events. Further investigation could
explore a wider range of hackathons to validate these findings.

Another limitation is the relatively small sample size of 22 survey respondents out of 127 participants, which restricts the generalization of the findings. Small sample sizes are common in hackathon studies
(Byrne et al., 2017; Gama et al., 2018; Place et al., 2017; Uys, 2019) due to the challenge of gathering data during fast-paced events where participants are deeply engaged in competition. Addressing this
constraint in future analyses could involve developing more robust methods for data collection that engage a larger number of participants without interrupting the event flow.
The findings are also based on student self-assessments, which may introduce response biases, such as over- or underestimation of their skills and learning. This limitation reflects the study ’ s focus on stu-
dent perceptions, which is a valuable perspective for understanding rapid skill acquisition in hackathons.
Finally, hackathons are often associated with computer science and IT fields, but our survey included students from various majors, with 81.8% from different engineering disciplines. Therefore, the conclu-
sions extend beyond computing and are relevant to engineering fields more broadly. While hackathons started in computing, they ’ ve since been adopted in many engineering domains, such as mechanical
engineering with events like MakeMIT and civil engineering with BuiltWorlds at the University of Illinois.
7. Conclusions
In this study, we sought to answer two primary questions: (1) Do participants develop engineering design skills during a hackathon? and (2) What factors contribute to the rapid acquisition of these skills,
according to the students? To address these questions, we collected and analyzed data from a nation- wide hackathon, based on a survey of 22 participating undergraduate students. The analysis of the
responses was conducted within the framework of two key perspectives: a standard conception of engineering design skills development and an educational approach grounded in Experiential Learning
theory.
First, this study generated insights into the development of engineering design skills during the hack- athon, as explored in our research question 1: Do participants develop engineering design skills during a
hackathon? Our results indicate that the hackathon provided an enabling environment for participants to engage in typical engineering design activities. Teams consistently generated standard outputs, such
as lists of design ideas, mockups, and functional prototypes, aligning with expected outcomes of engin- eering design processes. Additionally, students demonstrated the ability to perform key design tasks,
including identifying user needs, generating multiple solution alternatives, and making critical decisions in the face of real-world constraints. Beyond simply applying existing knowledge, participants also
reported significant learning gains, particularly in new techniques and technologies for designing their solutions. Moreover, the hackathon exposed students to real-world scenarios more directly than tradi-
tional coursework, further enhancing their learning experience. These findings strongly suggest that hackathons not only allow participants to apply engineering design skills but could also foster their
development in an experiential setting. This conclusion aligns with established educational frameworks for engineering design (ABET, 2023; Dym et al., 2005), reinforcing the hackathon ’ s potential as a platform
for learning and skill acquisition.
In addition, this study generated insights into the factors that drive the rapid learning of skills during a hackathon. The findings indicate that the competitive nature of the event played a crucial role, with
86.4% of participants citing competition with other teams as a primary motivator for their learning. The intense atmosphere of the hackathon and time constraints were also significant factors, with 68.2% of
respondents identifying these elements as influential in their learning experience. Furthermore, 59.1% of participants noted that the necessity to learn to complete their tasks contributed to their rapid skill
acquisition, as did the   ‘ Learning by Need ’   approach. These results expand upon Duhring ’ s (2014) pre- sumption that limited time may enhance rapid learning and provide further insights in relation to Gama
et al. (2018), who identified similar benefits but from an activity conducted within a course context rather than a hackathon.
Notably, our research revealed an interesting dynamics of factors, suggesting that the convergence of competition, time pressure, and an engaging environment creates an energetic atmosphere that signifi-
cantly propels skill development. This synergy not only amplifies the urgency to learn but also enhances the   overall   educational   experience,   highlighting   the   vital   role   of   intentional   hackathon   design   in

structured hackathons can significantly enhance the learning experience, addressing the second research question about the driving factors behind rapid skill acquisition in this context.
In the future, it would be useful to conduct experiences where hackathons would be combined with regular design courses for the same group of students. It would then be possible to get more insight
into the strengths and constraints of each setting for the development of engineering design skills.
It would also be useful to carry out similar studies to this one for other core skills in engineering edu- cation. We anticipate that, for example, teamwork and communication are also put to the test during
these events.
It is well established that engineering design skills are effectively developed when students engage in contextualized learning experiences. This research highlights how traditional courses can benefit from
incorporating hackathon-style experiences. By leveraging the stimulating conditions of these experiential environments, engineering educators can enhance the learning landscape and better prepare students
to tackle real-world challenges with their design skills.
