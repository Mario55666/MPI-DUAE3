Thiemo Wambsgan ss ,   University of Applied Sciences , Bern ,   Swi tzerland , thiemo.wambsganss @bfh.ch Abstract In today's information - driven society,   knowledge doesn’t last long .   P eople need to keep learning .   The
experiential learning approach, which focuses on practical use, becomes very important.   Combining this with Conversational Agents (CAs)   can improve learning . A study involving 150 participants, guided
by a CA in the fundamentals of argumentation, revealed that learners using a Conversational Agent with Kolb's Learning Cycle achieved superior outcomes in practical knowledge verification compared
to those relyi ng on a CA without the learning cycle. This underscores the effectiveness of merging experiential le arning principles with Conversational Agents for improved educational results. The
findings   suggest   that   embracing   technology   in   the   realm   of   experiential   learning   contributes significantly to individual knowledge acquisition and application, addressing th e demands of a swiftly
evolving knowledge landscape.
Keywords:   Conversational Agents, Experiment, Kolb Learning Cycle, Pedagogical Approach.
1   Introduction In the information age, continuous learning is essential for individuals and employees to keep pace with
rapidly advancing technologies. According to   this development , employees need to develop new skills because there is a substantial lack of digital talent in the respective skill domains (Hoberg et al., 2017).
Employees who acquire knowledge must be able to implement or apply it for productive use (Ch et al., 2021 ;   Chamorro - Premuzic, 2021). To counteract the rapid change of knowledge, continuous learning   is
an important measure to familiarize employees with new technologies   (Janssen et al., 2018) .
A survey from McKinsey about the success factors for digital transformation illustrates, that one important step to succeeding within a company's digital transformation process is to offer individual
learning modules to develop specific skills   (De la Boutetière et al., 2018) .   Besides the digital transformation process, the basic learning conditions are changing because companies are looking for
more flexible learning forms because of the integration within the daily work routine   (Eichhorst, W., Marx, P, o.   J.) .
Flexible learning gives employees or learners the freedom to decide on the frequency, timing, and duration of their learning (Harry, 1994). An appropriate learning medium that provides this flexibility
are Conversational Agents,   abbreviated further as CAs .   CAs are software programs designed to simulate conversation with a real person over natural language interaction interfaces   (Rubin et al., 2010) . An
important advantage of a CA is the natural language which allows learners to concentrate on the cognitive resources on the learning tasks and not spend the time understanding how to use the application
(Beun et al., 2003) . Through immediate and personalized feedback, CAs are increasingly adopted in the educational area   (Winkler & Söllner, 2018).   Due to their dialogue - based mechanisms, chatbots can

experiment setting was that it eliminates the necessity for a specific participant group with a technological background, but it enables us to check the effective usage of theoretical knowledge in   a
practical application. One reasonable valuation to support students in their learning process and to achieve successful learning outcomes is to build a CA considering the pedagogical aspects discussed
before. As stated above, there is a lack of using pe dagogical aspects in building CAs to support students in learning. To the best of our knowledge, there is no similar study that uses ELT in CAs to help students
improve their learning outcomes. To answer our research question, we created an experiment wher e the participants must complete a learning unit about the basics of argumentation taught by a CA. For this,
we have built three different CAs with Google Dialogflow. Two of them have integrated the four stages of Kolb’s learning cycle with the difference   that one CA provides the learner the possibility to select the
preferred learning stage to start with. The third CA provides general theoretical input about the basics of argumentation without any practical use. We believe Kolb’s learning theory embedded i n a CA could
help students in learning and lead to better learning outcomes because, within Kolb’s theory, the theoretical imparted is directly used in practical tasks to deepen the knowledge. This paper presents the
results of our experiment.   Within our p aper, we first focus on the theoretical background. More precisely, we describe the ELT theory and derive hypotheses for our research model. Afterward, we will
explain our conducted experiment in detail. Finally, we specify our methodological approach and illustrate the result of our study.

### 2   Conceptual Background and Hypotheses Development
Due to rapid technological advancements in a society characterized by knowledge and information, knowledge has become a crucial competitive factor   (Hinkofer & Mandl, 2003) . A common issue when
acquiring new knowledge, for example in the traditional form of instruction, is that theoretical knowledge is learned but cannot be effectively applied in practical situations   (Renkl, 1996) . According
to   Gottein, 2016 , challenges encountered during the acquisition of knowledge may include a lack of exercises or time r esources, complex learning units, and extended periods between a learning unit and a
specific practical situation. To overcome this gap, knowledge has to be transferred as applicable knowledge   (Reinmann - Rothmeier, 2001)   where learning is understood as an active process where the
learner is actively engaged during the learning process   (Bürg et al., 2003) . CAs with artificial intelligence can help to create active learning experiences for the learner. In the education context, they
can   positively   impact   learn ing   outcomes   and   student   satisfaction   (Winkler   &   Söllner,   2018) .
Nevertheless, for successful learning outcomes, technology - mediated learning environments must follow a pedagogical approach (San Chee, 2002) .   A learning theory that considers the active engagement
of learning during the learning process is Kolb’s Experiential Learning Theory (ELT).
Hence, in this chapter, our focus is on Kolb’s Experiential Learning Theory, employed as a framework in our experiment. Additionally, we give an overview of current research around CAs, especially in the
context of education. Based on Kolb’s Learning Cycle , we derive the hypotheses for our research model.
2.1   Conversational   a gents in   e ducation CAs are software programs designed to simulate conversation with a real person over natural language
interaction interfaces   (Rubin et al., 2010) . S tarting in 1966 with ELIZA in the context of psychotherapy, CAs have a long history   ( Weizenbaum, 1966) .   The domains in which CAs are meanwhile used are
diverse like healthcare   (Kowatsch et al., 2017) , customer services   (Xu et al., 2017) , counseling (Fitzpatrick et al., 2017)   or education   (Wambsganss et al., 2020; Winkler & Söllner, 2018) .   Since the
early 1970s, CAs in education have been utilized as conversational pedagogical agents (Laurillard , 2013). CAs in education are characterized by engaging learners in a dialog - based   conversation using AI
and are a subgroup of pedagogical agents   (Gu lz et   al., 2011).   A systematic review from Okonkwo and A. Ade - Ibijola (2021)   identified several areas where chatbots in education are used. The main section
(66%) was teaching and learning (Okonkwo & Ade - Ibijola, 2021). In teaching and learning, chatbots provide students with interactive and personalized learning experiences (Cola ce, De Santo, et al., 2018),
improving learning outcomes and student happiness   (Winkler & Söllner, 2018) . Compared to other ways of interaction in education (email communication an d in - person interaction), instant personalized
communication by a chatbot is more convenient for a student (Cunningham - Nelson et al., 2019). This adaptive feature of a CA can support a student to enhance their skills individually, and the speed of
learning   can be adapted (Colace, De Santo, et al., 2018). According to   (Wambsganss et al., 2021) , PCAs “   .. have been successfully used to adaptively support learners to conduct a task by mimicking the gold
standard of human tutors, e.g., for programming tasks mat hematical skills as well as for learning factual knowledge ”.   Besides this application areas, (Wambsganss et al., 2021)   created “Argue Tutor”, a CA
that assists students in writing persuasive texts and teaches them to improve their argumentation skills.
(Winkler et al., 2020)   designed a voice -   and text - based CA with an instructional scaffolding mechanism in an online video lecture context. When employing interactive dialogue - based systems like PCAs,
students demonstrate a higher level of engagement compared   to traditional technology - enhanced learning systems   (Weber et al., 2021) .   Compared with passive engagement, when learners consume
content like reading a text   (Weber et al., 2021)   interactive learning leads to better learning outcomes (Chi & Wylie, 2014) .

### 2.2   Kolb’s   l earning   c ycle as   k ernel   t heory
The learning cycle is one of the core concepts of the Experienced Learning Theory (ELT), and it understands learning as a process of constructing knowledge by structuring learning sessions into four
learning stages   (A. Kolb & D. Kolb, 2018) .   Besides other learning methods, we decided to use Kolb’s ELT theory because the learning cycle emphasizes the importance of experience in learning   (San Chee,
2002) . According to Kolb (1984), the cycle consists of four stages beginning with Concrete Experience,
followed by Reflective Observation, Abstract Conceptualisation, and finally Active Experimentation.
In addition to the depicted sequence of progressing throu gh the stages, the learner also has the option to independently choose the starting stage. Based on the learning preferences, it can be chosen   (Healey &
Jenkins, 2000) .   The cycle illustrates two related modes of grasping experience   -   Concrete Experience an d Abstract Conceptualization and two associated modes of transforming experience   –   Reflective
Observation RO and Active Experimentation   (A. Kolb & D. Kolb, 2005) .
Figure 1.   Overview of the four stages of Kolb’s Experiential Learning Cycle   (D. A. Kolb, 2014) The four - stage learning cycle, due to its simplicity and utility, provides an alternative to the traditional
model of information transmission, particularly by actively involving the learner in the learning process (A. Kolb & Kolb, 2018) . There are two dimensions in Kolb’s Learning cycle: The vertical dimension of
Concrete   Experience   to   Abstract   Conceptualization,   which   means   learning   results   from   direct experience   or   abstractions.   In   the   horizontal   dimension,   Reflective   Observation   to   Ac tive
Experimentatio n refers to reflecting information on the experience or action on the conclusions that have been drawn from the experience   (Svinicki & Dixon, 1987) . For a complete and successful learning
experience, all four modes of the learning cycle must be accessed. The process has the flexibility that the entry point could be based on students' learning preferences   (Kolb, 2014) . Phase 1, “Concrete
Experience” has a motivational aspect, and the online -   but also presence education video and podcast are an appropriate me dium to implement   (Eckert, 2020) . The counterpart in the dimension of grasping
experience is “Abstract Conceptualization”. Within this phase, the learner can reflect on the experiences made in the stage of “Concrete Experience” from different perspectives to constitute an initial theory
(Baasanjav, 2013) . In digital learning scenarios, quizzes are an appropriate element to support generalization processes   (Eckert, 2020) . The stage of Reflective Observation is one pole in the horizontal
dimension of the cycl e. Here the learner focuses on understanding ideas through observation and Thirty - Second   European Conference   on Information Systems (ECIS 2024 ),   Paphos, Cyprus   4

(Kohonen, 2007) . The counterpart of Reflective Observation is Active Experimentation, which requires doing from the learner through the use of theories to create practical outcomes   (Young, 2002) .
Based on the illustrated key elements of the learning cycle we pose the following hypothesis: H1:   Embedding practical stages (stage   Abstract Concept / Quiz and Abstract Conceptualization / Case
Study)   of the learning cycle, compared to not embedding practical stages, in a conversational learning exercise increases the engagement and learning outcomes of students .
H2:   Through the experiential learning approach within the cycle, knowledge is more deeply anchored, resulting in improved learning outcomes.
H3:   Through a clear structure and guidance through the stages of the learning cycle, attention to the learning content increases.
### 3   Research Methodology
3.1   Experimental   d esign and   p rocedure In this section, we explain the experiential setting of our study.   Our goal was to confirm our hypotheses.
In adopting a pedagogical approach for our study, we chose to apply Kolb's learning cycle, anticipating potential improvements in learning outcomes.   For the verification of our hypotheses, we conducted an
online experiment with three groups where the participants were asked to answer three questions in the context of argumentative   distinctiveness. The participants were randomly assigned. For this, we send an
invitation to potential candidates of our university. K - Lab,   a university department responsible for laboratory and online experiments conducted the invitations. The prospective participants received the
information that they could participate in our experiment until a specified date. As we prepared three different   scenarios for the experiment, three surveys were created, each with the distinction that a
specific link was integrated, calling up the respective CA for the learning unit about argumentative distinctiveness as a component of the experiment. For each surv ey, we were able to recruit 50
participants, in total 150 participants took part in the experiment. In each group, we had a balanced ratio between female and male participants, in total 83 female and 67 male participants. Over 85% had no
prior argumentativ e knowledge.   We asked the participants about their age and offered four different age groups to choose from: up to 25 years, 26 - 30 years, 31 - 40 years, and over 40 years. The majority of
participants, namely 62%, belonged to the group up to 25 years old. 32 % of participants were in the age range of 26 - 30 years. A smaller number of respondents, namely 5%, fell into the age group of 31 - 40
years.   Each participant received an 8 USD compensation for participating in a 20 – 30 - minute online experiment.
The first group used a CA that utilized the four phases of the learning cycle as an underlying framework.
This group was guided through the learning unit automatically. The second group CA also had the learning cycle integrated, with the difference that th e learner could flexibly select the entry point to start
the learning unit. A third group had a CA with theoretical explanations about the basics of argumentation but not the four stages of Kolb’s learning cycle integrated. Technically the CAs are implemen ted with
Google’s platform Dialogflow and a Zobot integration from SalesIQ. The SalesIQ integration was utilized to directly embed the video into the CA screen and implement the quiz questions.
We test the learning outcome through our survey. For this, the learners get a text on a conversation between two teachers arguing about student violence in a secondary school   (Flender et al., 1999) .

4.3   Post - S urvey   a nomalies Upon analyzing the responses to the post - survey items, notable distinctions emerged between groups
concerning two key inquiries: "Did active participation contribute to a better understanding of conveyed knowledge?" and "Did the utilization of graphic elem ents (video, quizzes, graphics, etc.) aid in the clear
communication of content?" This examination employed ANOVA and Tukey HSD post - hoc tests.
Concerning the first query, the ANOVA results revealed substantial group differences (F(2,143) = 5.499, p = 0.00 5). Tukey HSD multiple comparisons further illuminated specific distinctions, notably between
the LearnBot group and GuidedBot group (p = 0.0075344) and the LearnBot group and FlexBot group (p = 0.0274191). This implies that the mean values of these groups   significantly deviate from each other.
It is noteworthy that the p - value, falling below 0.05, attests that these distinctions are not haphazard but rather indicative of genuine group disparities. Post - hoc scrutiny underscored the discernible impact of
act ive participation on the dependent variable, with the LearnBot group showcasing markedly different outcomes compared to the GuidedBot and FlexBot groups.
Regarding the second question, "Did the use of graphic elements facilitate the clear conveyance of content?" ANOVA revealed a significant difference between groups (F(2,143) = 3.898, p = 0.0225).
Tukey multiple comparisons indicated a significant differenc e between the LearnBot and FlexBot groups (p = 0.0407827) and a suggestive difference between the LearnBot and GuidedBot groups (p =
0.0518408). This suggests that the mean values of these groups either differ statistically significantly or exhibit a disce rnible trend. Although the p - value for the LearnBot vs. GuidedBot comparison is slightly

### 5   Discussion
In our research study, we investigated whether the integration of a pedagogical approach within a CA could lead to better learning outcomes. For this purpose, we used the learning cycle from Kolb's
experiential learning theory as the basis for the design c onsiderations.   As demonstrated in our results section, the utilization of a conversational agent with an integrated pedagogical approach   can contribute
to improved learning outcomes. Particularly, in applying the learned content, in our experiment, the identification of an argumentative anomaly in a text, the Flexbot group exhibited a clear advantage over
the Learnbot group. The results sup port the hypotheses we formulated in Section 2.2. The combination of theoretical instruction on the topic of "argumentative anomalies"   and practical application through
quiz questions and case studies may have contributed to a deeper engagement with the subject matter by the learners and encouraged them to consider various perspectives.
It is important to emphasize that we chose an exploratory research approach to gain fundamental insights into considering a learning theory in a CA. This means that for the implementation of the learning unit
with the CA that integrated the learning cycle,   we did not explicitly instruct participants in this group to go through the learning cycle multiple times. Kolb suggests that optimal learning outcomes occur when
the cycle is repeated several times.   The insights gained from the experiment indicate that t he Kolb Thirty - Second   European Conference   on Information Systems (ECIS 2024 ),   Paphos, Cyprus   10

learning outcomes compared to a CA that doesn’t consider experiential learning.   A possible explanation for the good performance of Flexbot could be that the integration of the entire Kolb learning cycle in
conjunction with a conversational agent assuming the role of a learning companion contributes to better understanding and processing of information. Each   phase of the learning cycle also follows a didact ic
approach. In the phase of Concrete Experience, which we implemented in the form of a video, the goal was to arouse interest and direct attention to the topic "Fundamentals of Argumentation." We utilized
the Reflective Observation phase to encourage lear ners to reflect and contemplate through structured learning content. The quiz aimed to facilitate the active participation of learners in the learning process
and, finally, simulate a specific situation based on a case example. We believe that the combinat ion of different learning stages represented by different elements helped the learner during the learning process.
Participants' feedback from the post - survey questions indicates that the chosen design elements contributed to the structured and understandable delivery of the content through the CAs, as perceived
by the learners. Also, guided learning, i.e., the interac tive guidance of the learner through a learning unit (from stage to stage) and the application of the conveyed material in practical interactive questions (quiz
and case study), offer added value to the learner. Possibly, this combination of theory deliver y and practical application contributed to the longer - term retention of the material by the learner.
An alternative explanation for the results of our experiment could be that   learners interacting with CAs that integrate the Kolb cycle not only develop theoretical understanding but also better apply the learned
material in practical phases in concrete situations.   Participants were generally satisfied with how the material was presented and felt that the structure through the individual phases contributed to clarity.
Due to the interdisciplinary nature of the topic, the findings from our experiment can provide resea rchers in the fields of educational psychology, computer science,   and pedagogy with an impetus for further
work and exploring the complexity from different perspectives.
Our findings may also be relevant in the field of corporate training. Instead of training sessions, which often cover a broader range of topics, CAs can be developed specifically for particular issues focusing
on specific areas. This also enables individua l employee development or gradual introduction of new topics. Additionally, in the workplace context, employees often require skills that need to be practically
implemented. This can be specifically taught during the learning process with a CA, utilizing t he learning cycle. Immediate feedback helps identify and avoid mistakes promptly.   Hence, our work makes
several contributions to current research.
To the best of our knowledge, this study is one of the first to present empirical insights into the utilization of a pedagogical approach within dialog based CAs.   The present study contributes to integrating more
empirical data for the optimal use of pedagogical approaches and various learning theories in CAs. Thus, it aims to provide learners with a tool that optimally supports them in their learning process in the   future.
Our research is not without limitations. The planned experiment is embedded in a specific learning scenario. We cannot generalize that other technology - mediated learning scenarios will have identical
results. Therefore, the experiment should be repeated wi th other samples. Also, there needs to be evaluated in which learning context our CAs have the best learning outcomes.   Investigations over a
longer period could provide insights into whether the learned material is stored longer in the learner's memory wit h the various learning elements within each stage compared to the CA which does not follow
a pedagogical learning approach.

In our research project, we designed, developed, and evaluated three CAs. In two of the CAs, the different stages were structured based on the Kolb learning cycle, which constitutes the central element
of experiential learning by Kolb. The learner was spec ifically guided through the learning unit. The third CA pursued a purely interactive approach, where the learner independently accessed theoretical content
