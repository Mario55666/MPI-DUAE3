### 1.2. The rise and application of gamification in education
Gamification, as an emerging educational method, incorporates game mechanisms and elements to enhance learner engagement and motivation (Saleem et al., 2022). Extensive research has shown that
gamification can effectively improve learning experiences and outcomes. By introducing elements, such as point systems, badge rewards, leaderboards and task challenges, educators can design interactive
tasks that foster a sense of competition and collaboration, thereby making the learning process more immersive (Paredes et al., 2023). These elements collectively stimulate students ’   intrinsic motivation,
encourage experimentation and promote sustained focus (Dehghanzadeh et al., 2021). As a result, gami- fication is increasingly recognized for promoting deep learning, active participation and creative prob-
lem-solving   –   critical components of intercultural competence development.
### 1.3. Research questions and methodological framework
This study is guided by three interconnected questions that address the application and impact of gami- fication in intercultural communication courses. The first question explores how specific gamification
strategies, especially point systems, badge rewards, leaderboards and task challenges, can be effectively integrated into course design within higher education English programs. The second question investi-
gates the influence of these gamification elements on key educational outcomes, including student engagement, motivation, creative thinking and intercultural communication competence. By analyzing
data collected through surveys and pre- and post-course assessments, the study evaluates whether gamified approaches offer measurable advantages over traditional methods. Finally, the third question
seeks to understand students ’   perceptions of gamified courses, focusing on their satisfaction levels, per- ceived challenges and feedback regarding the course design and execution. This qualitative component,
enriched by in-depth interviews and classroom observations, provides nuanced insights into how stu- dents experience gamification and identifies potential areas for improvement.

2. Literature review on gamification in education and intercultural communication
A comprehensive review of the existing literature on the application of gamification in education is pro- vided, with a particular focus on its impact on intercultural communication. The review begins by explor-
ing the origins and development of gamification design, followed by an examination of the theoretical foundations that underpin gamification (Sailer et al., 2017). Practical applications of gamification in edu-
cational settings are also discussed, assessing its effectiveness in enhancing learning outcomes (Hanifah & Ninggolan, 2023). Furthermore, the review addresses the current state and challenges of intercultural
communication teaching and evaluates the applicability of gamification design in this context. Through this literature review, a solid foundation is established for understanding how gamification can be inte-
grated   into   intercultural   communication   courses   to   improve   student   engagement   and   learning effectiveness.
2.1. The origin and development of gamification design Gamification, originating in the late 20th century and rapidly developing in the early 21st century,
involves the application of game elements and mechanisms to non-game contexts to enhance engage- ment and motivate behavior (Kalinauskas, 2014). Initially applied in the business sector to increase user
loyalty and engagement through mechanisms, such as point systems, badges and leaderboards (Arufe- Gir  aldez et al., 2022), gamification has recently entered the educational field as an innovative teaching
method. Early studies primarily focused on its role in providing incentive and immediate feedback.
Recent research has expanded this perspective by demonstrating that case- and project-based gamifica- tion models in online learning can significantly boost student engagement and academic achievement
(Suartama et al., 2024). This evolution from commercial applications to educational innovations under- scores the potential of gamification in addressing the unique challenges of intercultural communication
teaching.
Core concepts of gamification design include point systems, badge rewards, leaderboards and task challenges (Laura-De La Cruz et al., 2023). These elements aim to enhance students ’   motivation and
engagement through incentive mechanisms and instant feedback (Triantafyllou, Sapounidis, & Farhaoui, 2024). Point systems quantify students ’   actions and achievements, providing immediate feedback and
rewards.   Badge   rewards   offer   visual   recognition   of   accomplishments,   enhancing   learners ’   sense   of achievement and belonging. Leaderboards foster competition, stimulating students ’   enthusiasm and par-
ticipation. Task challenges decompose complex tasks, providing clear goals and pathways, thereby enhancing learners ’   autonomy and persistence.

### 2.2. The relationship between gamification theory and education
Gamification theories provide a robust foundation for understanding how game elements can be effect- ively integrated into educational contexts to enhance learning outcomes (Rivera-Trigueros & S  anchez-
P  erez, 2020). Key theories in this field include self-determination theory, flow theory and social cognitive theory,   each   offering   unique   insights   into   the   mechanisms   that   drive   learner   engagement   and
motivation.
Self-determination theory emphasizes the critical role of intrinsic motivation in learning. According to this theory, learners become more motivated and engaged when their fundamental psychological needs
for autonomy, competence and relatedness are met (Beauchamp et al., 2019). In gamified educational settings, providing learners with choices in task selection or learning paths can enhance autonomy,
while clear goal-setting, immediate feedback, and progressively challenging tasks bolster their sense of competence (Sanchez Perez & Galera Masegosa, 2020). Moreover, collaborative tasks and social interac-
tions within learning communities foster relatedness, thereby further increasing intrinsic motivation.
Flow theory describes an optimal learning state characterized by deep immersion and complete con- centration, achieved when the challenge of a task aligns with the learner ’ s skill level (Gong et al., 2022).
If a task is too simple, learners become bored; if it is too difficult, anxiety may result. Gamification facili- tates flow by offering adaptive challenges through mechanisms, such as progressive levels, real-time
feedback and immediate rewards, which help maintain the delicate balance required to keep learners fully engaged and productive.
Social cognitive theory posits that learning occurs within a social context through observation, imita- tion and feedback (Cerone, 2023). In a gamified educational environment, features such as leaderboards,
peer reviews and collaborative projects enable learners to observe high-performing peers and model their behaviors, thereby reinforcing positive learning habits (Atiku & Anane-Simon, 2022). This approach
not only fosters a community of practice where knowledge is shared but also enhances individual per- formance through social reinforcement.
Building on these foundational theories, recent empirical studies in online learning have demon- strated that the integration of gamification elements   –   such as points, badges, leaderboards and pro-
gress indicators   –   can significantly improve student engagement and academic performance (Suartama et al., 2024). This evidence suggests that gamification not only stimulates intrinsic motivation but also
creates interactive and collaborative learning environments that are particularly beneficial for developing intercultural communication skills.
The   application   of   gamification   in   language   learning   is   well   exemplified   by   platforms,   such   as
Duolingo, Kahoot! and Classcraft, which employ task-based learning, leveling systems and competitive features to foster learner engagement and intercultural understanding (Zitha et al., 2023; Huseinovi  c,
2023). Through the integration of these gamification elements, these platforms effectively transform con- ventional learning settings into dynamic, interactive environments that not only stimulate active partici-
pation but also encourage creativity and facilitate effective intercultural communication. Such practical applications of gamification demonstrate its potential to enhance learning outcomes by creating immer-
sive and motivating educational experiences.
### 2.3. The impact of gamification design on language learning outcomes
Numerous studies have demonstrated that gamification design can significantly enhance language learn- ing outcomes. The integration of gamification elements into educational activities has led to notable
improvements in various aspects of the learning process, particularly in terms of motivation, engage- ment and academic performance.
One of the key findings from these studies is that gamification substantially boosts learners ’   motiv- ation. By incorporating game-like features, such as point systems, badges, leaderboards and challenges,
learners are provided with continuous incentives to participate actively in their language learning tasks (Gul & Uz Bilgin, 2021). This sustained motivation is crucial for language acquisition, as it encourages
learners to spend more time practicing and engaging with the material, which is essential for developing proficiency. Engagement is another critical area where gamification has shown significant benefits.

### 2.4. Current status and challenges of intercultural communication teaching
Current intercultural communication teaching primarily relies on methods, such as classroom lectures, case studies, role-playing and cultural exchanges (Huang, 2023). While these methods aim to enhance
students ’   intercultural understanding and communication abilities, they often lack the interactive and engaging components needed to fully inspire students. The complexity of cultural differences   –   which
includes language, values, norms and cognitive styles (Guo & Modehiran, 2023)   –   poses significant chal- lenges to traditional teaching approaches, which may not effectively integrate theoretical knowledge
with   practical   intercultural   experiences.   Consequently,   there   is   a   pressing   need   for   more   dynamic,
student-centered pedagogical models that can address these limitations.
2.5. The applicability of gamification design in intercultural communication teaching Gamification design offers practical solutions to the challenges of intercultural communication teaching,
particularly by enhancing student motivation and engagement (Bassanelli et al., 2022). Through gamified activities such as simulated cross-cultural interactions and role-playing tasks, students can actively prac-
tice and refine their communication skills. For example, role-playing tasks enable learners to experience authentic cultural scenarios, such as navigating workplace dynamics in international teams or negotiat-
ing in multilingual settings (Al-Dosakee & Ozdamli, 2021). In addition, real-time feedback mechanisms like leaderboards and badges allow students to monitor their progress and adjust their learning behav-
iors effectively. Although existing research supports the potential benefits of gamification in this field, further studies are needed to investigate how cultural differences influence the acceptance and effect-
iveness of these methods (Hayes, 2022). In particular, understanding how students from collectivist   ver- sus   individualist cultures respond to competitive gamification elements may inform the design of more
culturally sensitive and adaptive teaching models. Overall, gamification in intercultural communication

3. Gamification design methods
To effectively integrate gamification into educational contexts   –   especially in intercultural communica- tion courses   –   it is essential to understand and adapt the fundamental elements and principles of gami-
fication design to meet the unique needs of diverse student populations (Boivan & Kovtun, 2023). This chapter provides a detailed exploration of the key gamification elements and the core principles guiding
their implementation, along with practical guidelines for their application in intercultural settings.
3.1. Definition and classification of gamification elements The point system is one of the most fundamental and common elements in gamification design. It
quantifies students ’   accomplishments in tasks or activities, providing immediate positive feedback that stimulates their motivation to learn. Points can be used to track students ’   progress, and accumulated
points can unlock privileges or rewards, continuously motivating students to engage in learning activ- ities (Kim & Castelli, 2021). In intercultural communication courses, point systems can be specifically tail-
ored to reward not only task completion but also effective intercultural interactions and reflective learning processes.
Badge rewards serve as visual representations of achievements, recognizing student performance in specific tasks or activities. Badges function as symbols of honor that enhance students ’   sense of achieve-
ment and belonging (Park & British & American Language & Literature Association Of Korea, 2022). For instance, designing badges, such as   ‘ Cultural Explorer, ’ ‘ Language Master ’   and   ‘ Team Collaboration
Award ’   in intercultural communication courses can directly support the development of intercultural skills and creative thinking by acknowledging progress in culturally relevant projects.
Leaderboards display students ’   points or achievements rankings, using competition to boost enthusi- asm and motivation. They can be set up to reflect both individual performance and group collaboration,
thereby promoting personal excellence and teamwork. In the context of intercultural communication courses, leaderboards may be organized by overall participation, task-specific achievements and contri-
butions to intercultural projects, adding an element of fun and friendly competition that reinforces cross-cultural learning objectives.
Tasks and challenges are at the core of gamification design. They guide students through progres- sively challenging activities by setting clear, measurable goals. Tasks can range from simple language
exchange exercises to complex intercultural research projects, while challenges with varying difficulty levels stimulate interest and promote deep learning (Syam et al., 2023). For example, tasks might include
conducting cultural research, engaging in role-playing scenarios or collaborating on intercultural case studies   –   each designed to progressively build intercultural communication skills and encourage creative
problem-solving.
3.2. Principles of gamification design Incentive mechanisms form the backbone of gamification design, aiming to stimulate intrinsic and
extrinsic motivation through various rewards and feedback systems (Chung et al., 2019). For instance, point systems and phased rewards have shown significant potential in encouraging sustained engage-
ment, particularly in intercultural communication tasks where gradual progress is essential. Beyond tradi- tional   rewards,   incorporating   culturally   relevant   incentives   –   such   as   tasks   that   highlight   regional
traditions   –   can further enhance students ’   engagement.
Immediate feedback is another cornerstone of effective gamification. Providing real-time feedback enables students to continuously monitor their performance and adjust their learning strategies accord-
ingly. Studies indicate that timely feedback not only enhances learning efficiency but also builds confi- dence when handling complex intercultural scenarios (Fernandes et al., 2022). For example, immediate

This principle is particularly important in intercultural courses, where students ’   diverse cultural back- grounds lead to varying preferences and strengths (Bassanelli et al., 2022). Allowing students to choose

3.3. Application of gamification design in intercultural communication courses In intercultural communication courses, the design of gamified tasks and activities is crucial. Tasks must
be diversified and closely aligned with both the course objectives and the unique needs of students from different cultural backgrounds. Examples include cultural experience tasks, intercultural exchange
projects and language practice activities, each designed with a graded difficulty to stimulate deep learn- ing. For example, a   ‘ Cultural Explorer ’   task might require students to conduct fieldwork or online
research on various cultural customs and present their findings through multimedia reports.
Gamified assessment and feedback mechanisms are essential to ensure effective learning outcomes.
Assessments should integrate point systems, badge rewards and leaderboards that reflect both academic performance and intercultural competence. Feedback must be immediate and constructive (R ’ boul &
Bueno-Alastuey, 2021), enabling both instructors and peers to provide insights that enrich the learning process. Quantitative measures, such as points and rankings, provide valuable data on student progress,
while qualitative feedback from peer reviews and instructor evaluations adds depth to the overall assess- ment. For example, leaderboards can be configured to emphasize teamwork achievements and intercul-
tural project contributions, fostering collaborative problem-solving in diverse cultural contexts. When integrated with immediate feedback systems, these approaches contribute to creating a dynamic and
supportive learning environment that encourages continuous improvement and active participation.

were then compiled into multimedia presentations and shared during class discussions.
To maintain engagement, each stage of the task awarded group members 10 points, ensuring con- tinuous motivation and rewarding consistent effort. Points were allocated based on the completion and
quality of work, encouraging students to stay actively involved throughout the project. For instance, stu- dents earned points for conducting thorough research, demonstrating analytical depth and delivering
effective presentations. The culmination of the task was the awarding of the   ‘ Cultural Explorer ’   badge, which recognized their collective efforts and individual contributions. As shown in Figure 3, the balanced
distribution of points across the task phases fostered consistent participation and ensured that all group members contributed meaningfully. While the task was generally well-received, some participants noted
challenges in accessing relevant resources, particularly for less familiar cultural themes. This feedback highlights the importance of providing flexible task options or curated resources to better support stu-
dents ’   diverse needs in future iterations.

### 5.1. Evaluation methods and tools
The evaluation approach integrated multiple methods   –   including structured questionnaires, in-depth interviews, classroom observations and learning outcome analyses   –   to provide a thorough assessment
of the course ’ s impact. Prior to full implementation, the questionnaire was pilot-tested with a small group of students to ensure reliability and validity, with subsequent revisions made to enhance clarity
and relevance (see Appendix A). The questionnaire collected detailed data on dimensions, such as engagement, motivation, satisfaction and overall feedback. It comprised demographic sections, Likert

6. Conclusion
The exploration of gamification in intercultural communication teaching has yielded valuable insights into both its effectiveness and the challenges it presents. The findings demonstrate that gamified strat-
egies   –   integrating point systems, badge rewards, leaderboards and task challenges   –   can substantially boost student engagement, stimulate creative thinking and enhance intercultural communication skills.
6.1. Advantages and disadvantages of gamification design in intercultural communication teaching Gamification design has demonstrated significant benefits in intercultural communication teaching. One
of the most notable strengths lies in its ability to enhance students ’   learning motivation and engage- ment. Elements, such as point systems, badge rewards, leaderboards and task challenges offer immedi-
ate   feedback   and   meaningful   incentives,   cultivating   intrinsic   motivation   and   fostering   active participation both in and beyond the classroom. These gamified components transform the learning pro-
cess into an engaging and interactive experience, enabling students to find a sense of achievement and

Leaderboards and team tasks also serve as catalysts for collaboration and healthy competition, promot- ing teamwork and group cohesion.
Despite these advantages, gamification in teaching is not without challenges. The complexity of designing and managing gamified tasks can impose a significant burden on educators, especially when

### 6.2. Implications and applications of research results
The findings of this study offer important implications for both teaching practice and course design.
A key takeaway is the necessity of centering course development around real student experiences, ensuring that gamification elements are tailored to meet the diverse needs of learners. By integrating
flexible and culturally relevant gamification strategies, educators can create interactive learning environ- ments that not only enhance intercultural communication skills but also promote creative thinking.
Establishing systematic, multi-channel feedback mechanisms is crucial. Real-time scoring, regular class- room discussions and periodic assessments enable educators to monitor student progress effectively
while providing timely and constructive guidance. The empirical evidence presented here offers action- able strategies for designing language courses, intercultural communication programs and other related
disciplines. Gamified tasks, such as cultural exploration projects and language dialogues, provide rich opportunities for experiential learning and critical engagement with diverse cultural practices.
