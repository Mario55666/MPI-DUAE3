Introduction Undergraduate Science, Technology, Engineering and Maths (STEM) students are com - monly required to acquire large amounts of knowledge early in their studies, and then
apply   this   knowledge   to   solve   complex   problems.   Thus,   foundational   university science courses feature dense, content-rich curricula and pedagogical approaches that
facilitate the mastery of vast amounts of knowledge. Higher-order thinking in the sciences relies heavily on students’ prior learning of foundational core concepts.
Blooms (Revised) taxonomy of learning (Krathwohl,   2002 ) demonstrates how this foun - dational knowledge, is further developed, and extended as students apply, analyse, evalu -
ate, and, ultimately, produce new knowledge across the duration of their tertiary studies.

This progression of learning and application of higher-order skills has implications for how courses are designed and taught. Acquiring knowledge of foundational concepts is
generally associated with didactic teaching approaches (Elen et al.,   2007 ). In these con - texts, student learning is typified by teacher-directed learning with the objective of accu -
rate recall. Understanding the targeted disciplinary knowledge (e.g. relevant definitions,
### concepts, and rules) is foundational to student progression.
Constructivist learning theories (Biggs,   2014 ) recognise learning as a dynamic activity
### involving students building on and incorporating new information through active
### engagement with knowledge. When educators design courses, they need to minimise
cognitive overload by choosing fit-for-purpose pedagogical approaches and curriculum design. Frequently this means a careful and responsive interplay of approaches—incor -
porating both didactic pedagogical practices and active, student-centred approaches to
### learning (Elen et al.,   2007 ).
Active learning refers to teaching approaches that involve and engage students in learning strategies that require them to take some form of learning ‘action’ and owner -
ship over their learning, and is associated with terms like ‘student-centred learning’(Gle - ason et al.,   2011 ). Active learning is commonly contrasted against didactic learning
methods (Hopper & Brake,   2018 ; Richardson,   2008 ; Wolff et al.,   2015 ), although we recognise a greater nuance is at play. These two approaches are almost always discussed
as mutually exclusive, often with active learning approaches presented as the panacea to learning challenges. Didactic teaching is considered old-fashioned, disengaging, and not
supportive of deep learning, often presented as a strategy utilised by teachers who have not developed the skills to teach using active learning strategies (Gleason et al.,   2011 ).
Eggen et al. ( 2007 ) note that while didactic approaches have faced heavy criticism, they can be effective if the content to be learned is ‘extensive and well-defined’
(p.500). Foundational science courses are examples of extensive, well-defined content areas, with a body of knowledge required to understand complex ideas and enable stu -
dents to engage in deep, meaningful ways with intricate ideas. These courses challenge the idea that learning is either ‘didactic’ or ‘active’ and suggest rather that a combination
of active and didactic methods can be effective and complementary methods for student
### learning.
Active learning involves students ‘doing things and thinking about the things they are doing’ (Bonwell & Eison,   1991 ). Active learning has demonstrated several advantages
### over traditional educational formats for both in-person and online environments
(Hodges,   2020 ). Following a meta-analysis of 225 studies comparing active learning with traditional lectures (Freeman et al.,   2014 ), active learning approaches increased
examination performance by half a grade, while also reducing failure rates (Williams & O’Dowd,   2021 ). These benefits were due to improved student attendance and retention
(Tomkin et al.,   2019 ) and the promotion of higher-order thinking (Kressler & Kressler, 2020 ). Active learning approaches have also been shown to benefit underrepresented and
marginalised minority groups (Ballen et al.,   2017 ; Braun et al.,   2018 ; Lugosi & Uribe, 2022 ; Sandrone et al.,   2021 ; Tharayil et al.,   2018 ; Theobald et al.,   2020 ; Williams &
O’Dowd,   2021 ). Active learning also promotes equity and inclusivity (Sandrone et al., 2021 ) and increases student self-efficacy (Ballen et al.,   2017 ). Some authors have noted
some student resistance to active learning (Tharayil et al.,   2018 ), partly due to anxiety associated with new approaches (Cooper et al.,   2018 ). These issues can be overcome

activity. The process can be facilitated by using incremental steps in the active learning process (Richardson,   2008 ). Allied with these points is the demonstration that trust in the

Without a learned body of knowledge, a student’s ability to engage with complex con - cepts, at best, limits their opportunities to develop a deep understanding of these ideas.
Some concepts prove difficult for students to learn. At these discrete learning moments, didactic approaches on their own prove ineffective, and other more active learning
approaches are required. An approach to teaching based on choosing appropriate teach - ing methods allows for well-designed, student-owned active learning opportunities when
and where they are most needed and most effective. When designing courses, educators need to consider active learning opportunities in relation to key design parameters such
as the educational context (e.g. online or laboratory classrooms), student and industry needs (e.g. student pathways, prior knowledge, and accreditation requirements), and
structural requirements such as course and program learning outcomes and assessment.
In earlier studies, when course content was mapped against assessment over multiple offerings of courses, the authors noted that significant quantities of content could be

At University X, Biochemistry is a semester 1, foundational 2nd-year core course taken by students enrolled in Laboratory Medicine, Medical Science, Nutrition & Food
### Science, Pharmaceutical Science, Human Movement, and Science degrees. Approxi -
mately 130 students enrol in Biochemistry each year. Before undertaking this course, stu - dents have completed two semesters of chemistry as a pre-requisite. Immunology is a
semester 2, foundational 2nd-year core course for Laboratory Medicine and Medical Science students and an elective for all other cohorts. Approximately 90 students enrol
in Immunology each year. Students will have completed two semesters of biology and
### a semester of physiology as pre-requisite courses.
In Biochemistry and Immunology, the content was delivered via three or 2 lectures respectively, one tutorial and one laboratory session per week, and assessed through lab -
oratory reports, semester quizzes (two in Biochemistry) and (one in Immunology), and an end-of-semester examination. Feedback regarding practical report performance was
provided one week after the report was submitted for assessment. As part of this study, students were encouraged (participation was voluntary), to access the 15-
minute simulation to support their learning. We noted that for the Enzyme kinetics, mAb, and HDN simulations, approximately   ∼ 35%,   ∼ 25% and   ∼ 45% of students did
not engage with the simulation. As a result, the assessment of students who did not engage with the simulations was used as a comparison in performance to those students
who did use the simulations, since every other aspect of their education (lectures, tutorial,
### and laboratory) was identical.
Biochemistry and Immunology introduce complex novel concepts that are found chal - lenging by students, leading to cognitive overload. We describe three interventions, one
for a biochemistry laboratory (Enzyme kinetics) and two Immunology lecture topics
### (mAb production and haemolytic disease of the newborn). The authors noted that
student performance in written laboratory reports and examinations was lower than expected for these topics. We employed interactive simulations as a means of delivering
didactic content and active learning within one approach. Thus allowing the student to visualise the learning situation and through their responses effect the outcomes. We
define a simulation as ‘a model of a real activity, created for training purposes or to solve a challenge’ (Baek,   2009 ) which can be accessed at any time and as often as required.
Simulations are either: operational or conceptual (De Giovanni et al.,   2009 ), with oper - ational simulations teaching procedural skills while conceptual simulations focus on con -
cepts and strategic knowledge. Similarly, they are classified as either low or high fidelity (De Giovanni et al.,   2009 ). Fidelity relates to the degree of realism of the models and the
intended experience of the approach used. Our simulations are low fidelity as they use an

comparable learning outcomes to high fidelity (Massoth et al.,   2019 ; Munshi et al., 2015 ), yet given their lower developmental and financial costs, they provide access and
usability for all students. In contrast, highly realistic, high-fidelity simulations, e.g. a hos - pital mannequin, are limited to the number of students that can access the resource, while
being significantly more expensive to acquire than in-house developed simulations. The research question that underpinned this research was ‘how can interactive simulations
enhance student learning and performance of content typically found challenging by students’.
This paper explores the pedagogical challenges of designing courses that have both a high content load as well as a need for deep learning. It advocates for the careful adop -
tion of complimentary didactic and active learning approaches, specifically using inter - active simulations. Three case studies in two foundational 2nd year science courses
(Biochemistry and Immunology) at University X, illustrate the affordances of compu - ter-based simulation for combining didactic and active learning, supporting students
to manage cognitive load and acquire and apply knowledge using context-specific technologies.
2. Materials and methods
The scope of this paper is a broad, longitudinal evaluative study of the application and impact of content-specific simulations on student learning. The Biochemistry data
cover results from 2014-2020, (seven semesters of data), while the Immunology data were collected between 2016-2019 (four semesters of data). Following institutional
ethics clearance, a mixed methods study was undertaken to explore student learning of enzyme kinetics (Biochemistry), monoclonal antibody production (Immunology),
and haemolytic disease of the newborn (Immunology). For this study, interactive simu - lations were the active learning intervention that enabled an investigation of the impacts
of combining didactic and active approaches. This section now details the methodology: including simulation preparation and quantitative and qualitative analysis of the simu -
lation interventions.
2.1. Simulation preparation Articulate Storyline (v2 & v3) was used to develop the three simulations presented in this study. The simulations present content using a real-life narrative, which reflects strategies
used in case-based learning (Herreid,   1997 ; Merseth,   1996 ). We provide a clear context, a central character, and an educational challenge (Gijbels et al.,   2005 ). The narrative is
accompanied by supporting information that facilitates the scaffolding of knowledge.
An animated character was used to deliver written and audio instructions to students.
To enhance student engagement, the text was converted to audio using an online text- to-speech converter ( www.fromtexttospeech.com ). The simulations then underwent
extensive testing by the teaching team before their release. The simulation was compiled as a sharable content object reference model (SCORM) and loaded onto the University
Moodle learning management system (LMS). The LMS analytics provided information including which students had attempted the simulation when the simulation was used,

4. Discussion
This study sought to understand the potential of interactive simulations to support stu - dents challenged by complex content matter. Our intention was to explore the interface

---

INTERNATIONAL JOURNAL OF SCIENCE EDUCATION   185 between didactic and active learning. As indicated, the findings show that for Biochem -
istry there was efficacy in bridging the gap between the didactic delivery of lecture content and the more active learning seen in a physical laboratory session. While for
Immunology, simulations were also able to significantly improve student performance
### in two different lecture topics.
It is critical that when teaching Biochemistry and Immunology the curriculum pro - vides a strong basis on which students can apply their knowledge in more advanced
courses and future careers. Both Biochemistry and Immunology are difficult to learn due to the nature of the terminology used and the complexity of concepts (Anderson
et al.,   2021 ; Lee & Malau-Aduli,   2013 ; Wood,   1990 ) ( Figures 2–5 ) and the require - ment   for   students   to   integrate   knowledge   derived   from   prior   courses,   such   as
anatomy, cell biology, and genetics (Stranford et al.,   2020 ; Wood,   1990 ). As with
### many content-heavy courses with a high cognitive load, biochemistry, and immu -
nology students may adopt a surface learning approach (e.g. rote learning), instead
### of deep learning. This may lead to reduced understanding, knowledge retention,
and poor student performance (Fardilha et al.,   2010 ; Loertscher et al.,   2014 ; Mina - sian-Batmanian et al.,   2005 ; Minderhout & Loertscher,   2007 ; Watters & Watters,
2007 ). To better understand when these periods of high cognitive load occurred, we   mapped   the   curriculum   over   time,   as   represented   in   our   mountain   range
analogy. At times of high cognitive load, represented as peaks, we introduced simu - lations to provide an active learning approach for our students to encourage deeper
### learning.
Our simulations present content using a real-life narrative, which reflects strategies used in case-based learning (Herreid,   1997 ; Merseth,   1996 ). We provided a clear
context, a central character, and an educational challenge (Gijbels et al.,   2005 ), such as generating a mAb to a novel cancer protein or diagnosing the likelihood of HDN. The
narrative was accompanied by supporting information that facilitated the scaffolding of knowledge. As shown in   Figures 2–5 , our simulations include visual aids, animations,
and audio and require student input to progress. Active learning approaches which include a visual, auditory, reading, writing, and kinesthetic component have been
noted as requirements when teaching complex content, such as Immunology. Indeed, in one example, 85% of students preferred a multimodal form of information presen -
tation (Pathare,   2014 ). The kinesthetic aspect can be challenging to achieve in a compu -
### ter-based simulation; however, it can be addressed when a simulation is used in
combination with a face-to-face laboratory exercise. While didactic strategies were efficient and effective in presenting content in lectures (Costabile,   2021 ; Costabile & Tur -
kanovic,   2022 ), the enzyme kinetics simulation was required to support the laboratory
### component where enzyme activity is measured, manipulated and data generated in
real-time. A key aspect of our enzyme kinetics simulation was the generation, evaluation, and linking of data to foundational knowledge. Teaching students how to interrogate
data presented in scientific literature has also benefited from an active learning approach (Johnson,   2014 ). Limited prior experience with active learning can be a limiting factor in
uptake, but through small group work, student presentations and collaborative problem solving, issues can be effectively addressed (Johnson,   2014 ). A similar approach has also
been successfully applied to graduate students enrolled in biochemistry and pharmacy
### programs (Peng,   2017 ).

Others have reported teaching challenges when students are required to learn complex aspects of biochemistry, such as visualising receptor–ligand interactions (Satyanarayana -
jois,   2010 ), protein α-helix structure and function (Loertscher et al.,   2014 ) and electro - static and noncovalent interactions (Cooper & Oliver-Hoyo,   2017 ). In all cases, online
active learning approaches increased student understanding of fundamental biochemis - try (Kerwin,   2019 ; Loertscher et al.,   2014 ; Satyanarayanajois,   2010 ) and immunology
concepts (Cheng et al.,   2014 ; Elliott,   2010 ; Madiraju et al.,   2018 ; Mohan et al.,   2018 ; Stranford et al.,   2020 )
Further benefits were noted when coupled with a concurrent laboratory session. With the   enzyme   kinetics   simulation,   we   noted   two   immediate   benefits   following   its
implementation, firstly, students ceased failing the practical report and secondly students in the lower quartile increased their score by a half grade. This half-grade improvement is
consistent with the average improvement seen in a meta-analysis of 225 studies (Freeman et al.,   2014 ). As demonstrated for the HDN lecture topic, others have also found that per -
formance on examination questions is higher for students who accessed ‘active’ learning modules than for those who did not use the modules, with the most impact noted for
previously low-performing students (Hernick,   2015 ).
5. Conclusion
We have presented three case studies in two separate courses demonstrating that active learning approaches, in the form of simulations, can effectively enhance the learning of
complex content previously delivered using only a didactic approach. Consistent with a published meta-analysis (Schneider & Preckel,   2017 ), the authors conclude that a com -
bination of teacher- and student-centred approaches was more effective than either a purely didactic or teacher-centred approach. We effectively used simulations to
provide active learning opportunities for students to learn complex Biochemical and Immunological concepts. These simulations have positively impacted student learning,
as demonstrated by significant improvements in assessment outcomes, consistent with the benefits of active learning on student performance (Akiha et al.,   2018 ; Hodges,
2020 ; Tomkin et al.,   2019 ). Notably, students in the lower quartile experienced the most significant benefit in using active learning simulations. Given that all students
have a right to excel, the authors suggest that the effect of active learning on all students be investigated, particularly considering the disproportionate effect noted on lower-
performing students in this study and by others (Freeman et al.,   2014 ; Theobald et al.,   2020 ).
