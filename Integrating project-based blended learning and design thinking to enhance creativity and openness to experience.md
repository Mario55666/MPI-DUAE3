IMPACT STATEMENT This study develops the PjBBL-DT model and examines its impact on higher vocational students ’   openness to experience and creativity. A 15-week, three-phase, single-group
quasi-experimental study was conducted with 61 first-year higher vocational students.
The PjBBL approach was implemented in Phase I, followed by the PjBBL-DT interven- tion in Phases II and III. Five experts evaluated the model using a rating form based
on mean and standard deviation. Repeated measures MANOVA was used to analyze its effects on creativity and openness to experience. Results indicate that the PjBBL-DT
model is of high quality, significantly enhancing creativity with a retention effect.
While it also significantly impacts openness to experience, the retention effect is not evident. These findings confirm that the PjBBL-DT model is an effective pedagogical
approach   for   fostering   creativity   and   openness   in   vocational   entrepreneurship education.
1. Introduction
Entrepreneurship education (EE) is a powerful tool to promote economic development and poverty alle- viation (Naminse & Zhuang, 2018). EE accelerates pathways into entrepreneurship by influencing atti-
tudes, expanding knowledge (He et al., 2024) and developing competencies (Klarin et al., 2021; Vidal et al., 2021), and shaping entrepreneurial mindsets (Del Vecchio et al., 2021; Handayati et al., 2020;
Suguna   et   al.,   2024).   Since   the   Chinese   government   launched   the   ‘ Mass   Entrepreneurship   and Innovation ’   policy, universities and colleges provided EE for all students to create a new impetus for
sustained economic   growth, and   turn more   than 4 million   higher vocational   graduates   each year (Ministry of Education of the PRC, 2023) into an innovative workforce rather than a burden on the job
market.

Many universities and colleges continue to rely on traditional learning methods in EE (Cui et al., 2021; Huang et al., 2021), which primarily focus on theoretical explanations and case studies but fail to effect-
ively engage students in hands-on learning (Li, 2024). Student-centered learning can facilitate creative thinking (Shehata et al., 2024), and experiential learning enhances students ’   entrepreneurial abilities sig-
nificantly (Bonesso et al., 2018). Digital tools are becoming increasingly critical for developing entrepre- neurial   skills   (Ratten   &   Usmanij,   2021),   and   using   technological   platforms   has   grown   increasingly
important for EE (Minogue, 2017). Therefore, there is an urgent need to develop a learning approach for EE. This study integrated project-based blended learning (PjBBL) and design thinking (DT) as scaffolding
within EE. On the one hand, DT is effective in fostering creativity (Ladachart et al., 2024), Additionally, PjBBL can also increase creativity (Alamri, 2021; Chua & Islam, 2021; Kalam, 2020), and the integration of
DT in project-based learning has been proven to enhance creativity (Jiang & Pang, 2023; Lee et al., 2019). On the other hand, DT has performed well in developing components of OE (Arifin & Mahmud,
2021; Stock et al., 2016; Vassallo et al., 2023). Moreover, the integration of DT in project-based learning has been proven to further enhance these components (Jiang & Pang, 2023). Furthermore, DT-PBL has
been less commonly used in education and has demonstrated promising potential for the future (Jia et al., 2023). Hence, the integration of PjBBL and DT has great potential to promote both OE and
creativity.
Research on EE has primarily focused on entrepreneurial intentions (EI) (Fu et al., 2022; Iwu et al., 2021; Li, 2024; Xu et al., 2023), entrepreneurial mindset (Cui et al., 2021), entrepreneurial competencies
(Anubhav et al., 2024; Hahn et al., 2020), and barriers (Liu et al., 2021). However, there is insufficient research on Openness to experience (OE) and creativity in higher vocational colleges (Fan et al., 2024) in
the Chinese EE context. OE is one of the Big Five personality traits (Sparkman, 2020). Individuals with increased OE are more likely to be creative (Guo et al., 2021). OE is a strong predictor of EI (Araujo et al.,
2023). and has a positive impact on career exploration (Li et al., 2015; Reed et al., 2004). To continuously acquire new knowledge and skills in an increasingly digital economy, we need to maintain openness to
lifelong learning (OECD, 2024). Creativity is an important skill for the 21st century (Geisinger, 2016), and one of the top five global skills (World Economic Forum, 2021). Creativity is an antecedent of EI
(Tantawy   et   al.,   2021),   and   plays   a   significant   role   in   career   development   (Machali   et   al.,   2021).
Therefore, individuals should place greater emphasis on creativity to adapt to future challenges (OECD, 2019). In China, 70% of students in higher vocational colleges come from rural areas, with 52% originat-
ing from   ‘ farmer and migrant worker ’   families (Lian, 2015) and lower-income families (Lavelle, 2021).
Many of these students were left-behind children, with 66.63% raised by their grandparents (Wu, 2022).
Those students often exhibited lower levels of creativity (Pang et al., 2020), and reduced OE (Atherton et al., 2024). Another issue is that due to the emphasis on entrepreneurship competitions (ECS) in col-
leges (Zhou, 2018), creativity is primarily fostered among a small group of top students, while the major- ity are overlooked (Fan et al., 2024). To adapt to new challenges and opportunities, students in higher
vocational colleges must enhance their OE and creativity, enabling them to achieve employability, decent work, and entrepreneurial skills. These improvements align with the quality education goals out-
lined in the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs).
Studies on EE in higher vocational colleges have mainly focused on eastern China (Fu et al., 2022; Huang, 2024; Lavelle, 2021; Zeng et al., 2023), with less research in the western region. It is meaning-
ful to study EE in differentiated colleges (Deng & Wang, 2023). The development of EE in western China is relatively slow (Huang et al., 2021). Given its critical role in economic development and
employment, enhancing EE in this region is essential (Neal, 2017). Furthermore, EE is applicable across all   disciplines.   Exploring   how   EE   can   motivate students   in   non-business   disciplines   is   a   promising
research area with high potential (Ratten & Usmanij, 2021). As agriculture is both China ’ s primary industry and a key pillar of Guizhou Province ’ s economy, research on EE in agricultural disciplines is
particularly crucial.
As a teacher of EE in higher vocational colleges in western China, I am concerned with both the effectiveness of the courses and the career development of the students. To the best of the author ’ s
knowledge, there is currently a lack of research on learning methods integrating PjBBL with DT, particu- larly regarding the enhancement of creativity and OE among students in higher vocational colleges in
western China. This study evaluates creativity and OE, contributing to a deeper understanding of this

2. Research question
This study aims to address the lack of research on learning models, OE, and creativity in EE at higher vocational colleges. Therefore, this study intends to address the following questions:
RQ1. How to develop a PjBBL-DT learning model to foster OE and creativity in EE?
RQ2. Does the PjBBL-DT learning model significantly enhance students ’   OE and creativity in EE?
3. Literature review
3.1. Project-based blended learning (PjBBL) As educational technology advances, more students and educators are choosing blended entrepreneur-
ship programs as their primary learning approach (Chen et al., 2021). Blended learning has become a standard practice in China (Yang et al., 2022). Developers have continuously generated and updated
numerous EE applications. As a student-centered blended learning approach, PjBBL enables students to manage both online and offline project-based learning (Mursid et al., 2022), and allows students to man-
age online and offline learning of project works (Kabatiah et al., 2021). It is considered the most effective and efficient learning model for its advantages of both face-to-face learning and online learning (Suana
et al., 2023). However, PjBBL requires extensive online learning resources and a longer implementation period, making it less accessible to students with weaker foundational skills (Tong et al., 2020). This
study adapted the learning model of Dai et al. (2021) which categorizes four learning steps: (1) select the project topics, (2) make the implantation project plan, (3) implement the project plan, and (4) evalu-
ate the result of the project. This study modified the first step by dividing it into two phases: project perception and project definition, facilitating its integration with DT.
PjBBL provides a unique framework for developing 21st-century skills in the classroom. It enhances student engagement and motivation (Distyasa et al., 2021), fosters self-directed learning (Alamri, 2021;
Tong et al., 2020), and cultivates essential skills, such as critical thinking (Eliyasni et al., 2019), problem- solving (Chua & Islam, 2021), and creativity (Alamri, 2021).
3.2. Entrepreneurship education (EE) in higher vocational college Entrepreneurship is a key driver of economic growth as it generates technological innovation, increases
economic efficiency, and creates new jobs. Unlike students in top universities, vocational college stu- dents primarily engage in EE to secure employment or establish small businesses aligned with their
vocational skills, rather than pursuing technologically cutting-edge ventures (Alshurideh et al., 2022).
Therefore, the objective of EC in vocational colleges and universities is to cultivate students ’   emotional intelligence, improve their entrepreneurial self-efficacy, and prepare them to start a business, create eco-
nomic value, and develop entrepreneurship.
Despite the growing importance of EE in higher vocational colleges in China, researchers have con- ducted few empirical studies. Existing studies primarily examine students ’   attitudes (Lavelle, 2021), and
self-efficacy, which indirectly influence EI and are considered the first step toward entrepreneurship.
Limited research examines EE approaches in vocational colleges. Most programs emphasize classroom instruction over practical experience, relying on monotonous teaching methods. As a result, it cannot
cultivate students ’   innovation and creativity (Zhou et al., 2024). In the context of Mass Innovation and Entrepreneurship policy and the large number of students in higher vocational colleges, this is undoubt-
edly a huge gap, and empirical research on the effectiveness of EE in higher vocational colleges seems to be more meaningful.

### 3.3. Design thinking (DT)
Brown (2008) defines DT as a discipline aligns designers ’   sensibility and methods with technological feasibility and viable business strategies to create customer value and market opportunities. Kelley and
Kelley (2013) describe DT as a method for identifying human needs and developing solutions through design practitioners ’   tools and mindsets. In contrast, Martin (2009) emphasizes the thinking element,
defining DT as   ‘ the productive mix of analytical thinking and intuitive thinking ’ . This shows the core of DT, which starts from human needs and utilizes appropriate technology, aiming to create entrepreneur-
ial value through customer value. DT commonly appears in three forms: mindsets, processes, and tool- boxes (Brenner et al., 2016). DT is presented in the form of a process in this study as it is more suitable
for novices. This study adopts the D.School model, one of the most influential DT frameworks, which consists of five stages: empathy, define, ideate, prototype, and test. At the same time, it will also use the
tools and methods of DT to promote students ’   learning effectiveness.
DT is a student-centered approach that fosters creativity (Kalam, 2020), enhances critical thinking skills (Hashim et al., 2019; Rusmann & Ejsing-Duun, 2022), promotes collaboration (Pulyavina et al., 2022). It
also addresses real-world challenges (Silva & Zancul, 2023), encourages risk-taking and iterative learning (Kalam, 2020), supports metacognition (Rusmann & Ejsing-Duun, 2022). DT can be time-consuming and
resource-intensive, and difficult to identify clear goals and outcomes (Pereira et al., 2021). This can be challenging for educators and institutions with limited resources (Pulyavina et al., 2022). DT focuses on
generating   new   ideas,   exploring   alternative   solutions,   and   analyzing   and   evaluating   the   solution (Woraphiphat & Roopsuwankun, 2023), and applies to EE in vocational schools (Pratomo et al., 2021).
However, related research is very limited.
### 3.4. Openness to experience (OE)
OE is one of the Big Five personality dimensions (Sparkman, 2020). It has been seen in the breadth, depth, and permeability of consciousness, and in the recurrent need to enlarge and examine experience
(McCrae & Costa, 1997), which means someone who is intellectually curious and tends to seek new experiences and explore novel ideas. Curiosity, imagination, intellect, OE, and originality all stem from
this core trait (McCrae, 1987). Openness is closely related to having a flexible cognitive style in dealing with problems, i.e. being able to   ‘ think outside the box ’   (McCrae, 1987), and have a wide range of ideas,
feelings, and problem-solving strategies, which may lead to novel and useful solutions or ideas (Feist, 1998). Openness to emotional states leads to divergent thinking skills of free association, breadth of
attention, and fluidity of thought, as well as mobile aggregation and cognitive flexibility (Feist, 1998).
This study adopts Alderotti et al. (2023) definition of OE, which encompasses imagination, creativity, curi- osity, and unconventional thinking. It consists of the following factors: imagination, artistic interests,
emotionality, adventurousness, intellect, and liberalism for the good reliability (Maples-Keller et al., 2019).
OE can be increased through multicultural experiences (Sparkman et al., 2016). DT performs well in terms of adventurousness, curiosity, imaginativeness, and challenge, which are all related to OE, suggest-
ing the ability to think quickly and generate ideas from the user ’ s point of view to generate ideas (Vassallo et al., 2023). On this basis, the role of DT can be extended to the influence of the five personal-
ities (Stock et al., 2016). This is an encouragement for OE. Most research on OE adopts a psychological perspective, with limited studies exploring its promotion in education.
### 3.5. Creativity
According to the Oxford Dictionary (2023), creativity is the use of skill and imagination to produce some- thing new or create art. Sternberg and Lubart (1998) offer a simple definition: creativity is the ability to
create work that is both novel and appropriate. Treffinger et al. (2002) analyzed 120 definitions and identified common characteristics of creativity. Creativity can be learned. Anyone can be creative to
some extent (Sternberg & Lubart, 1998), and this has very important implications for education. This sug- gests that creativity is not limited to highly intelligent or gifted individuals but is accessible to all

There has been some research on promoting students ’   creativity through PjBBL which focuses on engineering education (Chua & Islam, 2021; Jiang & Pang, 2023), physical education (Nopiyanto et al.,

This study analyzed students ’   needs and reviewed the literature to design a model integrating PjBBL with DT. It then developed the model structure, established implementation steps for a one-group
experiment, and evaluated the results.

### 4.3. Measurement
This study invited five experts in education, psychology, and entrepreneurship to evaluate the PjBBL-DT model using an evaluation form. The evaluation form includes three aspects: content rationality, technol-
ogy support, and practice process, with four items in each aspect, and a total of 12 items.
A 12-item self-assessment, which adapted from Maples-Keller et al. (2019), was used in the study. This instrument comprised six parts: imagination, artistic interests, emotionality, adventurousness, intellect,
and liberalism. These questions were scored on a Likert scale ranging from 1 (strongly disagree) to 5 (strongly agree). This study assessed creativity using rubrics adapted from Vincent-Lancrin et al. (2019).
The rubrics evaluate four aspects of creativity: imagining, inquiry, doing, and reflecting, offering a com- prehensive assessment of both the process and product of creative endeavors, six criteria in total. The
rubrics categorized creativity into four levels: outstanding, excellent, emergent, and dormant, in descend- ing order.
Since all participants were Chinese, we translated the OE and creativity instruments into Chinese and assessed their content validity using the Item-Objective Congruence (IOC) index. The IOC values were
0.91 for OE and 0.95 for creativity, indicating acceptable validity. We confirmed the reliability of the OE instrument with a Cronbach ’ s alpha of 0.80. For creativity assessment, two instructors independently
evaluated 19 students using the creativity rubrics. The Pearson correlation coefficient was 0.864, indicat- ing good reliability.

### 4.4. Data collection
Figure 3 illustrates the experimental activities conducted in this study. Sixty-one students participated in a single-group, three-phase experiment, with each phase lasting five weeks and including 90 min of
learning per week. The same instructor facilitated all phases. Wenda Deng wrote the course textbook, which the Ministry of Education of China approved as a 13th Five-Year Plan textbook. Table 2 shows the
course schedule.
In phase I, this study utilized a learning PjBBL approach which was adapted from Dai et al. (2021).
The model consists of five steps: project perception, define the project, make the project implementa- tion plan, implement the project plan, evaluate the result of project. Each learning project was imple-
mented according to the model in five steps. The learning activities, such as online micro-learning, assigning, discussing, and submitting project tasks were mainly completed by the Superstar learning
platform. The first week began with a brief introduction to the course (the PjBBL methodology, OE self- assessment form, and creativity rubrics). At the end of the fifth week, the pre-test of OE and creativity
was completed after the learning of the Market Research I. Wenjuanxing collected the OE data, provid- ing a professional and convenient data collection method. The Superstar learning platform collected the
creativity data. Wenjuanxing and Superstar learning platform are widely used in China. Students are familiar with Wenjuanxing and Superstar, eliminating the need for training.
In phase II, the study implemented the PjBBL-DT approach, incorporating DT introductions and activ- ities on the Boardmix APP. At the end of the tenth week, the post-test of OE and creativity was com-
pleted with the learning results of Products & Services IV. The DT online course was based on Innovative Thinking taught by Associate Professor Wei Wu at Shenzhen Polytechnic University. This course is recog-
nized as a high-quality online learning resource by the Ministry of Education in China. The online course provided students with an initial understanding of the steps and methods of DT, preparing them for the
offline follow-up PjBBL of DT integration.
In phase III, the PjBBL-DT approach was still used. At the end of the fifteenth week, the retention test of OE and creativity were completed with the learning results of Business Plan Roadshow.

4.5. Data analysis For RQ1, experts evaluated the PjBBL-DT model using an evaluation form, utilizing means and standard deviations (SD) for analysis. For RQ2, this study evaluated the impact and retention of the PjBBL-DT

### 5.1.3. The result of PjBBL-DT model implementation
The Products and Services project was a key component of the course and lasted four weeks. The learn- ing objectives of this project were to develop a sense of innovation and information literacy, to master
the classification of products and services and the methods of differentiation, to design differentiated products and services, and skillfully use technology.
During the pre-class period, the instructor assigned learning tasks utilizing the Superstar learning plat- form. Micro-videos were organized following the five stages of DT, focusing on the development of
products and services. Each video lasts 5 – 10 min, allowing students to learn in short sessions (Ding, 2017).
During the in-class period, the activities undertaken by both the teacher and the students were as follows: 5.1.3.1. Empathy/project perception.   The teacher focused on establishing the problem situation and
introducing   the   project   topics.   In   the   first   week,   students   were   organized   into   nine   groups   and
selected   their   respective   project   topics.   For   the   ‘ Products   and   Services ’   project,   the   teacher   first
explained the learning objectives and creativity rubrics. Subsequently, the teacher reminded students to download, register for, and familiarize themselves with the Boardmix application. Following this, the
teacher provided a brief overview of the learning resources, addressed the users ’   demands for specific types of products, and lectured on the classification of products and services. The teacher also pre-
sented case studies on agricultural products and assigned a brainstorming task for students to discuss their projects ’   core   values and functions. Finally,   students submitted and shared their findings by
platform.
5.1.3.2. Define project.   This step clarified the project ’ s objectives and scope. This phase is informed by preliminary market research and the identified needs of primary users, specifically students, who are
tasked with designing differentiated products and services that fulfill core functions. Instructors provide lectures on the method of differentiated product design and assign group tasks to facilitate collaborative

6. Discussions
6.1. Discussions of the PjBBL-DT model Based on the observations from the implementation of Phase I, students generally adapted well to the
project tasks and completed them within the designated timeframe. Students also expressed enjoyment during the project presentations, and there was an increase in team communication on the learning
platform, aligning with the findings of Dai et al. (2021). However, three aspects of blended learning in the PjBBL model were lacking, which DT could effectively address. Firstly, student participation was
insufficient in PjBBL. Some students contributed minimally during team discussions due to a lack of self- confidence, anxiety, or fear of expressing ineffective ideas. Implementing DT tools, such as brainstorm-
ing, could enhance the quality of discussions by encouraging students to share more ideas and foster creativity (Gong et al., 2021; Paulus et al., 2023). Second, the outcomes of the project tasks were moder-
ate in PjBBL. Boardmix, as a mediated tool, could stimulate students ’   interest and allow them to actively construct meaning, leading to more meaningful results (Liu et al., 2024). Lastly, fear of failure and mak-
ing mistakes was prevalent in PjBBL. The DT process, however, encourages trial and error, promoting tol- erance for ambiguity (Micheli et al., 2019). Students showed greater willingness to express themselves,
take risks, learn from failures, and develop resilience (R osch et al., 2023). € The integration of PjBBL with DT is supported by well-established educational theories, providing a
strong framework for developing industry-relevant skills, entrepreneurial mindsets, and innovation capa- bilities in various educational contexts. This approach aligns with constructivist learning theories, particu-
larly those of Piaget and Vygotsky, which emphasize active knowledge construction through meaningful activities to develop higher psychological functions (Liu et al., 2024). By engaging in real-world, collab-
orative projects, learners gain practical experience while cultivating a proactive, problem-solving orienta- tion essential for professional success.
6.2. Discussions of the impact of the PjBBL-DT model 6.2.1. Discussion of the impact on OE The PjBBL-DT model had a positive impact on OE, consistent with Sparkman (2020), who found that
although OE as a personality trait is relatively stable, it can increase in appropriate contexts. The reten- tion effect was statistically insignificant. OE was assessed through self-evaluation, and after the post-test,
20 students participated in specialized entrepreneurship training in preparation for the school-level ECS.
This may explain the lack of statistical significance in the retention phase. The OE performance was slightly higher in males, which aligns with the findings of Sutu et al.. Students from rural areas reported
higher OE than those from urban areas, contrary to the findings of Atherton et al. (2024). Rural-born stu- dents were more open-minded and persevered in their studies to change the status quo, which aligns
with the findings of Lavelle (2021). However, the sample size of students from urban areas was small (only seven students, or 11.48%), and further research is needed to explore this discrepancy.
The PjBBL-DT model demonstrated positive outcomes across five dimensions of OE: imagination, artis- tic interests, emotionality, adventurousness, and intellect. In terms of imagination, the model provided
real-world project tasks within a blended learning environment and utilized the technology platform Boardmix to facilitate imaginative thinking. This approach aligns with Dijk and Rietveld (2025), and

### 6.2.2. Discussion of the impact on creativity
The results suggest that the PjBBL-DT model significantly fostered creativity among college students, with similarly favorable retention outcomes. This aligns with Zhong et al. (2024), who found that instruc-
tional models can enhance creativity. Gender differences in creativity levels were minimal, with differen- ces of   < 10%, supporting the findings of Taylor et al. (2024). There was a correlation between major and
creativity, with students majoring in Tea being more creative than those in Animal Science. Tea majors benefit from courses related to tea art performance, which enhances their artistic development. This
finding aligns with Aguilera and Ortiz-Revilla (2021) and Paulsen et al. (2021). These differences in cre- ativity can be attributed to variations in curriculum structure and learning experiences across disciplines.
Economic status did not correlate positively with creativity levels, which contrasts with the findings of Saleem et al. (2024). This may be due to the fact that most participants came from lower-income families
(Lavelle, 2021), and the income gap was not significant.
The PjBBL-DT model employed techniques, tools, materials, and learning methods that foster creativ- ity, consistent with Machali et al. (2021). Regarding its components, the model demonstrated positive
outcomes across four dimensions. The promotion of imagination, discussed in the OE section, will not be reiterated here. In terms of inquiry, the model required students to engage with online learning
resources at their own pace (Chua & Islam, 2021; Santoso et al., 2023). Students participated in inquiry activities collaboratively, consulting industry reports based on their rural experiences, learning objectives,
and real-life situations. This approach facilitated greater interest (Li & Zhu, 2023) and a deeper under- standing of the project ’ s background and requirements. It also developed skills related to their percep-
tions of the surrounding environment (Badia, 2017). Creativity enhancement can be attributed to the synergy between high levels of openness and attentiveness to peers ’   ideas (Pi et al., 2022). Moreover,
addressing meaningful industry problems stimulated creativity within teams (Joung, 2014).
In terms of doing, the 10-week implementation included 12 group activities, particularly prototyping, which required experimenting with different solutions, trial and error, and iteration (Tong et al., 2020)
using Boardmix. These activities strengthened the link between theory and practice (Chua & Islam, 2021) and enhanced problem-solving skills. Regarding reflecting, the model included presentation and assess-
ment stages, with teachers serving as assessors. Teacher assessments focused on encouragement and appreciation, motivating students to resubmit assignments for higher scores. This approach encouraged
students to reflect on their ideas and create something new and useful (Pratomo et al., 2021).
Teachers ’   pedagogical beliefs, instructional strategies, and behaviors are influential external factors that significantly impact students ’   attitudes toward creativity (Fredagsvik, 2023; Machali et al., 2021). This
aligns with Dewey ’ s experiential education theory, which emphasizes that educators ’   experiences and

7. Limitations and implications
7.1. Limitations First, students with weak learning foundations faced challenges. In the two groups, most students came from secondary vocational schools with a weak academic background. After two weeks of observation, the
instructor noted that students struggled with the learning process. In response, hierarchical tasks were introduced in subsequent assignments. When submitting assignments, students had the option to design
prototypes using Boardmix or present objects, encouraging greater participation in the learning tasks.
Second, the implementation was time-consuming. The PjBBL-DT model typically involves complex project tasks that require considerable time to complete. For example, the product and service project
spanned four weeks, including four steps: product and service features, differentiated design, prototype development, and testing and evaluation. To ensure systematic knowledge acquisition, certain content,
such as entrepreneurial resources and risks, could only be addressed through online learning and briefly reviewed in offline courses.
Lastly, interdisciplinary collaboration proved challenging. While the PjBBL-DT model encourages inter- disciplinary work, it is a compulsory course organized by the university, making it difficult to have stu-
dents from multiple disciplines in the same class. However, such collaboration was achievable when participating in competitive practice.
7.2. Implications This study has several implications. First, it introduces the PjBBL-DT learning model, establishing a novel
approach and theoretical foundation to meet the practical and vocational demands of higher vocational education. The model provides a new framework for integrating theory and practice, enabling higher voca-
tional institutions to cultivate applied talents capable of addressing real-world challenges. Second, further investigation of creativity within the framework of higher vocational education is essential. This context
provides a unique opportunity to explore creativity and contributes to understanding its components, such as imagining, inquiry, doing, and reflecting. Lastly, enhancing research on Openness to Experience
(OE) is critical. This study offers a fresh perspective on OE ’ s role in educational practices, promoting entre- preneurship and offering a practical examination of entrepreneurship development within curricula.
8. Conclusions
This study addresses the identified research gaps by introducing the PjBBL-DT model, which meets cur- rent needs in EE. It expands the research scope by emphasizing the importance of fostering students ’
OE and creativity, positioning these as key drivers in developing 21st-century skills. Additionally, this study adds empirical research on EE in higher vocational colleges in western China, drawing attention to
students in less developed regions and providing practical experience for EE in these areas.
The PjBBL-DT model emphasizes innovation and problem-solving, overcoming the limitations of tradi- tional higher vocational education, which primarily focuses on technical skills. It facilitates the shift from a
skill-oriented to an innovation-driven approach. By providing real-world problems for students to solve using resources and technology through teamwork, the model aligns with the core principles of contextual

9. Suggestions
The current assessment instruments for OE and creativity may not fully capture students ’   performance.
Future research should focus on developing assessment tools that are better suited for higher vocational students, particularly those capable of dynamically monitoring progress. For instance, creative assess-
ments, such as behavioral observations and open-ended tasks, should be explored further. Additionally, future studies should employ longitudinal tracking to examine the impact of the PjBBL-DT model on the
career development and continuous learning capabilities of higher vocational students.
Moreover, it is important to assess the effects of the PjBBL-DT model across various disciplinary con- texts, such as elective courses and training, to explore its potential in facilitating multidisciplinary learning.
The PjBBL-DT model requires students to engage creatively, participate in discussions, and share ideas. Therefore, it is recommended to keep class sizes small, with 5 – 6 participants per group, to ensure
meaningful learning outcomes. Additionally, as this study utilized a single-group experimental design, it would be valuable to examine the model ’ s effects in experiments involving two or more groups. Finally,
we encourage future research to use mixed research methods. While quantitative data provides valuable insights into OE and creativity, qualitative data could offer a deeper understanding of participants ’   sub-
jective experiences and the contextual factors influencing their OE and creativity.
