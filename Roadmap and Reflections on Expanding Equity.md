Keywords:   accessibility | curriculum | diversity | equity | inclusivity | indigeneity | mapping ABSTRACT Nurturing learning systems that are respectful and welcoming to diverse individuals is a step towards improving the expe-
rience of all students in pharmacology and toxicology (Pharm- Tox). This paper evaluates the Pharm- Tox curriculum at the
University of Toronto with a critical lens towards the incorporation of content that is Equitable, Diverse, Inclusive, acknowl-
edges Indigeneity, and is Accessible (EDIIA). A curriculum mapping approach examined the undergraduate Pharm- Tox cur- riculum to identify EDIIA gaps and areas for improvement. Key stakeholders that contributed to the curriculum mapping
process were undergraduate students, teaching faculty, and an external research associate who identified EDIIA themes used
to evaluate existing course materials. The curriculum map identified areas to improve EDIIA integration in individual courses
and resulted in the design of course- specific recommendations. Centrally housed department resources were also developed
to mitigate barriers to faculty implementation of the EDIIA recommendations. These resources included an internal EDIIA handbook on appropriate language in the classroom, a guide to creating accessible and inclusive PowerPoint slides, and a pre-
course survey to identify the student population and their needs. Resources were well received by faculty, and to assess the im-
pact of EDIIA recommendations on student learning, ongoing review of curricular changes will be conducted through student
surveys. The recommendations from this curriculum mapping process encourage faculty to explore opportunities for EDIIA integration in the undergraduate Pharm- Tox curriculum with the goal of strengthening the existing curriculum and improving
the student learning experience.

EDIIA- focused curricular changes train students to conduct and apply Pharm- Tox knowledge and research more equitably and
be aware of systemic inequities that persist in the healthcare and pharmaceutical industries [2–4]. Nurturing learning sys-
tems that are respectful and welcoming to diverse individuals is a step toward improving the experience and retention of un-
derrepresented minorities in Pharm- Tox; ultimately improving the learning experiences of students, including those who are
underrepresented, and supporting equitable admission [5, 6].
Research has shown that students of color who found curricula to be culturally insensitive averted contact with instructors and
showed a lack of interest in their program [6]. Reconstruction of curricula through an EDIIA lens improves engagement and ac-
ademic achievement of traditionally underrepresented students [7, 8]. Our strategy for strengthening an EDIIA- centered cur-
riculum is two- fold: the design and delivery of EDIIA- focused course material and the improved implementation of equitable
teaching practices that are both inclusive and accessible.
With these priorities in mind, our goal was to examine the un- dergraduate Pharm- Tox curriculum to identify EDIIA gaps as
well as areas for improvement. Curriculum mapping and de- velopment is an effective tool used to visualize and understand
the traits and outcomes of program curricula [9, 10]. In the con- text of EDIIA, curriculum mapping can be a powerful tool to
promote an inclusive learning environment while bolstering the quality of education and leading to improved program out-
comes [7]. An inclusive curriculum enhances the student's sense of belonging, improves retention in science, normalizes the ex-
pectation of inclusive research practices, and prepares the next generation of scientists to adopt these practices in their scientific
research [11, 12]. Curriculum mapping is a reflective and data- driven process that provides a holistic overview of the program

TABLE 1   |   Descriptions of department and course resources.
### Internal EDIIA handbook
### Target audience: course instructors
Content: Statement of the Department's position on the importance of EDIIA integration into Pharm- Tox learning. Introduction
to an open framework in teaching with best practices on how to discuss key EDIIA- related topics in the classroom. Guidance
around inclusive terminology was sourced from existing peer- reviewed literature and post- secondary EDIIA resources. Topics
include race and ethnicity, Indigenous peoples, gender and identity, sexual orientation, varying levels of abilities, mental health,
age, and socio- economic status. List of recommended readings on integration of EDIIA in teaching and learning.
### EDIIA PowerPoint guide
### Target audience: course instructors
Content: Example- based guide to demonstrate the key elements of accessible PowerPoints. These elements include perceivability
(effects of color, font and text size), usability (ability of students to interact with and use course materials), understandability
(ensuring there is no alternative understanding), and robustness (ensuring accessibility of all content on a variety of browsers,
### devices, and assistive technologies).
### Individual course summaries and recommendations
### Target audience: course instructors
Content: A guide outlining how course- specific content maps onto EDIIA curricular themes that were identified as part of the
curriculum mapping process. The guide also recommends how pertinent course material could be taught through an EDIIA lens, alongside new EDIIA topics that could be explored. A list of relevant research papers was also provided to support the
EDIIA interventions, helping to reduce the time required for implementation and offering a starting point for the necessary
### changes.
### Teaching assistant (TA) resources
### Target audience: TAs/course instructors
### Content: A compact version of the Internal EDIIA Handbook targeted to TA needs.
### EDIIA resources for undergraduates
### Target audience: undergraduate students
Content: Introduction to EDIIA with further reading suggestions around scholarship on key EDIIA topics. These topics include population diversity, sex differences, pharmacogenomics, inclusive approaches to research, the patient experience,
Indigenous populations and medicine, racial and ethnic minorities, cultural and socioeconomic status differences, and policy-
level intervention. A collated list of EDIIA student support resources that are available on campus, including but not limited to,
financial aid, academic support, community support, student wellness, accessibility services, and equity training.
