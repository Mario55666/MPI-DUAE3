### 1   Introduction
Currently, there has been a growing emphasis on experiential and service-based learn - ing, particularly in fields involving environmental awareness, conservation and ani -
mal welfare. Conservation education research has consistently shown that meaningful hands-on experiences are more effective in fostering pro-environmental attitudes and
long-term behavioural change compared to passive forms of learning [ 17 ,   41 ,   47 ]. Zoos in particular are recognised as key institutions for informal conservation education that
offer opportunities for direct engagement with animals and their care, which can sig - nificantly influence visitors’ knowledge, attitudes and sense of stewardship [ 23 ,   32 ]. One
notable platform for such engagement is through structured volunteer programmes conducted by zoological institutions, which serve not only as educational platforms
but also as gateways to inspiring future conservation advocates. Zoo Negara’s volun - teer programme in Malaysia is one such initiative that invites members of the public,
especially youth, to participate in zoo-keeping activities as part of a one-day immersive experience (Zoo [ 49 ]). Participants assist with tasks such as food preparation, enclosure
maintenance, and animal observation to gain first-hand insight into the complexities and responsibilities of animal care. [ 9 ] reported that this hands-on approach may provide a
rare and valuable window into the operational realities of animal care while simultane - ously promoting a deeper appreciation for wildlife and the role of zoos in conservation
and education.
Volunteerism in zoos plays a vital role in bridging public understanding of animal wel - fare, conservation, and the operational needs of zoological institutions [ 7 ,   39 ]. According
to [ 40 ], direct experiences in naturalistic settings such as zoos can positively influence pro-environmental attitudes and behaviours. Volunteer programmes are increasingly
utilised to foster community involvement while also enhancing learning through real- world engagement [ 1 ,   19 ]. Zoo-based volunteer initiatives were shown to increase par -
ticipants’ knowledge of animal behaviour, care routines, and conservation practices [ 7 ].
However, the effectiveness of such programmes often depends on the structure of the experience, namely, whether volunteers feel they are making meaningful contributions
or are appropriately supported by staff [ 7 ]. Rose and Riley [ 37 ] further noted that with the rising popularity of social media and a heightened public interest in wildlife, zoos
had also begun to reposition themselves not only as recreational destinations but as plat - forms for informal learning and community involvement.
According to [ 22 ], experiential learning in volunteer settings occurs when participants are actively involved in tasks that connect theory to practice, such as animal husbandry,
feeding or environmental enrichment. Satisfaction in experiential learning environ - ments is driven not only by the activity itself but also by the degree of engagement, sup -
port received, and perceived educational value [ 25 ]. Studies have shown that structured programmes with clear objectives, variety in tasks, and consistent staff guidance lead
to greater volunteer satisfaction [ 10 ,   43 ]. On the contrary, repetitive or unclear work, limited contact with animals or unprofessional staff behaviour can negatively impact the
volunteer experience [ 11 ]. Zoo Negara’s KeeperKu programme serves as an innovative attempt to align with these expectations by offering participants a chance to interact
directly with animals and understand the behind-the-scenes efforts of zoo staff. How - ever, while the programme has gained traction and attracted numerous participants,

who experience it first-hand, the volunteers.
Despite its growing participation rate, there is no empirical research assessing the actual experiences of volunteers in the KeeperKu programme. Current study suggests
that while many participants enjoy the novelty and learning opportunity, others express dissatisfaction regarding the nature of assigned tasks, limited interaction with animals,
and inconsistent staff professionalism. These mixed experiences raise important ques - tions about the overall quality, structure, and learning outcomes of the programme.
Without systematic evaluation, Zoo Negara risks overlooking key areas for improvement that could enhance volunteer satisfaction, engagement, and retention. Therefore, this
research aims to analyse volunteer feedback to identify recurring themes in their experi - ences with the KeeperKu programme, assess perceived strengths and weaknesses of the
programme from the participants’ point of view, and evaluate the impact of staff interac - tion, task variety, and animal engagement on overall volunteer satisfaction. By gaining
a deeper understanding of how these factors influence volunteer perceptions, the study offers data-driven recommendations to enhance the design, delivery, and management
of the KeeperKu programme. It is hoped that this study will contribute to the ongoing improvement of volunteer-based initiatives in zoological institutions, ensuring that such
programmes not only support zoo operations but also provide meaningful, educational, and rewarding experiences for participants. These insights can guide Zoo Negara and
similar organisations in creating more engaging and effective volunteer environments, ultimately fostering stronger connections between the public and wildlife conservation
efforts.
### 2   Methodology
The volunteer initiative at Zoo Negara was first introduced in 2000 under the name “Volunteer Programme”, which was open to all members of the public. In 2020, the pro -
gramme was restructured and rebranded as the KeeperKu Programme with participa - tion now limited to students aged 16 and above. This adjustment was made to prioritise
the safety of participants, as zoo-keeping tasks may pose risks to younger students and children. On average, the programme attracts up to 12,000 participants annually, with
the participation fee during the period of this study set at RM37 (USD 8.81), highlight - ing its popularity and educational value. While KeeperKu is exclusively for students, Zoo
Negara also offers a separate Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR) volunteering plat - form tailored for working adults and company groups, thereby providing opportunities
for broader community engagement in zoo-based conservation efforts. Volunteers for the KeeperKu Programme are recruited primarily through continuous promotion on
Zoo Negara’s official platforms, including its website and social media channels such as Instagram, TikTok and Facebook. The programme is open to all eligible students and
is not restricted to specific universities or schools. However, certain institutions appear more frequently among participants largely because some schools, colleges and universi -
ties incorporate community service or extracurricular service-learning components into their curricula, which can be fulfilled through this programme. This ensures a steady
flow of participants while also broadening awareness of conservation education among diverse student populations.

Thematic analysis was applied to the open-ended responses using a five-step procedure (agreement level reached Cohen’s Kappa   =   0.82) as outlined by [ 44 ]. First, two research -
ers familiarised themselves with the data through repeated readings to gain a compre - hensive understanding. Second, an inductive coding process was conducted to identify
key phrases, recurring sentiments, and notable language used by respondents. Third, codes were organised into broader categories and grouped to develop initial themes.
Fourth, these themes were reviewed, refined, and validated to ensure they were both coherent and distinct. Finally, in the interpretation phase, themes were examined in
relation to the study’s objectives to generate actionable insights and a holistic picture of participant experiences. Ethical standards were maintained throughout the research
process. All responses were collected with informed consent via Google Forms, and participants were assured that their responses would remain anonymous. No person -
ally identifiable information was collected, and all findings were reported in aggregate to protect individual privacy.
### 3   Results and discussions
Figure   1   shows the proportion of participants affiliated with various institutions who took part in the KeeperKu programme from 2023 to 2024. The chart highlights the
diversity of institutional representation among the programme volunteers.
The analysis of participant responses to the question “Which institution are you from?” revealed that the majority of KeeperKu participants came from private universi -
ties (47.30%, 109 participants), followed by public universities (34.71%, 149 participants).
High school students accounted for a smaller proportion, with 13.31% (42 participants) coming from public high schools and only 4.68% (15 participants) from private high
schools, as seen in Fig.   1 . These findings suggest that tertiary-level institutions, par - ticularly private universities form the largest contributor group to the KeeperKu pro -
gramme. Similar trends have been observed in volunteer programmes across other sectors, where university students were often the most represented due to their flexibil -
ity, academic alignment and stronger institutional linkages with external organisations [ 18 ,   21 ]. In the context of conservation education, previous studies have also found that
university students, especially those pursuing environmental or life sciences, are more likely to engage in experiential volunteer opportunities that complement their studies
[ 42 ]. The concentration of participants from private universities may therefore reflect both curricular relevance and the capacity of these institutions to integrate extracurricu -
lar experiences more flexibly into their programmes.

of variety and depth in task assignments, which led to boredom or feelings of under - utilisation. Coupled with the desire for animal interaction, this highlights a mismatch
between volunteer expectations and the reality of the work. Many participants hoped for more direct contact with animals, and some expressed envy over perceived unequal
task distribution. Managing expectations during recruitment and onboarding may help address this gap. Besides, organisation and communication challenges also impacted the
volunteer experience. Reports of not knowing what to do or facing a “slow day with no

such as implementing a group chat indicate a desire for more streamlined coordination which could be addressed through better pre-work briefings or digital communication
tools. Furthermore, although the majority of staff were praised, unprofessional staff behaviour was noted by a minority. Complaints such as staff being “arrogant” or “sarcas -
tic” undermine the otherwise positive perception of staff and highlight the importance of consistent training and monitoring of staff-volunteer interactions.
Other than that, environmental and logistical issues also detracted from the experi - ence. Under physical challenges, volunteers cited discomfort from heat, mosquitoes,
and inadequate gear such as dirty or wet boots. This theme emphasises the need for improved preparation and support for volunteers especially when working outdoors for
extended periods. Besides, cost vs. value perception also emerged as one of the concerns with some participants questioning the value of paying RM37 for what they perceived
as basic tasks. Comments like “We’re paying to work” reflect frustration and suggest the need to reassess pricing or clearly communicate what the fee supports such as for
animal care and facility maintenance. However, despite these criticisms, the suggestions for improvement theme reveals that many volunteers were still engaged and invested in
the programme’s success. The volunteers’ feedback, such as adding task variety, rotat - ing duties, and upgrading facilities was generally constructive and indicated a willing -
ness to help enhance the programme rather than dismiss it. Finally, the theme of overall enjoyment underscores that many participants had a rewarding experience overall.
Comments like “Best experience ever” and “Loved it despite some flaws” reflect a strong emotional connection to the programme and suggest that the positives outweighed the
negatives for many. This analysis highlights the complexity of volunteer satisfaction in experiential programmes. [ 13 ] reported that while learning outcomes and staff support
are strong assets, addressing logistical, organisational, and expectation-management issues will be crucial for improving volunteer retention and overall programme quality.
Moving forward, implementing feedback-driven changes could potentially help enhance the volunteer experience and strengthen the KeeperKu program’s impact.
A potential limitation of this study is the possibility of social desirability bias influ - encing participant responses. As many of the volunteers were students with academic
or career interests in zoology, veterinary science or conservation-related fields, their evaluations of the KeeperKu programme may have been positively skewed to align with
perceived expectations or to maintain favourable associations with Zoo Negara. This may partially explain the high levels of reported satisfaction such as over 60% select -
ing “Very Satisfied” and the strong intention to rejoin or recommend the programme (93.3%). Although anonymity and confidentiality of responses were emphasised during
data collection, such bias cannot be entirely ruled out and should be considered when interpreting the findings. Future studies may benefit from incorporating mixed methods
approaches such as follow-up interviews or third-party evaluations to help validate self- reported outcomes and reduce reliance on potentially biased responses.
### 4   Conclusion
This study provides a comprehensive evaluation of the KeeperKu volunteer programme which reveals a generally positive experience among participants particularly in areas
related to learning, staff support, and overall enjoyment. The majority of volunteers

willingness to rejoin or recommend the programme. Private university students were found to form the largest demographic group, likely due to academic alignment and
greater access to experiential opportunities. However, the analysis also uncovered areas for improvement. Participants highlighted challenges such as repetitive tasks, limited
animal interaction, unclear instructions, and logistical discomfort. A small but notable portion of feedback pointed to inconsistencies in staff professionalism and communica -
tion. Furthermore, concerns about value for money and the need for more engaging, var - ied tasks suggest that better expectation-setting and programme structure are needed.
Despite these issues, the presence of constructive suggestions from participants reflects their investment in the programme’s success. Enhancing pre-programme communica -
tion, diversifying volunteer roles, improving staff-volunteer interactions, and addressing logistical constraints could further strengthen the programme’s effectiveness. Diversi -
fying volunteer roles by rotating participants across different sections or introducing new conservation-related tasks would offer broader exposure and prevent monotony.
Improving staff–volunteer interactions through regular briefings, mentorship and feed - back sessions can foster stronger engagement and learning. Additionally, addressing
logistical constraints such as scheduling flexibility, workload balance and availability of tools or resources would further strengthen the programme’s overall effectiveness. By
integrating these insights, the Zoo Negara’s KeeperKu programme has the potential to offer a more inclusive, impactful, and rewarding experience, and thereby extending its
educational and conservation mission to a broader and more engaged volunteer base.
